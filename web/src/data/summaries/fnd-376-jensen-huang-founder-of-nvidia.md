## Background

> Senra distills Tae Kim's The Nvidia Way into 19 operating principles from Jensen Huang's three-decade run. Born in Taiwan, Jensen immigrated at age 10 — his mother drilled 10 English words daily — and survived a Kentucky reform school where beatings taught tolerance for discomfort. He worked days at AMD, nights at Stanford for eight years to finish a master's, then LSI Logic where CEO Wilfred Corrigan introduced him to Sequoia's Don Valentine despite a 'sick, crappy pitch.'
>
> Co-founders Chris Malachowski and Curtis Priem recruited Jensen from the Sun Microsystems account. NV1 and NV2 failed from Swiss-Army-knife overdesign; layoffs cut headcount from 100+ to 40. Riva 128 succeeded by reversing software-before-hardware testing via a $1 million Ecos emulator — shaving nearly a year off a six-month cash runway — and Jensen's showmanship rallying the team around a 30,000-unit STB Systems PO.

## Key Facts

> **F1** NV1 flopped because Nvidia stuffed features nobody wanted — Jensen's lesson: 'Nobody goes to the store to buy a Swiss Army knife.' Layoffs took the company from 100+ employees to 40 with ≈6–9 months of cash; the $1 million Ecos machine cut runway to ≈6 months but accelerated chip testing enough to ship Riva 128 before insolvency. Competitors released chips every ≈18 months; Jensen's 'three teams, two seasons' cadence forced parallel development.

> **F2** Jensen runs ≈60 direct reports with almost no one-on-ones — information travels fast in a flat org he rebuilt after early Nvidia became 'bureaucratic and political.' Employees call him 'Professor Jensen'; he spends meetings at the whiteboard because 'there is no hiding.' Public criticism at all-hands — e.g., calling NV30 architects out for poor planning — replaces private back-channel politics.

> **F3** CUDA took four years and pushed gross margin from ≈45% to ≈35% during the financial crisis as Nvidia bet GPUs could become programmable platforms. Customers discovered AI and scientific uses Jensen did not fully predict — the programmable layer made demand compound. Nvidia today employs ≈29,000 people; Jensen says the first 15 years as CEO he 'sucked' and would prefer not to have been involved.

## Mental Model · *Speed of Light Organization*

> **Components**
>
> Benchmark every project against the theoretical maximum speed physics allows — not last quarter, not competitors. Pair that with 'torture into greatness': public accountability, extreme work ethic ('no one will outwork me'), and self-criticism so severe Jensen told a room after a record quarter, 'I wake up every morning, look in the mirror, and say you suck.' The company is the CEO's race car — ≈60 direct reports, whiteboard transparency, and teaching as 90% of leadership per Jim Sinegal's Costco lesson Jensen cites.
>
> **Application**
>
> Platform bets require margin sacrifice and customer-led discovery — CUDA's value came from users finding workloads Nvidia did not pitch. Hiring A-players with maximum pain tolerance matters more than perfect strategy decks; reputation preceded Jensen at Sequoia. Complacency kills — Jensen grill employees at urinals rather than let comfort calcify.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Focus beats feature stuffing.
>
> **Q** What did NV1 teach Jensen?
>
> **A** The market wanted the fastest graphics for games at a fair price — not a multifunction card. Jensen: 'We learned it was better to do fewer things well.' Positioning must answer David Ogilvy's simple test: what the product does and who it is for.

> **2.** Bet the balance sheet on speed when cash is finite.
>
> **Q** How did Riva 128 save Nvidia?
>
> **A** With ≈6 months of cash after buying the $1 million Ecos emulator, Jensen reversed the process — building driver software before physical chips existed — shaving nearly a year off production. He read cash-on-hand from one pocket, a 30,000-unit PO from another, and the cafeteria erupted.

> **3.** Platform shifts require years of margin pain.
>
> **Q** Why did CUDA matter?
>
> **A** Four years of investment cut gross margin from ≈45% to ≈35% while making GPUs programmable. Scientific, industrial, and eventually AI customers built on CUDA — demand compounded because every new CUDA developer increased GPU value, a bet competitors treating GPUs as disposable gaming chips missed.
