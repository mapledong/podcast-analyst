## Background

> Martín Escobari helps run General Atlantic, a 45-year-old global growth equity firm with roots in Chuck Feeney's Duty Free fortune. He grew up in Bolivia, worked with 3G figures early in his career, co-founded Submarino.com, and built GA's Latin America program. The episode is rich with numbers: 3G's Brahma compounding from roughly $80M to $60B-plus, Submarino's $80M fundraise, Anthropic/Cognition revenue growth, GA's 4% loss ratio versus 20–40% common in venture and growth, 500-plus portfolio companies, and global valuation gaps between the US at ≈26x earnings, Europe ≈14x, Brazil ≈9x, and Mexico ≈10x. He repeatedly contrasts process and temperament: long periods of fieldwork, then very short execution windows when conditions break in your favor. The examples range from 3G's multi-step beer strategy to GA's crisis-era entry points in Latin America, where underwriting discipline and local context mattered more than consensus narratives.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Escobari says 3G's roughly $80M initial Brahma investment became $60B-plus excluding dividends after years of waiting for big beer deals; he describes a sequence where Brahma holders retained about 95% in one merger, then compounded through Antarctica, Interbrew, and the Anheuser-Busch combination over nearly two decades.

> **F2** General Atlantic has operated for 45 years, invested in 500-plus companies, and made roughly half its investments outside the US; Escobari pairs that breadth with a 4% loss ratio, arguing the edge comes from taking hard macro and geography risk only after product proof, unit economics, and downside entry price are all credible.

> **F3** He argues US equities trade at about 26x earnings for roughly 4% forecast growth, near the 97th percentile of the last 25 years, while Europe is closer to 14x, Brazil 9x, and Mexico 10x; he also notes select 40-50% growers outside the US can still clear at around 12-14x EBITDA. He uses these numbers to argue that disciplined entry price and time horizon can coexist with aggressive growth underwriting.

## Key Insights

> **1.** The best investors wait, then move faster than everyone else.
>
> **Q** What did he learn from 3G?
>
> **A** The Brahma story is the template: think about the target for years, wait for political or market stress, then close in a week or a few months. Every three to five years, he says there is a once-in-a-generation opportunity if you are ready — when dislocation arrives, years of preparation can compress into action so you can do seven years of work in one year. His framing is not about prediction accuracy; it is about preparation intensity before the window opens, so execution speed is earned rather than improvised.

> **2.** GA takes a lot of risk, but tries not to take dumb risk.
>
> **Q** How can a global growth firm have a 4% loss ratio?
>
> **A** Escobari says GA takes macro, technology, and international risk across 18 power alleys, but enters when product-market proof, unit economics, and downside valuation support are clearer — scenario planning where the worst case is the company grows into the price paid. That limits timing on new industries and may leave money on the table, but produces what he calls a great product: reasonable returns with surprisingly low capital loss versus 20–40% norms. He also emphasizes that low losses are a portfolio-construction outcome, not risk avoidance, because GA still takes concentrated bets across technology and emerging markets.

> **3.** US exceptionalism is real but now very expensive.
>
> **Q** Why does he push global diversification?
>
> **A** He still wants about half his assets in the US, but not 90–95%. With US debt/GDP at 125% and possibly 145% within five years, plus US equities at ≈26x earnings in the 97th percentile of 25 years while Brazil trades ≈9x and Mexico ≈10x, he thinks diversified investors should be rewarded over the next decade — especially for 40–50% growers outside the US available at 12–14x EBITDA. The key is position sizing plus valuation discipline: he wants enough US exposure to capture quality, but enough ex-US exposure to benefit when multiples normalize.
