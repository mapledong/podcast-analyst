## Background

> Matt Reustle talks with Opendoor CEO Kaz Nejatian immediately after Q1 2026 earnings. The episode is less a classic company history than a live operating update on whether Opendoor can convert an iBuying model from capital-intensive promise into repeatable housing liquidity. Nejatian explains why the seller experience remains broken for most households: agents, repairs, showings, financing uncertainty, and transaction costs that can run 6-7% before stress and timing risk. Opendoor's pitch is a certain offer and faster close, but the company historically paid for that convenience with inventory risk and weak margins. The new argument is narrower: focus on the 80% of homes where standardized underwriting works, use data that may be 90-120 days ahead of public comps, and keep engineering headcount below 70 while AI and product automation improve throughput.
>
>  The practical diligence item is inventory discipline: the transcript's seller examples show that Opendoor only earns the spread when it avoids overpaying for idiosyncratic homes and turns capital quickly. Fewer than 70 engineers matters because the prior iBuying model could look like software while behaving like a levered balance-sheet business.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Management says Opendoor became adjusted EBITDA positive as of April 1, 2026, after a year of product rollouts, sales velocity improvement, and margin recovery from the February 2025 starting point.

> **F2** Nejatian frames Opendoor's informational edge as 90-120 days ahead of the market, because live bids and acceptances reveal clearing prices before public transaction comps appear.

> **F3** The company is deliberately simplifying around the 80% of homes where automated processes work, rather than paying for the last 20% of complexity; transaction costs in the traditional sale can run 6-7%, leaving room for a convenience spread if underwriting is accurate.

## Mental Model · *Liquidity Spread Against Friction*

> **Components**
>
> The product works only if certainty, timing, and reduced hassle are worth more to the seller than the discount Opendoor needs for inventory risk. Data freshness, narrow eligibility, and lean engineering are the three levers.
>
> **Application**
>
> OPEN is not a generic housing beta if management can prove positive EBITDA through a cycle. Investors should watch contribution margin, inventory turns, and whether the 90-120 day data edge survives competition and stale housing volume.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Convenience must be priced, not subsidized.
>
> **Q** What is Opendoor really selling?
>
> **A** A guaranteed sale and faster close. The transcript's $400,000 house example shows the buyer does not get a free $100,000 spread; Opendoor must cover carrying risk and transaction friction.

> **2.** Scope discipline is strategy.
>
> **Q** Why emphasize the 80% of homes?
>
> **A** Management is avoiding edge cases where customization destroys unit economics. The claim is that 80% of sellers can be served with standardized underwriting while the last 20% creates disproportionate complexity.

> **3.** Data recency matters in illiquid markets.
>
> **Q** Why is the 90-120 day edge important?
>
> **A** Housing comps lag. If Opendoor sees live seller acceptance before public data, it can price faster than agents who are anchoring to stale neighborhood sales.
