## Background

> The Qualcomm episode explains how a room of engineers in 1985 became the company behind 3G, 4G, and 5G connectivity. The roots go back to Irwin Jacobs and Linkabit in 1968. Qualcomm bet on CDMA when GSM had 60-70% of subscribers and CDMA never exceeded roughly 20%, but CDMA ideas were incorporated into 3G and later standards. Over 30 years of R&D built a patent and modem stack that let Qualcomm lead transitions to 4G and 5G. By fiscal 2022 the company did $44B of sales and had an enterprise value around $145-150B. Competition shifted: early-2000s modem rivals disappeared, MediaTek dominates lower-end phones, and Apple needed Qualcomm's 5G modem during the transition.
>
>  The episode also explains why Qualcomm is not just a chip vendor. Fabless semiconductor companies can outsource manufacturing, but wireless standards require enormous upfront R&D and years of ecosystem negotiation before revenue arrives. Qualcomm's royalty model monetizes inventions even when the chip share shifts, while its modem execution gives OEMs a practical reason to buy silicon. Apple is therefore both a customer proof point and the obvious strategic risk.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Qualcomm was founded in 1985 by Irwin Jacobs and team after Linkabit; the company's technical lineage goes back to 1968 and decades of communications R&D.

> **F2** GSM once had 60-70% of subscribers while CDMA never exceeded about 20%, yet CDMA technology became part of the global 3G standard and helped shape 4G and 5G economics.

> **F3** Fiscal 2022 sales were about $44B and enterprise value around $145-150B; modem competition narrowed from roughly a dozen early-2000s rivals to a few key players, with MediaTek strong below $300 phones and Qualcomm strong in premium tiers.

## Mental Model · *Standards Tollbooth with Silicon Pull-Through*

> **Components**
>
> Invest ahead of standards, contribute essential IP, then sell chips that implement the technology first. Patents and product leadership reinforce each other.
>
> **Application**
>
> QCOM is attractive when handset cycles recover and automotive/IoT diversify the base. Investors must track Apple modem risk, royalty litigation, and MediaTek pressure in mid/low tiers.

> **2.** R&D duration is the moat.
>
> **Q** Why is wireless hard?
>
> **A** The transcript emphasizes 30 years of R&D and the enormous capital needed to make fabless wireless chips work at global scale.

> **3.** Premium phones matter.
>
> **Q** Where is Qualcomm strongest?
>
> **A** The company leads in expensive phones and early transitions like 4G and 5G, while MediaTek is stronger below roughly $300.
