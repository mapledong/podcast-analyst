## Background

> Senra covers Jeffrey Young's Steve Jobs in Exile on the 12 years between Jobs's 1985 ouster and 1997 return — a period Michael Moritz calls without parallel for a founder-engineered turnaround. Stripped of power at Apple, Jobs spent a summer in Paris considering a quiet life before poaching co-founders, triggering Apple's lawsuit, and launching NeXT for universities with no name, plan, or product — only a vision and reputation as a 'terrible infant' poisoned by the board fight.
>
> Ross Perot invested ≈$20 million for 16% after a PBS documentary; Paul Rand charged $100,000 for the NeXT logo with no options ('I will solve your problem and you will pay me whether you use it or not'). Jobs bought Pixar from George Lucas's divorce fire sale, burned cash on a cube-shaped computer, and turned over executives repeatedly until NeXT went profitable — just as Apple, bleeding under Gil Amelio, needed an operating system.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Jobs left Apple in September 1985 after a board power struggle; NeXT launched with co-founders poached from Apple and only a vague university-computer vision. Ross Perot put ≈$20 million into NeXT for ≈16% stake; Paul Rand's logo cost $100,000 with zero revisions allowed — staff joked 'One Millie logo' meant $100. Jobs told NeXT retreat: 18 months to build computer and OS from scratch.

> **F2** Canon invested ≈$100 million in NeXT; product manager Garrett Rice read Apple would spend tens of millions on Be OS and asked 'why don't we just fricking call Apple?' Jobs pitched Apple leadership with working demos — four QuickTime movies, 3D models, productivity apps simultaneously — while Be's founder arrived with no laptop and no presentation. Negotiation at Jobs's home settled at ≈$10/share (≈$400M+ deal); Jobs opened at $12 (≈$500M).

> **F3** By November 1995 Jobs became the leader people wanted to follow long term. Humbled Jobs: 'If you don't treat talented workers right, they can get another job in 10 minutes… the CEO is actually at the bottom.' NeXT veterans said intellectual combat at NeXT was so intense colleagues at new jobs told them to 'dial it back.' At 43, Jobs returned to Apple ready — 'Apple was his again, and this time he was ready.'

## Mental Model · *Exile as Leadership Forge*

> **Components**
>
> Failure of words versus actions — Jobs preached permanent teams and 'full-body absorption' but churned executives until NeXT's black ink. Painful lessons: form follows emotion (Hartmut Esslinger after firing the human-head cube designers), bet on demo not deck (working NeXTSTEP vs Be's entitlement), and invert the hierarchy — serve A-players or lose them in ten minutes. Revenge ads against Apple wasted years; product superiority and pragmatic negotiation won the return.
>
> **Application**
>
> Founders ejected from their companies must rebuild credibility through shipped code and retained talent, not mythology. When selling back in, speed and indifference to last-dollar price matter if control is the prize — five-minute kitchen negotiation beat corporate theater. Board warnings proved true: Jobs 'doesn't know how to do anything but lead.'

## Key Insights

> **1.** Demo beats pitch when OS is the product.
>
> **Q** Why did Apple buy NeXT over Be?
>
> **A** Avie Tevanian showed NeXTSTEP running four videos, 3D models, games, and apps simultaneously while Be's founder offered no demo — only 'your engineers met mine.' Apple engineers saw the future in working code; Be offered assumptions.

> **2.** Humility unlocked the return.
>
> **Q** How did exile change Jobs?
>
> **A** Gil Amelio saw a pragmatic executive who handed demos to Avie and acknowledged engineering concerns instead of dismissing them. Jobs said talented workers can leave in ten minutes — the CEO works for them. People who stayed by late 1995 remained for life.

> **3.** One phone call altered history.
>
> **Q** What triggered Apple's NeXT outreach?
>
> **A** Garrett Rice read Apple would buy Be for tens of millions and left CEO Ellen Hancock a voicemail pitching NeXT's overlooked technology — she said she hadn't considered it. Jobs then called Amelio: 'BeOS was the wrong choice.'
