## Background

> Ben and David describe Formula 1 as three overlapping competitions: drivers, engineers spending hundreds of millions on bespoke cars, and team-owner politics in the paddock. The sport counts 827M+ global fans — a scale that surprised many American viewers before Netflix. Bernie Ecclestone moved from London car dealer to commercial operator: he bought Brabham for ~£100,000 in 1972, centralized promoter fees and television rights while teams remained undercapitalized, and routed revenue through opaque vehicles without ever clearly owning the sport. Liberty's 2016 acquisition (≈$8B enterprise value) brought Chase Carey in to professionalize what Bernie had monetized — through a cost cap, social media, and Drive to Survive — helping turn chronically loss-making teams into assets averaging ≈$3.6B in value.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Formula 1 has 827M+ global fans; under Bernie, teams often received ≈$10,000 per race before commercial terms improved. Today average team valuation is ≈$3.6B with a ≈$1.5B floor — team valuations rose from ~£100,000 (Bernie's 1972 Brabham purchase) to billions partly because Bernie centralized economics away from the grid.

> **F2** Liberty acquired F1 in 2016 for ≈$4.4B equity / ≈$8B enterprise value; Bernie exited 2017 as Chase Carey implemented turnaround. For decades F1 made $0 in media rights while the NFL earned $50M+ in the mid-1970s — Bernie's TV centralization via FOPA and the 1981 Concorde Agreement changed that; FWONK is now the pure-play commercial wrapper.

> **F3** Bernie bought Brabham in 1972 for ~£100,000 (≈$2.3M today) and sold it five years later for ~£5M. He took ≈8% off the top of commercial deals plus his team owner split, routing money through SLEC Holdings — often without written contracts. He sold F1 four times without ever buying it back or losing control.

> **F4** Tobacco sponsorship poured ≈$4.5B into F1 teams before the EU ban in 2006; Bernie personally earned ~£44.5M in 1993 as Britain's top-paid executive while most constructors remained insolvent. Race-host fees exemplify Bernie's extraction: India ≈$40M/year, Sochi ≈$50M/year for seven years.

> **F5** Liberty negotiated a $145M team cost cap in the 2020 Concorde Agreement. Drive to Survive (2019) unlocked US audience growth — sponsors earn impressions even when fans only watch Netflix, not live races. CVC's 2006–2016 ownership extracted value before Liberty productized the asset.

## Mental Model · *Centralize Commercial Rights, Then Productize*

> **Components**
>
> Bernie showed that value concentrated in television rights, race fees, and paddock politics rather than team P&Ls. After taking leadership of the Constructors Association in 1972, he became the intermediary between promoters, broadcasters, and teams. His approach differed from Pete Rozelle's NFL model of shared league economics: Bernie retained roughly 8% of commercial proceeds, owned a team, and routed rights through opaque entities (FOPA, SLEC), often without formal contracts. He built a central television feed, benefited from European pay-TV expansion, and sold Formula 1 multiple times without ever holding clear title. Liberty retained the centralized commercial asset but managed it as modern sports media: a $145M cost cap for team solvency, revised promoter terms, social distribution, Netflix programming, and F1 TV — treating the sport as a platform rather than an informal European racing circuit financed by tobacco sponsorship and handshake agreements.
>
> **Application**
>
> In league sports, the more investable exposure often sits with the commercial-rights holder (FWONK) rather than individual competitors. Ferrari (RACE) uses F1 primarily as brand marketing; Red Bull as an engineering and marketing anchor; Liberty treats Formula 1 as a media and events business with promoter fees, sponsorship, and broadcast as the main profit pools. CVC demonstrated that value could be extracted without fixing the product; Liberty's bet is that product improvement raises the entire asset. Three questions apply broadly: who captures television and promoter economics, who improves the fan experience, and whether spending rules can make teams sustainable businesses rather than vanity projects.

## What Makes It Work

> Formula 1 differs from major US leagues because a single commercial operator historically controlled rights without owning the teams. Bernie's strength was monetization; Liberty's is product management. Bernie built barriers through contract complexity, race-host fees, and paddock politics, but invested little in marketing infrastructure, data, or direct fan products. Teams often remained insolvent while he captured the commercial upside. He profited from Ferrari's global mystique without operating in Maranello. Liberty's approach professionalizes the sport without removing its character: a $145M cost cap, stronger digital distribution, official games, and Netflix programming. Drive to Survive succeeded in the US partly because the audience had no prior F1 reference point — character-driven storytelling could add fans without offending long-time European viewers. Sponsors gain exposure even when audiences watch Netflix rather than live races. Rivals such as IndyCar and MotoGP lack Formula 1's global calendar, star depth, and accumulated history. The sport's advantage combines schedule breadth, driver recognition, and engineering competition — now distributed through modern media channels. Middle East and Asia host fees fund expansion; European heritage supplies prestige. Unlike the NFL's revenue-sharing model, Formula 1 long allowed the commercial operator to capture disproportionate upside. Liberty must preserve that centralized economics while making teams credible standalone assets (≈$3.6B average valuation). The layered structure of racing, engineering, and ownership politics also suits documentary formats in markets without deep existing fandom.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Bernie built economics without owning F1.
>
> **Q** How did one dealer control the sport 45 years?
>
> **A** He bought Brabham, joined the Constructors Association, and saw promoters and broadcasters held the value — not engineers burning cash to win. He centralized race-fee negotiations when teams averaged ≈$10K per Grand Prix, then captured TV rights via the 1981 Concorde Agreement while the FIA kept sporting rules at Place de la Concorde. He routed money through FOPA and later SLEC, taking ≈8% off the top plus his team owner split — all often without written contracts. Court cases decades later revealed the plumbing; Bernie sold F1 four times without ever buying it back or losing control because legal ownership was murkier than operational choke-point power.

> **2.** Liberty's fix started with teams.
>
> **Q** What was priority one after 2017?
>
> **A** Repair team economics through a new Concorde Agreement and a $145M cost cap. Chase Carey's plan prioritized team solvency, then promoter relations, then social media, then Netflix. Without financially stable constructors, media deals and Drive to Survive lose credibility. CVC had extracted value for a decade; Liberty needed to show teams could become billion-dollar assets rather than subsidized marketing programs for automotive and consumer brands.

> **3.** Drive to Survive was uniquely suited to F1's US gap.
>
> **Q** Why didn't hardcore fans kill the Netflix show?
>
> **A** Americans had limited prior exposure to Formula 1 before 2019, so paddock-focused storytelling did not conflict with established fan expectations in the way a superficial documentary might offend long-time followers of a domestic sport. Ben and David emphasize that team principals, constructors, and driver rivalries carry the narrative for new viewers more effectively than technical race detail. Teams that declined to participate still benefited from broader sponsor visibility driven by Netflix audiences.

> **4.** Red Bull proved sponsors can become constructors.
>
> **Q** Why is Red Bull more than a logo?
>
> **A** Dietrich Mateschitz built real engineering competency — not just marketing spend. Christian Horner's team became a central Netflix narrative from Vettel through Verstappen, turning an energy drink into a constructor powerhouse. Red Bull shows the inverse of Ferrari: a brand born from F1 success rather than road cars subsidizing racing losses. Horner's political skill in the paddock mirrors Bernie's, but with an actual racing organization behind it.

> **5.** Ferrari rationalizes F1 losses as brand spend.
>
> **Q** Why would a team lose $500M/year on purpose?
>
> **A** Ferrari would sacrifice profit to win — F1 success returns infinitely in road-car pricing power and global desire. Enzo was the only businessman among 1972 team owners; today Ferrari is the only team Liberty cannot replace. Liberty's professionalization increased the commercial value of that halo for all teams: higher valuations, better sponsors, and US growth make even loss-making constructors rational for conglomerates treating F1 as marketing.
