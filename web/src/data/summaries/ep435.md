## Mental Model · *Graduation Rate as Product*

> **Components**
>
> The art-market analogy is about spotting talent before institutions validate it. Naimi studies smaller galleries that repeatedly feed artists into the big four galleries. In venture, he wants to be the early spotter before the platform funds crowd in. Founder salesmanship matters in more than customer demos. Three forms of sales: fundraising, hiring, and customer/product momentum. Hiring is especially hard when a seed startup asks an engineer to take $150K instead of $400K at Meta, Google, or OpenAI. Dilution sensitivity is a signal of founder conviction. He prefers founders who know exactly why they need $3M or $5M and want to sell as little equity as possible, because each percent sold is a percent they never get back. The portfolio model targets about 8-10% ownership in 60 seed companies and maintaining around 10% in the best 10 through Series B.
>
> **Application**
>
> Naimi's seed investing style combines art-world taste with a fundraising machine. The useful idea is not celebrity access or pattern matching; it is building a seed firm whose product is helping founders reach a stronger Series A with less dilution. The firm may compound if its seed-to-Series-A graduation and dilution outcomes remain structurally better than peers. Naimi is valuation-aware in hot categories; seed AI deals north of $50M post-money need exceptional founders and markets to justify using two shots on goal. Naimi put half of a Lightspeed scout fund into Rippling's seed round, an example of early power-law exposure. His first AngelList deal attracted about $470,000 of commitments within four hours, and AngelList sourced all the capital in those syndicates. Abstract now leads roughly 80-90% of its portfolio companies and claims the highest seed-to-Series-A graduation rate to a tier-one VC firm, plus top-decile Series A valuations and low Series A dilution. He says sub-$20M average entry valuations are no longer possible, so the current target is closer to sub-$30M.
