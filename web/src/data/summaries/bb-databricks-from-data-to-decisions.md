## Background

> Matt Reustle revisits Databricks with Alan Tu of WCM Investment Management, which invested in December 2024 after a nine-month diligence process. Tu opens with a spreadsheet analogy: most analytics time is spent cleaning mismatched columns before a simple average — Databricks does that at enterprise scale for fraud alerts, pricing, recommendations, and inventory models.
>
> Seven Berkeley AMP Lab founders in 2009 bet on cloud, data at scale, and open source. Apache Spark drove adoption, but Tu stresses the harder second home run: a proprietary engine worth paying for while the free version remains your competitor. Databricks added MLflow, Delta, and a SQL data-warehouse product on pace for $1 billion of revenue — coining the lakehouse category Snowflake initially mocked.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Databricks was valued around $130 billion privately at recording (December 2025); Tu cites $4 billion-plus ARR with roughly $1 billion AI-related and disclosed net dollar expansion above 140%, with free cash flow positivity while staying private through the 2022 downturn.

> **F2** Seven Berkeley AMP Lab co-founders launched Databricks in 2009 on bets that cloud, big data, and open source would compound; WCM invested in December 2024 after nine months of diligence as the SQL data-warehouse product scaled toward $1 billion of revenue.

> **F3** Tu says Databricks' lakehouse/data-warehouse product approaching $1 billion dwarfed Snowflake's upstream data-engineering revenue, while many enterprises still run both in a process-then-store workflow. Late-stage fundraises largely fund RSU tax bills as employees get liquidity, not GPU capex. AgentBricks and Lakebase extend the stack toward agentic automation atop cataloged data gravity.

## Mental Model · *Two Home Runs on Open Source*

> **Components**
>
> First home run: build an open-source standard (Spark) that wins mindshare. Second home run: ship a proprietary, higher-performance platform layer enterprises will pay for — not just SSO add-ons. Tu argues you need both because free Spark remains your competitor even after mainstream adoption. Ali's team mocked Delta with 'Spark on acid' T-shirts before the lakehouse category became industry-standard.
>
> **Application**
>
> Databricks monetizes compute usage plus strategic layers like governance, while using open formats and free storage to disrupt Snowflake's data-movement model. Staying private through the 2022 downturn let Databricks stay FCF positive above $4 billion ARR while investing in R&D. Tu argues you do not have an AI strategy without a data strategy — pipelines and cataloging benefit Databricks even if model hype cools.

## Key Insights

> **1.** You cannot run AI without clean, cataloged data.
>
> **Q** How does AI change the Databricks thesis?
>
> **A** Tu says roughly $1 billion of Databricks' $4 billion-plus ARR is already AI-related, but the durable tailwind is data engineering itself. Enterprises now prioritize pipelines and cataloging because models fail on messy inputs—benefiting Databricks even if model hype cools. AI-native labs and RAG workloads still need governed tables, vector tooling, and model evaluation layers Databricks is productizing.

> **2.** Lakehouse expansion beat Snowflake's upstream push empirically.
>
> **Q** Who is winning the convergence?
>
> **A** Tu acknowledges coopetition—many clients process in Databricks and warehouse in Snowflake—but says Databricks' structured SQL product scaling toward $1 billion has far outpaced Snowflake's data-engineering revenue. He cites the lakehouse term critics once mocked; industry analysts now treat it as a defined category.

> **3.** Staying private funded offense through the 2022 downturn.
>
> **Q** Why keep raising capital while FCF positive?
>
> **A** Tu explains late-stage rounds mostly offset RSU tax bills as employees get liquidity—the IRS treats mature private RSUs as taxable compensation—not heavy GPU buys. Databricks stayed FCF positive above $4 billion ARR while investing in R&D; Tu argues going public in 2022 would have forced cuts public peers made. Ali's decade-long Azure partnership shows how Databricks keeps hyperscaler coopetition from turning existential.
