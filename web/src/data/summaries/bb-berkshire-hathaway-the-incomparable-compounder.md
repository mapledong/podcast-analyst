## Background

> Patrick O'Shaughnessy breaks down Berkshire Hathaway with Chris Bloomstran, president and CIO of Semper Augustus. Rather than retell every Buffett and Munger story, the conversation isolates the machine: insurance float, surplus capital, stock investing, regulated energy, the BNSF railroad, operating subsidiaries, and a culture designed to avoid blowups. Berkshire began as a textile mistake, but the 1967 purchase of National Indemnity gave Buffett an insurance platform that could invest float in common stocks when equities were cheap. Over decades the company used that base to buy General Re, MidAmerican Energy, BNSF, Apple, and now Alleghany. Bloomstran frames Berkshire as a tax-efficient, conservatively run compounding bond: bought at an earnings yield near 8%, with retained earnings reinvested at roughly 10-12%, backed by businesses unlikely to be disrupted.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** Berkshire has almost $150 billion of insurance float and roughly $300 billion of statutory surplus in insurance book value. GEICO writes about $40 billion of premium, Berkshire Hathaway primary writes about $13 billion, and the reinsurance operations write about $20 billion; the whole global reinsurance industry has roughly $650 billion of surplus, while Berkshire has about 40% of that surplus and only about 7% of premium volume.

> **F2** The insurance portfolio holds about $320 billion of common stocks, plus bonds and cash, allowing Berkshire to earn equity-like returns where most insurers hold only small stock allocations. Bloomstran estimates underwriting profit near 5% pre-tax on about $70 billion of premium, or roughly $3.5 billion, but the real engine is look-through earnings from Apple, Coca-Cola, Chevron, Occidental, and other common stocks.

> **F3** Berkshire's non-insurance engines are now large enough to reduce dependence on float: BNSF earns about $7 billion, Berkshire Energy about $4 billion while retaining all profits, and manufacturing/service/retail subsidiaries throw off about $11 billion. Bloomstran estimates roughly $50 billion of economic earnings against a market cap near $600 billion, or about 12 times earnings. Berkshire Energy has spent about $35 billion on wind and is building an $18 billion grid, with coal plants closing through 2040.

## Mental Model · *Permanent Capital Without Fragility*

> **Components**
>
> Berkshire compounds because it pairs cheap, durable capital with refusal to chase volume. Insurance float is useful only when underwriting discipline prevents losses and surplus capital prevents forced selling. The company uses no stock options, pays modest salaries, carries debt mainly inside regulated subsidiaries, and keeps capital at headquarters until an attractive opportunity appears. Mistakes like Precision Castparts shrink as the asset base grows.
>
> **Application**
>
> The framework for investors is to find capital allocators who can retain profits, reinvest at high returns, avoid leverage traps, and treat shareholders as partners. Berkshire is closer to an equity bond than a normal conglomerate: current earnings yield is retained and reinvested in rail, utilities, stocks, bolt-on deals, or buybacks only when shares are cheap. Culture is the moat after Buffett; Greg Abel and the board must preserve underwriting discipline, tax efficiency, and moral responsibility to shareholders. The board's job is to keep activists and quarterly pressure from changing the compounding machine.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Float works only with surplus and discipline.
>
> **Q** Why can't every insurer copy Berkshire?
>
> **A** Insurance is a bad industry in aggregate; most underwriters break even or lose money. Berkshire can walk away from mispriced premium because it has enormous surplus, pays about $40 billion of annual cash losses without balance-sheet stress, and can invest far more reserves in stocks than peers.

> **2.** The capital source matters as much as the stock picks.
>
> **Q** What is the overlooked Berkshire lesson?
>
> **A** Bloomstran argues the lesson is not only what Buffett bought, but where the money came from. Retained earnings and float gave Berkshire permanent, tax-efficient capital; management never had to issue equity, chase quarterly revenue, or sell in bad markets.

> **3.** Berkshire's next decade is more predictable than most businesses.
>
> **Q** What drives Berkshire after Buffett?
>
> **A** BNSF, Berkshire Energy, insurance, Apple-heavy investments, and operating subsidiaries are durable, cash-producing assets. The railroad is hard to disrupt, energy has decades of regulated renewable capex, and insurance gets stronger from a Fort Knox balance sheet.

## Investment Ideas

> **1. BRK.B** · 🟢 LONG · ●●○ Medium
>
> Bloomstran owns Berkshire as a top position because roughly $50 billion of economic earnings, conservative leverage, $150 billion of float, and reinvestment at 10-12% make the stock more attractive than the S&P 500 when purchased below intrinsic value.

> **2. AAPL** · 🟢 LONG · ●○○ Low
>
> Apple illustrates Buffett's durability filter: bought around 12 times earnings, it shifted from tech risk to consumer-product ecosystem moat, with users unlikely to leave the architecture once phone, tablet, notebook, and services are connected.

## Chronology

> *Berkshire Hathaway*
>
> **1965** Buffett buys Berkshire Hathaway, then still a textile company.
>
> **1967** Berkshire acquires National Indemnity, creating the insurance float engine.
>
> **1969** Buffett winds down his partnership while Berkshire's insurance platform keeps capital coming in.
>
> **1998** Berkshire buys General Re with stock, tripling float from roughly $7 billion to $22 billion.
>
> **1999-2000** Berkshire buys MidAmerican Energy after diversifying away from an overvalued stock portfolio.
>
> **2009** Berkshire acquires BNSF after the financial crisis.
>
> **2015** Precision Castparts becomes Berkshire's most visible overpayment and later impairment.
>
> **2022** Berkshire agrees to buy Alleghany and adds large Chevron and Occidental common-stock exposure.
