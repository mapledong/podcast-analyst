## Background

> Recorded live at the University of Washington, this interview episode departs from acquisition history to trace Convoy's origin. Lewis's path — consulting at Oliver Wyman on airlines and logistics, startup Skydeck (later Hiya), Microsoft product management, Wavii (acquired by Google), then Amazon — mirrors the "CEO factory" alumni building B2B disruptors. Ben notes Convoy was founded between Acquired's lunch conversation and first episode — a thousand-person company by the live taping. Convoy launched ≈2015 when smartphone penetration among truckers made passive location data and mobile load acceptance feasible — the clear "why now" after failed digital freight attempts. Lewis and co-founder Grant Goodwin recruited five engineers pre-idea at a UW table, hung out at truck stops, and bootstrapped liquidity by acting as a licensed broker: matching shippers to carriers in the Pacific Northwest before a $16M Greylock Series A funded national expansion. Dan never applied via job portals — every role came through relationships. Strategic choices: expand beyond one corridor early (national carrier network effects because long-haul carriers roam coast-to-coast), offer free TMS software as shipper top-of-funnel, pay carriers within one day, and sacrifice unit economics for liquidity density. PepsiCo and Fortune 500 shippers adopted early for remote employees. David's B2B marketplace principles: demand drives supply, price is unset, community is real but secondary to cash. By episode date Convoy ranked among the largest tech-enabled brokerages despite incumbents being decades old — top-20 brokerage in roughly five years.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Roughly one in four U.S. semis runs empty; 90% of trucking companies operate six or fewer trucks and 97% fewer than 20 — extreme fragmentation Convoy targets with software matching.

> **F2** Convoy raised approximately $668 million total and was valued around $2.7 billion at the live show; Dan Lewis cited ≈1,000 employees and revenue/GMV growth outpacing typical startup curves at 4½ years old.

> **F3** Greylock led a ≈$16M Series A when $16M A rounds were considered large; the company deliberately expanded nationally from one Pacific Northwest corridor because long-haul owner-operators already operate coast-to-coast.

> **F4** Convoy pays carriers within roughly one day of delivery — solving the broker float problem where truckers need cash immediately but shippers pay net-30 — a core B2B marketplace wedge distinct from consumer on-demand apps.

> **F5** Smartphone adoption among truckers in the 2014–2015 window (not 2007 iPhone launch) is the episode's decisive "why now": passive GPS, mobile document upload, and elimination of phone-tag dispatch.

## Mental Model · *Demand Pulls Supply in B2B*

> **Components**
>
> Consumer marketplaces (Uber, DoorDash) optimize rider/courier experience and onboard supply first. Convoy inverts: shippers control spend, so onboard demand aggressively — even at negative unit economics per load — until carrier density makes the marketplace self-reinforcing. Money velocity beats product delight: same-day pay, broker license to intermediate cash flow, and free TMS for shippers create top-of-funnel without requiring immediate Convoy load volume. National expansion beats city-by-city because trucking supply is inherently interstate; winning carriers in Seattle helps St. Louis shippers. Perception is operational: early-stage startups "scrape and bite" for trust before earning it.
>
> **Application**
>
> When evaluating B2B marketplaces, ask whether the buyer or seller controls budget and payment timing — that side likely needs subsidized onboarding. If supply is mobile across geographies, network effects may be national from day one rather than hyperlocal. "Why now" should tie to a specific enabling technology adoption curve (here, trucker smartphones ≈2015), not generic digitization narratives.

## What Makes It Work

> Convoy's edge stacks software on a broker's license and balance sheet — full-stack rather than SaaS-only freight tools incumbents ignored. Mobile-first dispatch, automated status (no "have they arrived?" calls), and instant pay address pain points human brokers cannot scale. Data moats accumulate: historical lane pricing, carrier reliability, and shipper demand patterns improve matching and eventual price-setting in an historically inefficient, relationship-driven market. Scale lets Convoy amortize sales teams and technology across growing GMV; Unilever-style anchor shipper deals (episode cites early Fortune 500 pilots) validate enterprise readiness. Weaknesses: thin brokerage margins (≈10–20% gross), capital intensity, and incumbents (C.H. Robinson, XPO) plus well-funded rivals (Uber Freight). The Amazon alumni playbook helps hiring and ops discipline but does not eliminate freight's structural economics. Long-term thesis: once liquidity is dense, spread between optimal matches widens and Convoy can capture margin by setting price more efficiently than fragmented brokers — aggregation via information, not just coordination. The Amazon six-pager culture shaped how Lewis structured Convoy's early planning even after leaving Seattle. Full-stack brokerage beats flywheel taxi apps that never scaled in freight. Incumbents like C.H. Robinson have scale but not mobile-native carrier UX or instant settlement — Convoy's wedge is operational speed plus capital to float payables. Risk: capital markets shifting toward unit economics over growth (2019 zeitgeist) pressures the subsidize-for-liquidity strategy that built the network.

## Key Insights

> **1.** B2B marketplaces monetize trust and cash flow, not delight alone.
>
> **Q** How is Convoy different from Uber for trucks?
>
> **A** Uber optimized passenger experience; Convoy leads with shipper sales and carrier pay speed. Truckers join for paid loads today, not app polish. Brokers historically float shipper payments while drivers wait — Convoy's one-day pay is an economic incentive, not a feature checkbox. David codifies: demand drives supply, price is unset, and community is secondary to getting paid. Enterprise shippers like PepsiCo validated the model beyond startup tenants.

> **2.** National expansion was correct because trucking supply is not local.
>
> **Q** Why not expand city-by-city like Uber?
>
> **A** Owner-operators with 3–5 trucks roam nationally; winning Seattle carriers helps Boston shippers immediately. Local-only density would strand drivers who return to legacy brokers at corridor ends. Convoy chose national network effects over replicated city launches — a rare marketplace decision the analysis treats as obviously right in hindsight.

> **3.** Team-before-idea recruiting is unusual but reduced execution risk.
>
> **Q** Why hire five engineers before product-market fit?
>
> **A** Lewis and Grant assembled talent at a UW table before choosing freight — betting execution capacity would survive pivots. It helped fundraising and confidence but carried payroll risk without customer signal. This reflects deliberate Amazon-style conviction, not standard lean startup.

> **4.** Smartphones in cab were the real inflection, not cloud or APIs.
>
> **Q** Why did digital freight fail before Convoy?
>
> **A** Prior attempts lacked mobile adoption among drivers — desk software in cabs was non-starter. By 2014–2015, passive location and photo upload worked. Combined with broker licensing to handle payments, the stack became viable. Ben calls this the clearest "why now" answer on the show.

> **5.** Startups manufacture deservedness before they earn it.
>
> **Q** What role does hype play in Convoy's growth?
>
> **A** Ben cites Mike Galgon: startups often fight for customers and hires they do not yet deserve. Convoy needed shippers and carriers to trust loads would materialize — buzz and scale signaling reduce friction in a relationship industry. Growth speed itself becomes moat when competitors need years to match liquidity.
