## Background

> Zack Fuss breaks down Exor with Krishna Mohan Raj of Diamond Hill. Exor is a Dutch-listed holding company controlled by the Agnelli family, tracing to Giovanni Agnelli's Fiat in the late 19th century. Three leaders shaped the group: founder Giovanni, grandson Gianni (who tied up Ferrari and globalized Fiat), and Gianni's grandson John Elkann, nominated heir at 21 in 1997 and sole family representative by 2007 amid four CEOs in three years and near-bankruptcy.
>
> Elkann simplified a tangled IFI/IFIL structure into Exor in 2009, moved headquarters to the Netherlands for tax efficiency, and recruited Sergio Marchionne to save Fiat — extracting ≈$2 billion from GM on a put option, merging with Chrysler, and spinning Ferrari, Stellantis, CNH Industrial, and Iveco. Today ≈70% of ~€45 billion gross asset value sits in four listed stakes — Ferrari, Stellantis, CNH Industrial, and Philips — plus Juventus, Clarivate, Economist, and a growing alternatives platform called Lingotto (~€6.4 billion AUM).

## Key Facts

> **F1** Exor's gross asset value is roughly €45 billion; ≈70% sits in four listed companies — Ferrari, Stellantis, CNH Industrial, and Philips — with Ferrari the largest stake. Ferrari grew from ≈15% of NAV to nearly half before Exor sold ~€3 billion of shares in February, signaling comfort with 25–30% concentration while funding buybacks. Stellantis is now only ≈10–12% of assets after the Fiat-Chrysler-PSA merger.

> **F2** The stock trades around €100 per share versus spot NAV near €180 — a persistent 50–60% holding-company discount. Raj argues recent NAV per share growth came almost entirely from Ferrari; without it, Exor looks like a collection of cyclical turnarounds. Elkann responded by trimming Ferrari to repurchase shares at steep discounts to NAV, treating Exor itself as the best-known investment.

> **F3** Capital allocation follows a duality: entrepreneurial spirit ('building great companies with great people') and financial discipline — net debt kept at 10–20% of assets, mostly bonds, little bank debt, maturities spread over four to six years. Success is measured by NAV per share growth versus the MSCI World. PartnerRe was bought for ≈$9 billion in 2015, sold after ≈5–6 years at ≈9–10% IRR when catastrophe losses and high reinsurance multiples made exit rational.

## Mental Model · *Patient Capital, Decisive Renewal*

> **Components**
>
> Elkann inherited a near-dead Fiat, learned from Marchionne that status quo meant failure, and rebuilt Exor as a simplified global holding company with tax-efficient structure and concentrated bets on exceptional assets. The operating philosophy pairs long-horizon ownership with willingness to change — trim winners, buy back discounted stock, sell when strategic rationale fades (PartnerRe), and recruit outsiders (Marchionne at Fiat, Benedetto Vigna at Ferrari) when insiders cannot solve the next platform shift.
>
> **Application**
>
> Holding-company investors should underwrite NAV per share compounding, not discount closure — management explicitly will not optimize for multiple expansion. The PartnerRe case shows Exor tolerates decent but unspectacular outcomes when volatility structure or market pricing shifts. Future value may come from healthcare (Philips, Institut Mérieux), luxury (Ferrari, Louboutin, Shangxia), and technology bets (Via mobility IPO path, Meta board seat for AI visibility), but renewal is slow and measured.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Ferrari is the engine; everything else is optionality.
>
> **Q** What drives Exor's NAV today?
>
> **A** Ferrari listing unlocked massive value and still dominates NAV even after the ~€3 billion trim. Raj says removing Ferrari leaves Stellantis, CNH Industrial, and Philips — cyclical, capital-intensive businesses trading at undemanding multiples. The market discounts Exor because it cannot see NAV growth without Ferrari's luxury multiple.

> **2.** Buybacks at a 50–60% NAV discount are rational capital allocation.
>
> **Q** How should investors think about the holding-company discount?
>
> **A** Elkann treats the discount as opportunity, not injustice — trimming Ferrari to repurchase Exor shares at ~€100 versus ~€180 NAV. Raj warns against owning Exor hoping for discount closure; alignment requires believing Elkann compounds NAV per share over decades while keeping debt at 10–20% of assets.

> **3.** People decisions matter as much as portfolio mix.
>
> **Q** What did Elkann learn from the Fiat crisis?
>
> **A** Four CEOs in three years taught Elkann decisive action beats thumbsucking. Marchionne's turnaround — GM put cancellation, cost cuts, Chrysler merger — became the template: find exceptional leaders, act as critical friend, and change course when CEOs fail. More than half of Exor's role is talent selection and space to execute.

## Investment Ideas

> **1. RACE** · 🟢 LONG · ●●○ Medium
>
> Ferrari drove most of Exor's NAV per share growth and remains the highest-quality asset — ultra-luxury positioning, controlled volume, and EV transition as further proof it is not a mass-market automaker; many Exor holders may prefer direct Ferrari exposure at full multiple.

> **2. STLA** · 🟡 WATCH · ●○○ Low
>
> Stellantis is a pure auto cyclical at ≈10–12% of Exor NAV, facing EV transition and overcapacity headwinds; Raj sees reasonable-to-cheap valuation but no moat comparable to Ferrari — useful as a embedded value option inside Exor, not a standalone high-conviction long from this episode.

## Chronology

> *Exor · Agnelli · Fiat*
>
> **1899** Giovanni Agnelli founds Fiat; family wealth later managed through holding structures IFI (1927) and IFIL.
>
> **1997** John Elkann named heir at 21 after family tragedy; takes control as Fiat faces four CEOs in three years.
>
> **2004–2007** Marchionne recruited as outsider CEO; extracts ≈$2B from GM, turns around Fiat, later merges with Chrysler.
>
> **2009** Exor formed to simplify family holdings; legal headquarters moved to Netherlands.
>
> **2015** Ferrari IPO begins unlocking value; Exor buys larger Economist stake from Pearson.
>
> **2015–2021** PartnerRe acquired for ≈$9B, sold to Covéa at ≈9–10% IRR; Lingotto asset management launched (~€6.4B AUM by 2023).
>
> **2025** Exor sells ~€3B Ferrari shares for buybacks; NAV ~€180/share vs ~€100 market price on Fuss-Raj breakdown.
