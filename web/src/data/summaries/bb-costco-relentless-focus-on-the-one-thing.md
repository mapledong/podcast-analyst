## Background

> Matt Reustle breaks down Costco with Zach Fuss of Continental Grain and Chris Bloomstrand, CIO at Semper Augustus and a long-time shareholder. Fuss walks the core equation: ≈800 warehouses across 12 countries doing roughly $180 billion in sales as the second-largest U.S. retailer behind Walmart's ≈$550 billion, powered by a maniacal focus on lowest price for members.
>
> The conversation covers the inverted economics where ≈75% of operating income comes from membership fees rather than merchandise margin, Jim Sinegal's process power from pallet-ready packaging to 15-times inventory turns, and Kirkland private label at roughly 25% of sales. Bloomstrand adds international lessons from Iceland — where one store signed up the entire 350,000-person country — and Sinegal's long-term public-company mindset versus quarterly earnings pressure.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** Costco runs roughly 800 warehouses across 12 countries on about $180 billion of annual sales as the second-largest U.S. retailer behind Walmart at ≈$550 billion. About 55 million members pay $60 a year with retention in the 90% range — 50 million paid members on ≈100 million total store memberships — and Fuss estimates ≈75% of operating income comes from membership fees, roughly $3.5 billion annually at ≈80–90% incremental margin, not product markup.

> **F2** Costco marks merchandise up 12–13%, backing into an ≈11% gross margin versus 25–35% at typical retailers; it buys at $10 and sells at ≈$11 while peers might charge $13–15 on comparable specs. Kirkland private label is ≈25% of sales — standalone among the largest CPG businesses in America — with only ≈4,000 SKUs versus ≈40,000 at a grocer or ≈100,000 at a Walmart supercenter, and Fuss notes Costco turns inventory ≈15 times a year with negative working capital on much of the store base.

> **F3** Sales per square foot have climbed from roughly $800–1,000 historically to ≈$1,500 today — about 2.5–3× Walmart productivity — as basket size and traffic compound positive comps year after year. New warehouses cost $75–100 million including land and inventory, ramp from ≈$100–150 million to $200 million-plus in sales over 8–10 years, and generate roughly $8–10 million of free cash flow per store annually at ≈3% store-level margins.

## Mental Model · *Membership-Funded Scale Economies Shared*

> **Components**
>
> Charge a recurring membership fee, keep merchandise margins near break-even, and pass vendor negotiating leverage back to members as lower prices. Fuss calls Costco the original consumer subscription — before CAC/LTV parlance — allegedly inspiring Amazon Prime. The flywheel: low prices attract members, higher member counts increase purchasing scale, better vendor terms fund further price cuts. Process power reinforces the loop: pallet-ready packaging eliminates backrooms, bulk sizing raises basket size and cuts shrink, and industrial-site locations lower rent — savings reinvested into wages above $20/hour and the $1.50 hot dog.
>
> **Application**
>
> Retailers competing on merchandise margin alone cannot match a model where membership subsidizes ≈1% product margin while comps stay positive year after year. Bloomstrand's Iceland example shows the playbook travels: beat local grocers on unit price even when absolute dollars are higher than U.S. warehouses. Operators outside retail can borrow the 'one thing' discipline — Sinegal refused hot-dog price increases and vertically integrated rotisserie chickens when Tyson birds grew too large — but replicating 40 years of culture and supplier trust is the hard part.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Membership income, not markup, drives profitability.
>
> **Q** How does Costco make money?
>
> **A** Fuss says ≈75% of operating income comes from ≈55 million members paying $60 a year at ≈90% retention, flowing through at ≈80–90% incremental margin. Merchandise is marked up only 12–13% for an ≈11% gross margin — Costco acquires at $10 and sells near $11 while Walmart might charge $13–15 on the same item.

> **2.** Kirkland turns brand equity into margin without raising price.
>
> **Q** Why is private label central?
>
> **A** Kirkland is ≈25% of sales and disintermediates national brands so Costco can charge less yet earn more per unit — quality often matches or beats branded peers (vodka, golf balls). With only ≈4,000 SKUs versus ≈40,000 at a grocer, every item is intentional and the Kirkland name carries the trust to convert trials.

> **3.** Long store maturation requires patient capital.
>
> **Q** What makes unit economics distinctive?
>
> **A** Bloomstrand notes new stores cost $75–100 million, start around $130 million in revenue, and mature above $200 million over 8–10 years while returning only ≈$8–10 million of annual free cash flow per site. Sinegal ignored short-term ROE critiques because half the chain was still ramping — a private-company mindset inside a public stock.

## Investment Ideas

> **1. COST** · 🟢 LONG · ●●○ Medium
>
> Recurring membership fees fund sub-11% merchandise margins while ≈$1,500 per-square-foot productivity and positive same-store comps compound; Bloomstrand argues mature-store economics were masked for years by a ramping base of younger warehouses.

> **2. WMT** · 🔴 SHORT · ●○○ Low
>
> Walmart's everyday-low-price model still runs 25–35% gross margins and ≈30% back-of-house footprint versus Costco's no-backroom pallet model — Fuss argues the membership-funded ≈11% gross margin structure is an impenetrable retail moat.

## Chronology

> *Costco*
>
> **1983** Founded in Seattle by Jim Sinegal and Jeff Brotman on membership warehouse retail.
>
> **1990s-2000s** Scales to hundreds of U.S. warehouses; Kirkland private label grows to ≈25% of sales with ≈4,000 SKUs.
>
> **2004** Bloomstrand buys in when ≈350 stores include many still-maturing units masking headline return on capital.
>
> **2010s** International expansion accelerates across Canada, Asia, Europe; opens first China store in Shanghai to overnight lines.
>
> **2017** Opens Reykjavik, Iceland — one store captures the 350,000-person market by undercutting local grocers.
>
> **2021** Reustle hosts EP.4 breakdown with Fuss and Bloomstrand as Costco nears ≈800 warehouses and ≈$180 billion in sales.
