## Background

> Ben and David fly to Nvidia HQ after Parts I–III research to interview Jensen Huang — filmed on Nvidia's production gear. Jensen explains founding bets: RIVA 128 saved the company when competitors would have caught up; CUDA was simulated before launch, betting the firm on a market (ML/scientific computing) that barely existed in 2006. He discusses org design (40+ direct reports, mission-based), learning without imitating (Star Trek conference rooms, sci-fi taste), Mellanox as seeing LLM-scale networking before the market, and CUDA as rebirth of UDA after Microsoft co-opted the developer-platform idea. LSI Logic shifted him from 'real men have fabs' to focusing where Nvidia adds unique value. Founders should address developers and build platforms — Google excepted among megacaps. Jensen reflects on drawdowns, long-tenured investors (Sequoia, Sutter Hill), and that at 30 he would still start an accelerated-computing company today.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Jensen bet the company on RIVA 128 and again on CUDA after simulating outcomes — CUDA required every future Nvidia chip to run it, a platform commitment competitors would not match; early DirectX battle with Microsoft taught him not to cede developer relationships.

> **F2** Nvidia's data-center leadership predates AI hype: GeForce NOW (cloud gaming) built latency and operations muscle; Mellanox acquisition (≈$7B) secured InfiniBand when others assumed Ethernet sufficed — many researchers call Mellanox among the best tech acquisitions ever for LLM training clusters.

> **F3** Jensen runs 40+ direct reports organized around shipping missions (e.g., DGX Cloud), not traditional hierarchy — enabling multiple flagship-class product cycles per year versus annual cadences at peers.

> **F4** CUDA (Compute Unified Device Architecture) unified GPU programming across all Nvidia hardware — original plan was DirectNV competing with DirectX; Microsoft already owned developer relationships, forcing Nvidia to own the full stack.

> **F5** Jensen joined AMD believing in integrated fabs, then LSI Logic changed his view; Don Valentine invested after LSI CEO endorsement though Jensen says that was not actually decisive — Sequoia and Sutter Hill held through multiple 80%+ public drawdowns.

## Mental Model · *Simulate, Then Bet the Company*

> **Components**
>
> Jensen's decision rule: do not push chips in on faith alone — simulate RIVA 128, CUDA, and data-center moves first; once the model clears, commit fully ('otherwise go home'). Platform strategy beats point products: after Microsoft absorbed the developer-platform idea, Nvidia made CUDA mandatory on every chip — a rebirth of UDA spanning decades of hardware. Data-center competence layered: consumer GPU → cloud gaming latency → Mellanox InfiniBand for rack-scale bandwidth → DGX systems. 'Zero-billion-dollar markets' (AI before revenue) become trillion-dollar ones if you stand near the action and acquire when the apple is ripe — better approximately right than exactly wrong (Buffett/Munger). Org design follows mission cadence: 40+ direct reports, flat to Jensen, trades traditional manager span for shipping speed. Luck matters (1997 3D gaming inflection, AlexNet) but preparation converts luck — without data-center groundwork, OpenAI scale would not run on Nvidia.
>
> **Application**
>
> Founders should build developer-facing platforms, not just products — most big tech started that way. When incumbents own distribution (Microsoft with DirectX), vertical integration of software plus hardware may be the only path. In capital-intensive tech, simulate before existential bets; after a win, resist re-inserting 'stops' that limit ambition. Acquirers should watch adjacency investments years before the market names them (InfiniBand before LLMs). Investors evaluating Nvidia should weigh platform lock-in (CUDA) and systems margin (DGX bundles), not quarterly GPU units alone.

## What Makes It Work

> Jensen frames Nvidia as one of few true platform companies — CUDA is the moat, not any single chip generation. Every GPU runs CUDA, giving developers, cloud providers, and enterprises a single addressable stack from gaming through H100 clusters. Competitors can match silicon peaks; replicating 20 years of software, libraries, and developer habit is harder. Mellanox adds networking optimized for model parallelism when Ethernet was 'good enough' — now table stakes for training at scale. Systems integration (Grace CPU, Hopper GPUs, NVLink, DGX boxes at $150K–$300K+) captures margin beyond component sales — bundle economics Jensen explicitly embraces. GeForce NOW and prior data-center products built operational skills before AI revenue arrived. Weaknesses: cyclical gaming exposure historically; customer concentration in hyperscalers; geopolitical export controls; Jensen's bet-the-company cadence fails if simulation is wrong. Org unconventionalism does not export easily — but that is also defense. Against cloud custom silicon (TPU, Trainium), Nvidia argues CUDA ubiquity and time-to-production favor the platform. Jensen's lesson from Microsoft: never again let someone else own the developer relationship.

## Key Insights

> **1.** CUDA is the company; chips are the delivery vehicle.
>
> **Q** Why mandate CUDA on every chip?
>
> **A** After Microsoft leveraged existing developer ties to win the DirectX era, Jensen became militant that all Nvidia silicon runs CUDA — a platform rebirth of UDA dating to the first chips. Developers learn once; Nvidia captures value across generations. Competitors can ship faster GPUs, but not an installed base of accelerated code without migration cost. That is why researchers call Nvidia a platform company, not a chip cycle story.

> **2.** AI dominance required non-AI data-center bets first.
>
> **Q** Was Nvidia 'lucky' on ChatGPT?
>
> **A** Jensen argues no — GeForce NOW taught cloud latency and ops; Mellanox secured InfiniBand before LLMs proved rack-scale bandwidth; DGX packaged systems before enterprises knew they needed them. OpenAI in 2015 mattered, but without those layers Nvidia would not power training at scale. Data-center segment is not synonymous with AI — AI rode infrastructure built for other missions.

> **3.** Org design enables shipping speed.
>
> **Q** How does 40+ direct reports work?
>
> **A** Jensen organizes around missions (ship DGX Cloud, next architecture) not classic trees — fewer intermediary managers, more direct information flow to him. Trade-off: nontraditional career ladders, high personal load. Benefit: Apple-level quality at multiple major releases per year — a cadence Ben and David used illustratively, not as knock on Apple. Mission clarity beats perfect hierarchy when technology moves this fast.

> **4.** Acquire when you see the curve, not the headline.
>
> **Q** Why Mellanox before LLMs?
>
> **A** Jensen saw sharded models needing bandwidth beyond Ethernet years early — standing near researchers let him move when Mellanox became available. Buffett/Munger's 'approximately right beats exactly wrong' applies: you do not need perfect timing, just earlier than the market. Many Nvidia alumni cite this deal as among tech's best — structural for training clusters, not a financial engineering play.

> **5.** Founders must simulate before all-in bets.
>
> **Q** Should founders always bet the company?
>
> **A** Jensen pushes all chips in only after simulation — RIVA 128 and CUDA were modeled before commitment; conviction follows analysis, not bravado. Luck (1997 3D inflection, AlexNet) still mattered, but preparation converted it. After wins, he resists re-imposing limits ('keep stops out'). For builders: learn widely, imitate nothing; LSI Logic changed his fab philosophy; at 30 years old today he would still chase accelerated computing — retirement is not the plan.
