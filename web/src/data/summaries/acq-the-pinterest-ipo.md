## Background

> Season 4 Episode 5 — second in Acquired's A+ IPO series, recorded two days after Pinterest began trading. Ben and David trace Ben Silbermann from Des Moines (ophthalmologist parents, med-school path abandoned after a Google stint and a Divya dinner ultimatum) through Cold Brew Labs, failed Tote catalog-shopping app, and Evan Sharp's grid UI pivot. Growth came from Salt Lake Alt Summit bloggers, Ben's mom recruiting patients, and invite-only virality — not Palo Alto growth hacks. FirstMark invested ≈$200K at 1 cent/share on Tote; Bessemer led a $10M Series A at 17 cents. Ben became CEO April 2012; Leslie Kilgore joined as employee 10. Promoted Pins launched May 2014. IPO narrative frames Pinterest as discovery/curation advertising — smaller TAM than search or social, but high commerce intent.

## Key Facts

> **F1** On April 17, 2019 Pinterest priced its IPO at $19/share for a $12.6B market cap — above the $15–$17 range set April 8 ($8.5–$11B) but essentially flat versus the $12.3B 2017 private round. First trading day (April 18) the stock popped 28% to $24.40, a $16B cap — modest versus early investors who bought at 1 cent/share (FirstMark, ≈9% still owned) or 17 cents (Bessemer Series A, May 2011).

> **F2** Revenue accelerated after Promoted Pins (May 2014, first partner Vineyard Vines): $300M in 2016, $472M in 2017 (>50% growth), $755M in 2018. Operating loss after tax fell from $126M (2017) to $63M (2018). S-1 showed $600M cash on hand and ≈$1.5B raised lifetime — hosts argue a direct listing was viable given trending profitability.

> **F3** User scale at IPO: 250M global MAU but only 82M in the US (up from 65M in Q1 2016). US growth flatlined after ≈100M users as core female/planning demographic saturated; international MAU grew but monetized far below US. Product used by 80% of American moms; roughly two-thirds of users are women. ≈25–26% of engineers are women — above Silicon Valley average.

> **F4** Valuation arc mirrors hypergrowth then plateau: May–August 2011 valuation jumped $40M to $200M (share price 72 cents) after hitting 1M invite-only users and Time's 50 Best Websites. March 2015 private mark hit $11B. Domestic saturation and 'next Facebook' narrative deflated expectations; men largely never adopted despite years of product work.

> **F5** Competitive framing at IPO: Pinterest had more users than Snap, fewer than Twitter. Discovery/inspiration advertising sits below search and social in TAM — hosts cite DoorDash/Instacart pattern where largest ad pools fill first. Instagram Collections mimics boards; bear case is Facebook copying the format. Bull case: S-1 positions product as 'media-rich utility' — catalog browsing with commercial intent, not disruptive News Feed ads. Ben Silbermann has long said the goal is inspiring offline projects, not maximizing screen time.

## Mental Model · *Intent Graph vs Social Graph*

> **Components**
>
> Facebook/Instagram monetize who you know and what you did; Pinterest monetizes what you plan to do — weddings, remodels, recipes. The S-1 calls it 'the productivity tool for planning your dreams': inspiration enabling offline action, not maximizing time-on-site. Lower viral coefficients than social networks, but users assemble boards over weeks, creating durable commercial intent. Pinterest lacks classic social network effects (you care about content volume, not which friends are online) but runs a strong flywheel: more pins → more inspiration → more re-pins → more users in the planning-oriented segment.
>
> **Application**
>
> Evaluate consumer apps by graph type — social (relationship), interest (TikTok), or intent (Pinterest). Intent graphs monetize closer to search/shopping catalogs than Stories. Compare ARPU potential to Google Shopping and Instagram checkout, not Snap streaks. Tools businesses can still win ad budgets if a backend flywheel improves the personal utility as corpus grows — but TAM ceilings matter: hosts note only ≈25% of people who search also need inspiration/curation, and Pinterest's US base skews female with limited male adoption.

---

## What Makes It Work

> Pinterest owns planning-phase mindshare — users pin future projects (wedding boards, nursery layouts) weeks before purchase, giving advertisers high-intent signals versus ephemeral Stories. Ad format mirrors catalog browsing: intentional inspiration, not disruptive feed inserts. Evan Sharp's liquid grid layout became a decade-defining UI paradigm; SEO long-tail pin URLs and blogger-driven invite chains drove cheap early acquisition. Operationally, Ben invested in finance, HR, and culture ahead of hypergrowth (Leslie Kilgore as employee 10) — hosts contrast this measured management with Uber/Lyft chaos, noting Pinterest's smaller market rewards discipline. Promoted Pins built ad sales infrastructure before launch (2014), not patch-on monetization. Weaknesses: US MAU saturation (≈86M-ish, mostly women); men resist product despite years of fixes; international monetization immature; Instagram Collections and commerce push; discovery ad TAM structurally smaller than search/social; raised at $11B+ making acquisition-track unlikely. High fixed cost / near-zero marginal cost model means once revenue per user covers fixed costs, incremental margin is strong — but niche consumer product caps scale versus Facebook.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Pinterest is not a social network.
>
> **Q** Why 'non-social social network'?
>
> **A** Users catalog interests for themselves — asymmetric follows, no friend obligation. Ben's stamp-and-bug collecting DNA shaped the product. S-1 explicitly rejects pure media or utility labels, calling Pinterest 'discovery.' Investors mislabeled it 'the next Facebook' after $11B 2015 round, but engagement loops differ: planning sessions versus all-day feed scrolling. Kevin Hartz (Eventbrite) praised the episode as the best Pinterest breakdown he'd heard.

> **2.** Intent beats attention for certain ads.
>
> **Q** Bull case on monetization?
>
> **A** Planning a kitchen remodel beats scrolling past vacation photos for Home Depot ads. Revenue grew from $300M (2016) to $755M (2018) after Promoted Pins; losses narrowed to $63M. Ben's stated goal is inspiring offline projects, not maximizing online time — aligning ad products with catalog-style browsing. Hosts cite Porter-style power in the commerce flow: Pinterest steers attention between inspiration and purchase. Closing the ARPU gap to Facebook requires shoppable pins and measurement, but intent quality can compensate for smaller MAU.

> **3.** US-first is bearish near-term.
>
> **Q** Why MAU geography matters?
>
> **A** 82M US users versus 250M global — domestic growth flat since ≈100M as core demographic saturated (65M US in Q1 2016). International looks high-growth on MAU but monetizes at a fraction of US. Hosts grade A+ as cracking international monetization toward $20–30B cap; F if US churns or international never matches domestic ARPU. Product skews female (~two-thirds users, 80% of moms) — large niche, not universal platform.

> **4.** Tote → Pinterest is classic pivot.
>
> **Q** Origin story lesson?
>
> **A** 2008–2009 Tote digitized paper catalogues on 3.5-inch iPhones — hand-entered product data, zero traction. FirstMark's ≈$200K at 1 cent/share bet the team, not the idea. Users kept pinning products despite payment friction; Evan Sharp's grid layout and Thanksgiving Dos Equis naming moment ('Pinterest') reframed the company. Paul Skiera was initial CEO; Ben took over April 2012. Playbook: pivot the failed thesis, keep the team — norms and trust compound across attempts.

> **5.** 2019 IPO cohort shared conservative pricing.
>
> **Q** Link to Lyft/Zoom episodes?
>
> **A** After Lyft priced aggressively and traded down, Pinterest set a below-last-round range ($15–$17 vs $12.3B private), then priced at $19 with a 28% first-day pop — strategy hosts contrast with Lyft's red-herring escalation. Part of Acquired's A+ IPO saga alongside Uber, Zoom, Slack. Pinterest least media-darling of the cohort but capital-efficient relative to ride-share burn; ≈$600M cash meant IPO proceeds were optional — hosts favored direct listing logic (Barry McCarthy school).
