## Background

> Ben and David open with the 1991 Bainbridge Island meeting where Bill Gates and Warren bond over focus, Gates predicts Kodak's demise while defending networks, and Warren buys 100 personal Microsoft shares he cannot discuss with Bill. The narrative runs through Coca-Cola (10× first decade, ≈3.5× next 25 years), Buffett's technology avoidance framed as circle-of-competence discipline, Class B shares (1996) to thwart unit trusts shadowing Berkshire, and the 2008–2020 era of too much cash and too few scalable deals. Apple dominates the redemption arc — Ted Weschler's pitch as consumer franchise, not tech — while Todd Combs/Ted run sub-portfolios unlike Warren's style. Succession names Greg Abel CEO and Ajit Jain vice chair; the episode grades Warren's full career A (not A+) with recent five-year performance a C matching the index despite Apple.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Berkshire's Apple position grew from roughly $1 billion initiated in May 2016 to ≈$120 billion market value by the 2021 annual report — about $89 billion in gains in five years, initiated when Warren was 86 and framed as a consumer-brand moat despite being Apple.

> **F2** From 1993 through 2021 Berkshire's compound return was ≈13.5% annualized versus ≈27% from 1970–1992 — scale, missed tech, and IBM/airline errors compressing the middle-age record.

> **F3** Warren's blended 63-year record (1959 partnerships through 2020 Berkshire) produces a ≈22.3% IRR; $100 invested at the 1959 partnership start would be worth ≈$26.2 million held through today.

> **F4** Coca-Cola became ≈20% of Berkshire's equity portfolio at peak (≈2009); cumulative return reached ≈35× on cost — largest non-wholly-owned stake before Apple — while Coke itself only ≈3.5×ed after the first decade's 10×.

> **F5** Berkshire A shares rose from ≈$10,000 in 1992 to ≈$34,000 by the late-1990s tech bubble while Buffett issued Class B shares (1/30 economic, minimal voting) in 1996 to block unit trusts cloning the portfolio without paying Berkshire.

## Mental Model · *Different Strategy at Different Scale*

> **Components**
>
> Compounding large numbers is hard: a $100 billion check must find $10 billion of incremental value to move returns 10%. Warren's static-world investing — ham-sandwich businesses, cola unless cancer, newspapers until the internet — worked when change was slow; Gates at Sun Valley 1997 described streaming media accurately while Microsoft faced DOJ chains. Berkshire's structural advantages persist: infinite horizon, insurance float, no fees, decentralized operators, reputation in crises. But diseconomies of scale, four-headed capital allocation (Greg, Ajit, Ted, Todd), and halo dependence complicate post-Warren compounding. Apple proves Munger's quality thesis can arrive late and still dwarf decades of cigar-butts — if you finally buy the right franchise.
>
> **Application**
>
> Size caps strategy: what worked at $10 million (cigar butts) and $1 billion (See's, GEICO) fails at $500 billion unless you can buy oligopoly platforms or entire insurers. Question whether management transitions preserve process power or merely branding. For tech-era investors, Buffett's miss list is a reminder that circle of competence can become circle of omission when the economy's growth shifts to software.

---

## What Makes It Work

> Mature Berkshire's moat is structural more than stock-picking alpha. Float and insurance scale: GEICO, Gen Re, and Ajit Jain's specialty lines supply tens of billions in investable float when underwriting discipline holds — a funding advantage banks and PE cannot replicate without regulatory licenses. Permanent capital and tax deferral: No fund redemptions, no 2-and-20, and decades-long holding periods let unrealized gains compound — a tax-efficient structure public markets rarely reward equally. Reputational branding: Crisis investments (Goldman 2008, etc.) and Salomon survival cemented "Buffett premium" on deal access — though recent airline sales and IBM show branding cannot fix bad theses. Decentralization with capital centralization: Operating managers run businesses; Omaha allocates cash — a model that scales culture without synergies forcing integration. Weaknesses in part three: Tech omission, airline round-trip, IBM write-down, Precision Castparts, size forcing index-like holdings, and succession splitting investing among Ted/Todd while Greg lacks Warren's capital-allocation genius. Apple concentration saves recent returns but masks Warren's direct underperformance the last five years. Todd Combs and Ted Weschler's sub-portfolios already diverge from Warren's style — Amazon and Snowflake appear alongside Apple — previewing a more tech-tolerant Berkshire under Greg Abel even if Warren never fully embraced the shift.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Apple absolved decades of technology avoidance in dollar terms — not in narrative terms.
>
> **Q** How ironic is Buffett's largest win?
>
> **A** Ted Weschler pitched Apple as a consumer franchise with switching costs, not semiconductors. Warren bought ≈$1 billion in 2016, then scaled to ≈$120 billion — ≈$89 billion gain in five years, possibly more absolute dollars than the rest of his career. He still personally owns only 100 Microsoft shares; Todd/Ted bought Amazon and Snowflake while Warren sat out Google and Facebook. Apple redeems the scorecard without validating the anti-tech stance.

> **2.** Buffett's world shifted from static to exponential — and he stayed static.
>
> **Q** Why skip tech after Intel and Microsoft?
>
> **A** Alice Schroeder: Buffett wanted businesses a "ham sandwich" could run; fast-moving tech failed that test. Gates described networks versus Kodak accurately in 1992, but Warren chose Coke and durable cash flows. The internet accelerated change; Coke's value only ≈3.5×ed in 25 years after an initial 10×. David's takeaway: never bet against the internet even when Buffett does.

> **3.** Scale transforms edge from stock-picker to conglomerate structure.
>
> **Q** Why does 13.5% IRR since 1993 still beat many funds but disappoint?
>
> **A** Moving $10 billion matters at Berkshire size; opportunities must be public oligopolies or whole companies. Warren piled cash before the dot-com crash (1999 Fortune article) but also missed Visa/Mastercard IPOs despite owning AmEx. Apple proves one great position can offset many dogs — but recent five-year Berkshire performance roughly tracks the S&P ex-Apple, a C grade for an active allocator.

> **4.** Succession splits investing brain from operating throne.
>
> **Q** Can Greg Abel replace Warren?
>
> **A** Greg excels running non-insurance ops; Ajit runs insurance; Ted and Todd manage ≈$40 billion with different styles (Ted bought Amazon/Snowflake). Warren's criterion was operating experience at scale — not investment genius. Charlie on stage: "Greg will preserve the culture." Risk: four fiefdoms without a single allocator who can write a $50 billion check in an hour during the next crisis.

> **5.** Berkshire remains a religion and a widows-and-orphans stock — not a growth vehicle.
>
> **Q** What do Ben and David do with the shares?
>
> **A** David holds as conservative ballast against concentrated tech and crypto — alternative to negative-yield cash. Ben never owned despite research; views it as stay-rich infrastructure. Both agree halo fades when Warren leaves; bear case is BRK becomes mere investment, not oracle. Ho Nam's tweet: Warren built the only >$500 billion company as investor-founder — unmatched, but past tense looms.
