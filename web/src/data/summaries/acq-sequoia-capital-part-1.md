## Background

> Episode opens at Silicon Valley's 1957 birth: traitorous eight leave Shockley → Fairchild (Sherman Fairchild/Arthur Rock structure). Don (Yonkers, Fordham, Raytheon) joins Fairchild ~employee 40, scales sales ≈$2M→$150M+, pioneers selling chips into new verticals despite east-coast parent board vetoing ecosystem investments — so Don angel-invests personally. 1972: Capital Group (American Funds, >$1T AUM lineage) backs Don's full-time startup investing. 1974: independent Sequoia Capital Fund I after brutal LP raise. Early bets: Atari (≈$600K), Apple (1977, Mike Markkula via Don), EA, Oracle, 3Com, Cisco. Don recruits journalist Mike Moritz (1980s) and sales-driven Doug Leone — succession before falling asleep at wheel. Contrasts Kleiner Perkins' entrepreneur-first style. Don's Stanford talk: invest in markets, augment/replace management, questions ≤20 words.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Ben cites Sequoia-backed companies at ≈$3.3 trillion aggregate public market capitalization (part 1 scope) — versus ≈$1T cited for NeXT acquisition episode as comparison.

> **F2** Fairchild under Don grew from ≈$2M to ≈$150M+ annual sales in a few years (late 1950s–60s dollars); Don ran National Semiconductor earnings calls when CEO Charlie Sporck refused — meeting Capital Group LPs.

> **F3** 1972 Capital Group "Sequoia Fund" = $5M; independent Sequoia Capital Fund I ≈ $3–5M raised over ≈3 years (1974) — entire proto-VC industry met monthly at SF Mark Hopkins hotel.

> **F4** Sequoia invested ≈$600K initial in Atari (already ≈$30M revenue); ≈$2M total in Apple early — distributed ≈$6M Apple gains within ≈2 years for LP tax bills, missing multi-decade compounding.

> **F5** Cisco (1987): $2.5M for ≈30% from Stanford campus router founders; IPO ≈$160M+; Don stayed chairman into mid-1990s — "We network networks" as ideal pitch.

## Mental Model · *Market First, Active Partner*

> **Components**
>
> Don inverted engineer-founder VC: pick massive markets about to explode from Fairchild/National roadmaps, back teams receptive to help, accept replacing founders if needed ("Genghis Khan" quote). Socratic 20-word questions test storytelling clarity — proxy for focus/speed/stealth. Early funds concentrated capital (often only private round) into hardware with manufacturing — high check sizes vs today's seed. Lesson from Apple: distribution for tax liquidity destroys compounders; Cisco proved holding public winners still wins.
>
> **Application**
>
> Seed investors should separate market-size conviction from founder polish — but require coachability. Fund size and hold period must match winner magnitude; early exits on generational companies dominate IRR errors. Succession planning (Moritz, Leone) preserves firm brand beyond founder.

## What Makes It Work

> Sequoia's moat in this era: Don's Fairchild/National pipeline (sourcing), technical roadmap foresight (diligence), Capital Group credibility (fundraising), and willingness to be toughest board member (governance). Raiding Xerox PARC/IBM for commercializable tech after Apple success. Weaknesses: software blind spot (Oracle bootstrapped $2K, anti-VC Larry Ellison); Apple sale; hardware-margin mindset early. Kleiner competed with founder-centric model — both worked. Enduring ethos: "partnership" language, long-term public holds (post-Apple trauma), multi-generation partner handoffs — part 1 closes teasing Doug Leone and Mike Moritz's generation scaling Sequoia into today's $12+ billion global growth platform. Trip Hawkins/EA chose Don for forced scale. Oracle anti-VC rhetoric then Sequoia partnership at year six. Notre Dame endowment best manager over 30 years supports A+ grading debate. Wave Capital founders cite Greg McAdoo alignment lesson from Sequoia history.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Don invented VC by treating startups as sales territories.
>
> **Q** Why did Fairchild prepare Don for Sequoia?
>
> **A** He sold novel tech to defense then diversified applications despite parent bureaucracy — same as picking startup markets. Personal angel checks when board said no proved model; Capital Group scaled checks from thousands to millions.

> **2.** Apple distribution is Sequoia's costliest lesson.
>
> **Q** What went wrong with Apple?
>
> **A** Fund I had taxable corporate LPs needing 1979 distributions; Don sold Apple stake for ≈$6M total fund gain. Apple became trillion-dollar company; Sequoia learned to hold winners public for decades (Airbnb/Google cited).

> **3.** Cisco refined the playbook Apple broke.
>
> **Q** Why is Cisco the template?
>
> **A** $2.5M upfront for 30%, fast IPO, Don stayed chairman, no early dump. Sandy/Len non-traditional founders OK because router market exploding. Three-word pitch discipline.

> **4.** Markets over founders — but founders must listen.
>
> **Q** How reconcile Don's founder assessment with "invest in markets"?
>
> **A** Market size is necessary; team must execute with focus and accept Sequoia operating help. Engineers alone (traitorous eight style) lacked go-to-market; Don added sales/marketing/finance DNA — sometimes replacing CEOs.

> **5.** Would Silicon Valley exist without Don?
>
> **Q** What would have happened otherwise?
>
> **A** Ben leans Don accelerated ecosystem; David notes Kleiner Perkins parallel success — capital finds opportunities. Don's specific contribution: active company-building plus semiconductor ecosystem map at inflection moment.
