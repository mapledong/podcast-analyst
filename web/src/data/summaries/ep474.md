## Background

> Darren Farber examines modern conflict as a contest of state power, industrial capacity, and narrative control. He discusses Iran's internal fragility alongside the coercive resilience of authoritarian structures, then broadens to China and Taiwan deterrence dynamics. A recurring point is that military effectiveness now depends on iteration speed: drones, autonomy, and software-defined systems improve in active combat loops, not in slow peacetime acquisition cycles. Farber argues this dynamic advantages organizations that can ship, learn, and redeploy quickly. He also highlights institutional bottlenecks in US defense: one-year appropriations, repeated continuing resolutions, and weak demand visibility for smaller suppliers. The neoprime thesis is therefore conditional. Venture capital can finance experimentation, but durable scale requires congressional demand signals, contracting pathways, and multi-year authorities that let successful systems survive long enough to become programs of record. He emphasizes that this is not just a technology race but an institutional race: whichever system aligns budget authority, contracting speed, and industrial execution can adapt faster under stress. That perspective is why he spends as much time on congressional process and incentives as on weapons categories. He also contrasts adversaries that can centralize decisions quickly with democracies that must earn legitimacy repeatedly, arguing that this legitimacy is still a strategic strength if institutions can translate it into timely procurement. ## Key Facts

> **F1** At recording time the Strait of Hormuz was effectively closed in practice and Iranian military capability had been degraded. Even if 85-90% of Iranians want regime change, outcomes depend on whether there is a credible alternative force structure rather than public sentiment alone. Adversaries that centralize decisions quickly hold an operational edge, but democratic legitimacy remains a strategic strength when institutions can translate it into timely procurement.

> **F2** He describes the IRGC as controlling roughly 50% of Iran's economy while elites move wealth abroad into homes, villas, and large external balances. In contrast, he cites an average Iranian ATM withdrawal limit around $7, using that gap to illustrate how regime insiders preserve optionality while ordinary citizens absorb financial stress.

> **F3** On US defense economics, he points to annualized budget churn with roughly four to five continuing resolutions per year, references a designed FY27 defense budget near $1.5T, and invokes the Manhattan Project's ≈1% of GDP intensity as a benchmark for what true technical mobilization can look like when political demand is explicit. He argues those budgeting mechanics directly shape whether innovative suppliers can survive the valley between prototype and scaled deployment.

## Mental Model · *Power-Legitimacy-Procurement Triangle*

> **Components**
>
> Leg 1 is coercive power: who controls force, financing channels, and narrative machinery in real time. Leg 2 is legitimacy: who can sustain domestic and allied support through prolonged uncertainty. Leg 3 is procurement velocity: who can convert budgets into fielded capability quickly enough to learn from conflict. Weakness in any one leg lowers strategic endurance even when the other two look strong.
>
> **Application**
>
> Farber applies this triangle by separating tactical battlefield novelty from durable industrial advantage. For investors, the framework says to back firms that can survive procurement volatility and still translate wartime feedback into repeatable programs. For policymakers, it argues that multi-year demand and fewer continuing resolutions are not administrative details; they are preconditions for turning neoprime innovation into long-cycle national capability.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Authoritarian regimes combine control and fragility.
>
> **Q** Why can unpopular regimes still endure?
>
> **A** Opposition sentiment is insufficient when coercive institutions remain intact. Even an 85-90% desire for change in Iran does not produce transition unless military, financial, and organizational structures can realign. Regime analysis is a force-structure problem, not a polling problem.

> **2.** Combat accelerates hardware-software learning loops.
>
> **Q** Why did drones improve faster than legacy programs?
>
> **A** He argues active theaters create immediate feedback: systems are tested under pressure, copied if effective, and abandoned if fragile. This compresses iteration cycles compared with traditional acquisition timelines that rely on slower testing and budget gates. The result is a practical edge for teams built around rapid software updates, modular hardware, and continuous operator feedback.

> **3.** Neoprimes require policy demand, not just capital.
>
> **Q** What lets defense startups become enduring primes?
>
> **A** Farber emphasizes budget structure over venture enthusiasm. Annual appropriations and repeated continuing resolutions can starve high-potential programs before they mature into systems of record. Multi-year authority and better channeling of federally funded R&D toward industry execution let battlefield-proven products scale instead of stalling in procurement limbo — stable demand signals transform tactical wins into strategic industrial scale.
