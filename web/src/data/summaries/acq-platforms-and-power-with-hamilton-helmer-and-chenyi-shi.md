> Hamilton Helmer and Chenyi Shi preview work-in-progress on applying Seven Powers to platforms — intermediaries of transactions spanning marketplaces, Windows-style dev platforms, and aggregators. Three assessment questions: (1) quantify value creation and its trajectory; (2) how each participant segment perceives that value; (3) what prevents competitors from reaching equivalence — the power question. TSMC under-extracts to secure $10–50B fab customer commitments vs Apple's maximally extractive 30% App Store (≈$10B+ cited). ## Mental Model · *Three Questions Before You Capture*

> **Components**
>
> Platform power analysis separates value creation from value capture. Q1: objective value and trajectory (technology lowers friction — AI feeds, digital notebooks vs village matchmaker). Q2: heterogeneous segments perceive value differently — YouTube searchers (Ben/Hamilton) vs feed browsers (David/Chenyi); Amazon sellers want volume at market price, not premium buyers. Q3: power exists only if competitors cannot reach equivalence — Uber/Lyft multihoming and metasearch arbitrage (Ben's killed startup) break moats; Airbnb unique supply harder to replicate than Uber drivers. Extraction dial: YouTube raised ad loads after years of low ads; premature extraction kills segments still building. TSMC trades near-term rent for customer retention enabling irreversible CapEx; Apple vertical stack funds CapEx via hardware without same ecosystem dependency. Per-segment powers: 1850 general store had one analysis; modern platforms need Seven Powers per supply/demand cohort.
>
> **Application**
>
> Founders with PMF must ask Q3 before celebrating flywheels — network effects slides ≠ network economies. Investors: test diminishing returns curve shape (steep = no power, gradual = YouTube-like compounding). Incumbents with power should time subsidy reduction like YouTube ads, not Apple App Store day-one maximization unless switching costs guarantee retention. Compare Airbnb vs Uber when evaluating marketplace defensibility.

## What Makes It Work

> YouTube (Alphabet) illustrates platform power done patiently; Uber illustrates power absent despite scale. YouTube network economies: catalog scale plus personalization slows marginal utility decline — latest SNL skit assumption (Ben/Hamilton search intent) and algorithmic feed (David/Chenyi) coexist across segments. Creators, advertisers, and viewers all perceive high value; multihoming to Spotify/YouTube for Acquired episode itself shows creator side, not viewer destabilization. Uber/Lyft: low differentiation, easy multihoming, metasearch enables arbitrage — Q3 fails despite massive GMV. Ride-share membership/insurance switching costs weak vs Airbnb host guarantees (better than wiring money, not vs Vrbo). TSMC process + scale + cornered EUV supply: under-extraction secures Apple/Nvidia long-term — competitor cannot replicate fab customer portfolio quickly. Apple: branding + switching costs on integrated stack; 30% IAP viewed as extractive because developers/consumers won't leave ( affluent iPhone users, Android→iPhone flow cited). Weakness of framework-in-progress: segment taxonomy hard in practice; flywheel decks overstate power; extraction timing remains art (Steve Jobs power computing tangent). Not every platform earns multi-decade moats — idea-sharing platforms end in question marks.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Power is the third question, not the first.
>
> **Q** What do the three platform questions test?
>
> **A** Q1 maps total value creation and trend; Q2 maps who feels that value (segments differ — YouTube search vs feed users); Q3 asks competitive equivalence barriers — only then Seven Powers apply. Uber discussion: Q1/Q2 okay; Q3 fails because rides are fungible and apps multihome.

> **2.** YouTube's lead compounds slowly because content fit is personal.
>
> **Q** Why isn't YouTube's scale like ride-sharing density?
>
> **A** Even at hypothetical 100B vs 10B hours, marginal catalog value stays high — creators and viewers match on mood, personality, moment. Diminishing returns curve is gradual → network economies power. Steep curves (taxi density) prevent any player gaining durable advantage.

> **3.** Extraction timing is a platform dial.
>
> **Q** When should YouTube raise monetization?
>
> **A** Years of low ad loads subsidized growth; recent ad-load increases test power — users stayed while revenue captured more value. Contrast Apple 30% IAP from strength (developers won't abandon affluent iPhone users) vs TSMC keeping foundry fees moderate to ensure customers fund $10–50B fab cycles.

> **4.** Network effects without economies power is common.
>
> **Q** Why distrust flywheel slides?
>
> **A** Hamilton/Chenyi separate network effects (more users → more value) from network economies power ( durable profit advantage). Many startups show flywheels; few pass Q3 equivalence test. Founders exhausted after PMF must begin second climb — power invention, not rest.

> **5.** Analyze power per transactor segment.
>
> **Q** Is one Seven Powers analysis enough for platforms?
>
> **A** Ben's summary: 1850 general store needed one analysis; modern platforms need powers evaluated per supply/demand segment (Amazon seller vs buyer vs consumer). Technology magnifies transaction volume — platform businesses are where most valuable tech companies live, so segment granularity is mandatory.
