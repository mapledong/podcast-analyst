## Background

> Zach Fuss breaks down Intuit with John Feeley, deputy CIO and portfolio manager at Findlay Park. Founded in 1983 by Scott Cook, a former Procter & Gamble marketer, Intuit began with Quicken after Cook watched his wife struggle with the family checkbook. The company's name captured the product mandate: software should feel intuitive to non-technologists. Customer listening exposed an accidental business use case for Quicken, which became QuickBooks, while Microsoft pressure in the 1990s pushed Intuit toward TurboTax and sharpened paranoia about defending its core. Today the company owns TurboTax, QuickBooks, Credit Karma, and Mailchimp. Feeley frames the business as digitizing irritant necessities: taxes, bookkeeping, payroll, payments, customer outreach, and personal finance decisions that consumers and small businesses must handle whether the economy is strong or weak.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Intuit generated around $13 billion of revenue last year and traded near a $120 billion market cap. TurboTax handles about 30% of all U.S. tax filings and is roughly 30% of company revenue; QuickBooks and small-business/self-employed products are just over half of revenue, with about 90% U.S. share in small-business accounting.

> **F2** QuickBooks has around 6 million businesses on the platform out of roughly 75 million addressable customers, less than 10% penetration. The median QuickBooks customer pays about $70 per month, and 40% of QuickBooks customers connect at least one other app. Payroll is priced per employee, payments monetize card interchange, and QuickBooks Advanced costs about 3x the base product for larger customers.

> **F3** Credit Karma has about 100 million members and 38-40 million monthly users; Mailchimp has about 13 million users on a freemium model, with paid plans from roughly $11 to $300 per month. Intuit has grown every year since 1998 except 2015 due to an accounting change, grew 4% during the financial crisis, posted 24% pro forma growth last year, and guided to 14-16% organic growth. Sales and marketing is about 27% of GAAP expenses, while R&D is about 18% of sales.

## Mental Model · *Empathy-Driven Standards Business*

> **Components**
>
> Intuit wins by making required financial work simple for non-technologists, then turning that product into a standard. Accountants recommend QuickBooks because clean records save time and reduce tax-season uncertainty; third-party apps integrate because QuickBooks is where the small-business records live; consumers use TurboTax because the tax code is complex and the refund check matters. Standards give each parcel the right to make the next sale.
>
> **Application**
>
> The operator lesson is to solve customer problems rather than chase adjacencies. Cook's P&G training created follow-me-home customer observation, focus groups, and direct engineer exposure to user pain. Investors should value the reaction time created by workflows for non-technologists: users do not want to relearn accounting or tax software, so Intuit can survive code rewrites, entrants like Xero, and even free tax products while adding higher-ARPU services such as TurboTax Live and QuickBooks Advanced. The key monitor is whether Credit Karma and Mailchimp solve earlier customer problems rather than merely expanding the product map.

## Key Insights

> **1.** QuickBooks is a standard, not just software.
>
> **Q** Why is QuickBooks hard to displace?
>
> **A** Accountants do not want records from five accounting systems, and small businesses do not want to relearn bookkeeping. QuickBooks' 90% share, app integrations, and accountant referral channel reinforce each other, as Xero discovered when it failed to crack the U.S. market despite pricing and referral incentives.

> **2.** Tax complexity creates demand, but simplification may not be fatal.
>
> **Q** What happens if tax filing gets easier?
>
> **A** Feeley argues simplification could shift more assisted tax dollars into DIY or TurboTax Live. Intuit already serves 8-10 million low-income filers for free, the federal government committed not to enter tax prep, and Credit Karma Tax peaked around 3 million filers before falling after divestiture.

> **3.** Credit Karma and Mailchimp are data and timing bets.
>
> **Q** Why did Intuit buy outside the core?
>
> **A** Credit Karma extends Bill Campbell-era personal finance ambitions by matching consumers to financial products using richer tax-return signals. Mailchimp reaches small businesses before they choose accounting, payroll, or payments, and brings a more international, viral SMB acquisition motion than QuickBooks alone.

## Chronology

> *Intuit*
>
> **1983** Scott Cook and Tom Proulx create Quicken after Cook observes household checkbook pain.
>
> **Early 1990s** Surveys reveal many Quicken users are small businesses, inspiring QuickBooks.
>
> **1994** Microsoft offers about $1.5 billion for Intuit; DOJ blocks the deal.
>
> **1990s** TurboTax joins Intuit as Microsoft pushes into personal finance software.
>
> **2013** Brad Smith era refocuses the company on core products and sheds assets including Quicken and Rocket-related holdings.
>
> **2020** Intuit acquires Credit Karma, adding a 100 million-member consumer finance site.
>
> **2021** Intuit acquires Mailchimp, adding CRM and marketing tools for small businesses.
