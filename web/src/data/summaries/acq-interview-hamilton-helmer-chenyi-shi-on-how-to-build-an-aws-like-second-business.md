## Mental Model · *Second Invention Under Power Umbrella*

> **Components**
>
> Step 1: Brutally identify existing Power (counter-positioning, scale, network, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power). Step 2: Ask if new business expands under same umbrella — Uber Eats partially overlaps drivers/consumers but not fully (hence separate app). International expansion often replicates hard engineering but repeats expensive marketplace density build. Step 3: If no umbrella fit, search lower-right — existing capabilities, different customer need, prefer differential skill vs competitors (AWS, 3M Post-its from weak adhesive). Step 4: Founder/sponsor required for lower-left (new skills, new customers). Power-law returns justify innovation portfolio despite low hit rate — Microsoft historically required $1B revenue potential. Acquisitions: seek revenue synergies (unlimited upside) over cost cuts (capped); enterprise plug-ins (Salesforce, Oracle) high probability, lower magnitude than Marvel. Apple coaction pattern vs car's new-skill burden.
>
> **Application**
>
> Before launching 'AWS for X,' map whether capabilities or power transfer — most network expansions are weaker than they appear geographically. Incumbents with distribution (Visa credit extension, Blockbuster stores) can fail by not exploiting power when business model shifts. Strategy investors should separate PMF validation from moat construction timeline. Founders post-first-hit must avoid repeating exact process — luck/skill unknowable.

Weakness: framework less predictive 10 years out; hit-rate still low — Blockbuster, failed conglomerates abound. Enterprise M&A success doesn't produce AWS-scale outcomes. ## Key Insights

> **1.** Seven Powers defends; it doesn't ideate.
>
> **Q** When should founders use the framework?
>
> **A** After product-market fit when building durable advantage 4–8 quarters out — not to validate raw ideas. Bezos baked current quarter years ago; powers ask whether castle holds once built. Second invention is separate creative act from first insight.

> **2.** Half of profits come from 'second acts.'
>
> **Q** How common are second businesses?
>
> **A** Chenyi's research ≈50% of profits at largest US companies from non-original innovation — lower than Ben's 70–80% guess but economically huge. Power-law distribution means portfolio approach can be rational despite low replication rate.

> **3.** Network power rarely teleports.
>
> **Q** Why didn't Uber leverage Seattle in SF?
>
> **A** Local two-sided density is geographic — drivers and riders in Seattle don't help San Francisco. Eats adds partial driver overlap but different consumers; separate Uber Eats app reflected incomplete network overlap vs rides.

> **4.** AWS is the lower-right matrix ideal.
>
> **Q** Where do second winners come from?
>
> **A** Repurpose existing capabilities for different customer jobs where you have differential skill — Amazon infrastructure to AWS, 3M adhesives to Post-its. Lower-left needs founder champion; top-right is core product; lower-right is corporate strategy sweet spot.

> **5.** Nintendo had scale and network together.
>
> **Q** 1980s Nintendo: scale or network power?
>
> **A** Ben/David debated; Hamilton/Chenyi affirm both intertwined at ≈95% share — third-party publishers followed installed base (Final Fantasy, Dragon Quest), amortizing development across massive console base while base grew from must-have titles.
