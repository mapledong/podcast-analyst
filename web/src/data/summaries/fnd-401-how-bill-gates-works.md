## Background

> Senra builds a 'how Bill Gates works' episode from Source Code plus Hard Drive, Overdrive, and other Microsoft histories — stripping everything except company-building mechanics. Young Gates was hyperkinetic, contrarian, and combative with his mother until a therapist told his parents: 'Give it up — he's going to win.' Lakeside's teletype mainframe let teens program when almost no schools had computers; Gates and Paul Allen devoured nights and weekends while classmates drifted away.
>
> Gates discovered lean engineering through memory constraints — sloppy code hogged scarce kilobytes — and ran Microsoft the same way: visceral hatred of waste, constant cost howling, and deliberate energy allocation. He read biographies of Roosevelt, Napoleon, and Henry Ford with Allen, analyzing paths to success 'with intensity.' Family therapy's verdict — loosen your grip or you will break before he does — foreshadowed decades of immovable will.

## Key Facts

> **F1** At Lakeside, Gates had access to a $500,000 mainframe by phone line at ≈13 — rare for any teenager. He once worked nearly 100 hours straight and routinely did 36-hour coding stretches collapsed into 10-hour sleeps. Hard Drive notes a habit of 81 hours in four days; when Gates checked in, he asked engineer Bob Greenberg what he was working on tomorrow.

> **F2** Microsoft sold 5% for $1 million to TVI (Technology Venture Investors) as a carefully planned cushion — capital that, years later with ≈$5 billion more, Gates said still sat in the bank because software economics needed no heavy capex. When IBM came calling with ≈$30 billion in revenue, Microsoft had ≈$2.4 million in annual revenue — Gates held software-only focus while rivals bundled hardware.

> **F3** Gates ran cost control as religion — Warren Buffett's 'demon on cost' — with John Mackey noting Whole Foods might have survived independence with founders-level expense discipline. Gates lived in binary states: apathetic or completely obsessed; reading was default state, devouring World Book A-to-Z and entire shelves on a topic. Larry Ellison: 'There are very few people in the world that have his focus and endurance.'

## Mental Model · *Fanatical Focus on Software Economics*

> **Components**
>
> Design the company around innate personality — Gates's fanaticism, lean-code discipline, and competitive combativeness became cultural defaults. Constraints bred quality: limited memory forced tight programs; limited capital forced expense obsession. Virgin territory (no playbook for microcomputer software) attracted hard-core adherents who would not quit when peers left. Gates deliberately chose what deserved energy and ignored everything else.
>
> **Application**
>
> Software businesses win by owning a layer rivals treat as accessory — IBM brought distribution, Microsoft kept the high-margin software and repeated the model against every hardware partner. Cost discipline in boom times preserves optionality; Gates never stopped howling about waste even at ≈20,000 employees and ≈$20 billion net worth. Endurance over decades requires authentic fit — Gates could not understand why founders rarely still ran companies 21 years in.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Lean engineering is good engineering.
>
> **Q** How did mainframe limits shape Microsoft?
>
> **A** Tiny memory forced concise code — easier to write sloppy long programs than tight short ones. Gates carried the same distaste for waste into business operations, matching James Dyson and Elon Musk on simplicity as engineering virtue.

> **2.** Hard core beats head count.
>
> **Q** Who survived Lakeside's computer club?
>
> **A** Most kids lost interest; a small hard-core group stayed — Gates's favorite word. He was an eighth grader convinced his intensity let him match or beat older programmers, determined that 'no one would get anything on me.'

> **3.** Software-only was the contrarian wedge against IBM.
>
> **Q** Why refuse IBM's hardware path?
>
> **A** While ≈20,000-employee Microsoft faced giants doing hardware and software, Gates insisted on software only — the high-margin, scalable layer. He saw ≈$55 billion of industry value ahead while IBM brought $30 billion of revenue and brand.

## Chronology

> *Bill Gates · Microsoft*
>
> **1968** Lakeside School connects students to mainframe computer; Gates and Allen become hard-core programmers.
>
> **Early 1970s** Gates does 36–100 hour coding stretches; reads World Book A-to-Z; family therapy yields 'he will win.'
>
> **1975** Gates drops out of Harvard; co-founds Microsoft with Allen on software-only conviction.
>
> **1980** IBM PC deal — Microsoft keeps software rights while IBM brings ≈$30B revenue scale.
>
> **1981** TVI buys 5% of Microsoft for $1 million; revenue ≈$2.4 million that year.
>
> **1986** Microsoft goes public; Gates maintains cost obsession and software focus.
>
> **1990s** ≈20,000 employees, ≈$20B net worth — Gates still says IPO cash sits in bank with billions more.
