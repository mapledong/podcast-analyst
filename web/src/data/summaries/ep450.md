## Background

> David George runs a16z Growth and explains how the firm invests across private and public technology markets. He discusses AI consumer products, enterprise AI, Waymo, robotics, market leadership, technical founders, Figma, Roblox, Cursor, Databricks, and the institutionalization of private markets. The episode is less about one stock and more about how growth investors compete when the best private companies are obvious, expensive, and relationship-driven. George's answer is years of founder work plus product and market insight that a spreadsheet cannot show.

## Mental Model · *People, Product, Market Edge*

> **Components**
>
> Consumer AI may be huge, but the interface is probably not just chat. George says ChatGPT has staggering usage but monetizes a tiny share. The next leap may come from AI interacting with the web and completing tasks, not from a static chatbot interface. Market leadership still matters most in technology. He uses the Glengarry Glen Ross framing: first prize matters and second or third can be painful. Most market cap creation goes to the leader, especially in software, consumer, and fintech. Growth investing now requires private-market maturity. Technology is now eight of the ten largest market-cap companies, seven of them West Coast venture-backed. With ≈$5T of private market cap, the best rounds attract large multi-stage firms and specialist boutiques, so years of relationship work matter.
>
> **Application**
>
> George describes years of relationship-building before many competitive rounds, then using product and market conviction to act at valuations that looked expensive at the time. In Figma, the critical call was that design would converge with engineering workflows, expanding the addressable market. In AI, he applies the same logic to autonomy and consumer software where monetization may lag adoption by years.
