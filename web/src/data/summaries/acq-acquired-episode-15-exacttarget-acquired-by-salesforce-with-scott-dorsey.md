## Background

> Interview format with Scott Dorsey — Ben's cousin and ExactTarget co-founder CEO. Founded Dec 2000 Indianapolis post dot-com bust with ≈$200K friends-and-family; bootstrapped through dry cleaners and pizza shops before enterprise stair-step to Microsoft as customer. Inside VC raised $10.5M (2004) and $7M more (2006) at ≈$40M revenue trajectory. IPO March 2012 at $19/share; trading ≈$22 day before June 2013 acquisition at $2.5B ($33.75/share, ≈50% premium). SEC proxy disclosed Party A/B/C competing bidders. Category: business line — Salesforce selling to sales orgs needed CMO/marketing automation (Pardot bundled). Scott ran Marketing Cloud post-close.

## Key Facts

> **F1** ExactTarget was founded in December 2000 in Indianapolis with roughly $200,000 in friends-and-family funding after the dot-com bust; co-founders Scott Dorsey, Chris Baggot, and Peter McCormick bootstrapped through dry cleaners and pizza shops before stair-stepping to enterprise customers including Microsoft. Hosts quote Mark Benioff preaching ExactTarget as template Salesforce acquisition on big stages.

> **F2** Salesforce acquired ExactTarget in June 2013 for $2.5 billion at $33.75 per share — roughly a 50% premium over the roughly $22 trading price the day before; the company had IPO'd in March 2012 at $19 per share, just over one year earlier. 2011 private raise exceeded proceeds cited from the eventual IPO per episode discussion.

> **F3** ExactTarget's final standalone year (2012) produced roughly $294 million in revenue; Salesforce's Marketing Cloud division reached roughly $654 million in fiscal 2016 (calendar 2015) — more than 2× growth in under three years post-acquisition. Salesforce bundles Pardot alongside ExactTarget for marketing automation completeness versus Oracle.

> **F4** Inside VC invested $10.5 million in 2004 and $7 million in 2006 when ExactTarget reached roughly $40 million in revenue; a 2007 IPO filing was withdrawn amid the 2008–09 financial crisis before a successful March 2012 public debut. Scott discussed M&A process stress and board subcommittee formation when offers arrive.

> **F5** Ben Gilbert graded the deal A- (Marketing Cloud grew from $294M to $654M); David Rosenthal agreed; the SEC acquisition proxy disclosed competing bidders labeled Party A, B, and C; Salesforce still reports Marketing Cloud as a distinct business line that Scott Dorsey ran after the close. David's tech theme: four enterprise giants win via account control with CIOs and CMOs.

## Mental Model · *Account Control Expansion*

> **Components**
>
> Enterprise suite vendors (Microsoft, SAP, Oracle, Salesforce) win through account control — owning CIO and CMO relationships then cross-selling modules. ExactTarget gave Salesforce its first major CMO channel alongside Sales Cloud, Pardot, and the gorilla enterprise sales force Scott Dorsey's Midwestern commission culture built.
>
> **Application**
>
> B2B investors should map suite completeness versus point solutions — Salesforce needed marketing automation to match Oracle's Eloqua acquisition. Founders outside Silicon Valley can win via capital efficiency and SMB-to-enterprise stair-steps. Sub-$1,000 credit-card SaaS bypasses CIO procurement for land-and-expand.

---

## What Makes It Work

> ExactTarget moat was enterprise email deliverability, cross-channel expansion (SMS, social CoTweet), and Midwestern enterprise sales discipline. Salesforce added gorilla distribution — same customers, new buyer persona (CMO). Marketing Cloud branding persists as P&L line Scott Dorsey ran. Hosts grade A-: business-line acquisition giving Salesforce CMO channel via 'account control' sales force. Weakness: marketing automation point products faced Oracle/Adobe roll-ups; standalone IPO path blocked by 2008 crisis; Scott skeptical one independent marketing SaaS giant could emerge at scale — ExactTarget, Eloqua, others swallowed by suite vendors.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Business line, not feature.
>
> **Q** Category?
>
> **A** Full marketing hub suite (email, SMS, social) with channel to customers Salesforce lacked — still called Marketing Cloud in earnings. David: Salesforce needed CMO access after years selling only to sales orgs; Pardot bundled alongside ExactTarget. Scott confirmed business-line classification; Salesforce still breaks out Marketing Cloud revenue separately in fiscal results. David categorized this as a business-line acquisition giving Salesforce its CMO channel alongside Sales Cloud.

> **2.** Bootstrap to $294M.
>
> **Q** Indianapolis lesson?
>
> **A** Scott built on $200K through SMB stair-step — dry cleaners, pizza shops, franchisees — to Microsoft enterprise logo. Commission-only early sales shaped org DNA. Inside VC $10.5M (2004) and $7M (2006) at ≈$40M revenue; 2007 IPO filing exposed financials pre-JOBS Act before 2008 withdrawal. Raised private capital exceeding IPO proceeds before March 2012 public debut at $19/share. Scott built commission-only early sales culture through dry cleaners and pizza shops before Microsoft logo.

> **3.** Buy vs sell timing.
>
> **Q** Why sell at premium?
>
> **A** Hosts quote 'great acquisitions happen when companies are bought not sold' — Q1 beat during process supported $2.5B price ($33.75/share, ≈50% premium over ≈$22 trading). SEC proxy disclosed Party A, B, C competing bidders. Just over one year from IPO to acquisition (March 2012 to June 2013). Scott on process stress and subcommittee formation when offers arrive. Party A, B, and C competing bids appear in the SEC acquisition proxy Scott discussed on air.

> **4.** Marketing roll-up era.
>
> **Q** Could standalone giant emerge?
>
> **A** ExactTarget, Eloqua, others swallowed by Oracle/Salesforce — Scott skeptical one independent marketing SaaS giant at scale. David asked whether consolidation among marketing companies could have built a fourth enterprise giant; hosts conclude suite vendors (Microsoft, SAP, Oracle, Salesforce) own account control with CIOs and CMOs. Revenue grew $294M (2012 standalone) to $654M Marketing Cloud (FY2016). Scott was skeptical one independent marketing SaaS giant could emerge — roll-up era swallowed Eloqua too.

> **5.** Credit-card land expand.
>
> **Q** Tech theme?
>
> **A** Sub-$1000 SaaS lets advocates buy without CIO — opens PSL/Pioneer Square Labs-style fast first customer landings. Ben: advocate in organization can use credit card when product under $1000. David's tech theme: enterprise outsourcing non-core functions to SaaS; ExactTarget exemplified gorilla channel pushing new product through existing Salesforce relationships — 'account control' expansion. Ben cited sub-$1,000 credit-card SaaS enabling PSL-style first customer landings without CIO approval.
