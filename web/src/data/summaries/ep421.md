## Background

> Cliff Sosin founded CAS Investment Partners after Houlihan Lokey, Silver Point, and UBS. He built the firm around concentrated, long-duration ownership of businesses through public stocks, even though that approach is hard to market because feedback loops are noisy and multi-year. Carvana is the centerpiece. Sosin first studied it in 2018, watched the company scale rapidly, then defended the position through a 99% drawdown when units, losses, leverage, and sentiment all moved against him.

> **F2** Carvana was founded in 2013. > **F3** Carvana's large inspection and reconditioning centers can handle up to about 40,000 cars per year and hold 6,000-8,000 cars. ## Mental Model · *Ugly Path, Superior System*

> **Components**
>
> Carvana's moat is the whole system, not the website. The experience requires national inventory, local hubs, nine-car haulers, large IRCs, customer financing, trust, reconditioning speed, and edge-case handling. Competitors can imitate pieces, but the integrated network took years and billions to assemble. The 2022 crisis was partly self-inflicted and partly survivable. Carvana scaled for a much larger 2022 after selling roughly 425,000 cars in 2021, then demand slowed, losses ballooned, ADESA added debt, and the stock fell 99%. Sosin's claim is that the business is now more profitable and back on track. Long-only concentration is hard because it is anti-marketing. He buys a small number of stocks as business ownership and waits through noisy, multi-year feedback loops. That does not produce the clean, short-cycle proof points committees like, so it maximizes returns more than marketability.

>
> **Q** Why is online used-car retail so hard to copy?
>
> **A** The experience requires national inventory, local hubs, nine-car haulers, large IRCs, customer financing, trust, reconditioning speed, and edge-case handling. > **2.** The 2022 crisis was partly self-inflicted and partly survivable.
>
> **Q** What went wrong?
>
> **A** Carvana scaled for a much larger 2022 after selling roughly 425,000 cars in 2021, then demand slowed, losses ballooned, ADESA added debt, and the stock fell 99%.
