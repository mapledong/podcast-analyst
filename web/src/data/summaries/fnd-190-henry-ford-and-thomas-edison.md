## Background

> Senra covers the friendship between Ford and Edison — poor boys who became icons, mutual suspicion of financiers, and 'Vagabonds' camping trips with Harvey Firestone and naturalist John Burroughs. Edison told young Ford in 1896, 'Young man, that's the thing — keep at it,' when Ford showed an internal-combustion engine path while most cars were steam or electric failures.
>
> Ford failed twice before the third company succeeded; the Model T arrived 12 years after Edison's encouragement — a reminder not to quit. Ford idolized Edison yet became far wealthier by expanding an existing market rather than inventing new ones. Edison jumped between projects; Ford stuck with one car, one process, one obsession: eliminate waste and cut price continuously.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** Model T introduction: ≈194,000 cars owned in America; six years later ≈2 million — ≈10× growth with Ford holding nearly half the market. Ford cut prices relentlessly (ads showed $295 Model T) and stopped traditional advertising because price drops were newsworthy. Black paint only because it dried fastest — faster assembly line throughput. Model T weighed ≈1,200 pounds, half competitors, lowering repair and tire costs.

> **F2** In 1914 Ford announced $5 daily wages and 8-hour days versus industry norms of ≈$2 for 10-hour shifts, 60-hour weeks — 'everyone in America was talking about it.' At ≈1919 Ford owned 100% of Ford Motor Company valued around $500 million in contemporary dollars. He later borrowed ≈$60M of ≈$106M to buy out minority shareholders and gain full control.

> **F3** Edison and Ford bonded over distrust of Wall Street and classroom elitism — neither had college degrees. Ford bought Burroughs's family farm outright when Burroughs feared losing it; gifted Model Ts for publicity when Burroughs rode in them. Edison invented broadly but struggled to capture value; Ford's hobby was work — 'devoted almost every waking minute' — with employees forbidden to disagree.

## Mental Model · *Price as Marketing, Waste as Enemy*

> **Components**
>
> Ford's loop: simplify product (any color black if it speeds paint), simplify labor (assembly line, $5 day to reduce turnover), pass savings as price cuts that generate free press. Lean engineering and aiming for simplicity were explicit principles from My Life and Work. Edison supplied inspiration and friendship; Ford supplied industrial discipline — one market, one product, decades of incremental process improvement versus Edison's portfolio of unfinished commercializations.
>
> **Application**
>
> Manufacturers who compete on features lose to those who compete on delivered cost per unit — if your line takes 5 hours per car and Ford's takes 2.5 minutes, you lose. Wage increases can expand your customer base when paired with productivity gains. Inventors need Ford-style operators (or become them) to compound wealth; friendship with heroes does not substitute for capture mechanics.

---


## Key Insights

> **1.** Simplicity and speed compound.
>
> **Q** Why only black Model Ts?
>
> **A** Black paint dried fastest — cars moved through the line quicker, saving labor dollars. Combined with ≈1,200-pound weight (half rivals), utilitarian design passed savings to buyers and made each price cut headline news.

> **2.** Pay workers enough to buy the product.
>
> **Q** What did $5/day accomplish?
>
> **A** Doubling prevailing ≈$2 wages while cutting hours to eight shocked the nation — marketing coup and customer creation. Ford controlled ~half the U.S. auto market six years after Model T launch as car ownership 10×'d.

> **3.** Stick to one market longer than inventors do.
>
> **Q** Why was Ford richer than Edison?
>
> **A** Edison jumped across inventions without capturing full value; Ford expanded an existing market with one car and process obsession for 12+ years after Edison's 1896 encouragement. Ford: 'He only needed to expand them.'
