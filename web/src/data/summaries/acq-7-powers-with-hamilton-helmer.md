## Mental Model · *Power = Mechanism for Persistent Differential Returns*

> **Components**
>
> Moat is the outcome (sustainable profit above cost of capital); power is the causal mechanism creating it. Seven Powers: counter-positioning, scale economies, network economies, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power — each with entry conditions (often during industry flux/take-off). TAM is necessary but insufficient — big market without power yields commoditized growth. SaaS switching costs underpin VC models (low churn assumption). Counter-positioning differs from Innovator's Dilemma low-end disruption — can be objectively better product that incumbent cannot copy economically. Helmer's Strategy Capital applies the grid in public equities; book club LPs got early manuscript access.
>
> **Application**
>
> When analyzing Acquired companies (or studio investments), ask which power(s) apply at inflection — not just "great product." Startups should seek counter-positioning versus incumbents whose margins forbid matching (Blockbuster late fees). Public investors (Strategy Capital) use one-page grid for real-time decisions. Rare powers (cornered resource) shouldn't be assumed in tech — default to scale, network, switching costs. Watch market take-off phases with "radar screen" for power creation windows. Branding power (luxury goods, Apple ecosystem) and process power (Toyota production system) appear less often in Acquired tech episodes but complete the seven. Scale economies often compound with network effects (AWS, Facebook) — diagnose primary versus reinforcing power. Helmer teases book two on applications and book three on geography — framework still evolving when recorded.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Power and moat are related but not interchangeable.
>
> **Q** How does Helmer define power versus moats?
>
> **A** Moat describes sustained advantage outcome; power names the mechanism producing persistent differential returns above cost of capital. Seven Powers are exhaustive categories Helmer found after decades — each with identifiable entry barriers. Moat-talk without mechanism risks post-hoc storytelling; Powers framework forces explicit diagnosis (e.g., Netflix counter-positioning on late fees, not generic "better UX").

> **2.** Counter-positioning is the startup's sharpest power — if conditions fit.
>
> **Q** When can a newcomer beat entrenched incumbents?
>
> **A** New model must be attractive standalone AND economically toxic for incumbent to imitate because of cannibalization (Blockbuster late fees ≈40% of profit). Not always Christensen low-end/worse product — can be objectively superior. Gurley "Less Than Free" Android distribution parallels giving dollars for 90¢ to buy share. Helmer's favorite power because relevant at founding — unlike scale/network that need years. Netflix example recurs across Acquired catalog as canonical teaching case.

> **3.** Switching costs finance the SaaS venture model.
>
> **Q** Why do low per-seat prices work in enterprise software?
>
> **A** Salesforce-style switching costs (data gravity, workflow embedding) make churn low enough that inside sales + land-and-expand math works — VCs capitalize expected retention. Slack example: innovated team messaging but now battles Microsoft Teams with worse economic customer acquisition. Switching cost power erodes when incumbent can bundle (Office + Teams). Export-to-competitor features (Slides from PowerPoint) never perfect — friction protects incumbent.

> **4.** Cornered resource is overrated in tech, underrated when truly present.
>
> **Q** Pixar versus Intel as cornered resource cases?
>
> **A** Pixar combined Lasseter (story), Catmull (tech), Jobs (capital/vision) — non-replicable bundle; Bob Iger bought company because hiring away failed. Intel/Gordon Moore cornered early transistor know-how but faced race-to-bottom as capability spread. Biotech patents are classic cornered resource. Tech founders overclaim talent moats; Helmer urges rarity default — look for scale/network/switching first.

> **5.** Powers emerge in take-off flux — maintain strategic radar.
>
> **Q** How do you spot power before it's obvious?
>
> **A** Helmer advises watching industries in flux (take-off phase) where barriers reset — Netflix streaming shift, early cloud, etc. Not ready for "applications" book yet — still codifying. Powers often compound (AWS: counter-positioning + scale + switching). Acquired applies Helmer every episode; this remaster exists because framework became show grammar without listeners hearing primary source.
