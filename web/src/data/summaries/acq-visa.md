## Background

> Ben and David trace payments from pre-war charge plates through Diners Club (1950) and Amex (1850 freight roots) to BofA's 1958 Fresno drop — 65,000 unsolicited cards, ≈$20M fraud, 22% delinquency versus ≈4% traditional lending, 2M cardholders year one. Franchise model spread BankAmericard nationally until banks demanded independence at 1970 Columbus summit. Dee Hock — National Bank of Commerce, Iowa — led revolt and 1977 Sausalito summit: created NBI/Visa as for-profit non-stock membership corp, proportional ownership, non-transferable equity, interchange to settle issuer/acquirer splits. International banks merged into IBANCO; rebranding to Visa (1976) solved BankAmericard's English-only limit. Dave Stearns' Electronic Value Exchange is primary source. Episode frames Visa as neutral railroad — among highest-margin large companies, not from lending but from moving authorized transactions.

## Key Facts

> **F1** 1958 Fresno drop: Bank of America mailed 65,000 unsolicited BankAmericards — first mass credit card experiment. ≈$20M fraud losses in pilot; 22% delinquency versus ≈4% on traditional lending — only BofA's California scale could absorb hit and continue. Reached 2M cardholders year one versus Diners Club taking years to reach 1M.

> **F2** Dee Hock structured Visa as non-stock membership cooperative: ownership proportional to transaction volume, non-transferable — cannot sell stake and exit network. Designed like NFL revenue share where 17% transaction share yields 17% of network economics; Hock asked franchise banks: own a few percent of global default rail or 100% of BankAmericard?

> **F3** Interchange emerged when issuer and acquirer became different banks — unlike Amex closed-loop where one company owns both sides. Visa settles net flows between thousands of institutions; merchant discount fees evolved from check-courier 'discounting' deposits before electronic authorization.

> **F4** 1970 Columbus summit: franchise banks revolted against BofA keeping brand, rules, and interchange headaches. 1974 antitrust pressure; Sausalito summit (1977) solidified structure; IBANCO merged international operators; rebranded Visa (1976) because BankAmericard was English-only — blue-gold-blue mark translates globally.

> **F5** Visa IPO 2008 as public company atop for-profit cooperative origins — myth of nonprofit conversion is wrong per episode. Today processes trillions at software-like margins without balance-sheet credit risk on Visa Inc itself; Amex closed-loop still competes on premium spend while Visa wins on open-loop ubiquity.

## Mental Model · *Cooperative Ubiquity Race*

> **Components**
>
> Payment networks win by simultaneous density on both sides — merchants need universal acceptance signal; consumers need one card. BofA's Fresno experiment (65,000 unsolicited cards, ≈$20M fraud, 22% delinquency) proved demand but franchise model failed because franchisor captured brand and rules. Hock's insight: banks own collectively proportional to participation, not tradable equity — prevents exit cascades. Interchange aligns issuers (fraud, credit) and acquirers (merchant sign-up) while Visa Inc runs rails. Brand mark (blue-white-gold) was moat when technology primitive. International required neutral name; IBANCO merger before Visa rebrand (1976). Closed-loop (Amex) captures premium; open-loop (Visa) becomes default rail.
>
> **Application**
>
> Platform builders choosing open vs closed: open networks sacrifice per-transaction capture for ubiquity — Visa chose slice of giant pie over 100% of small BankAmericard. Membership coop structures fit multi-party industries where no single owner should control rules. When evaluating fintech, ask who bears fraud/credit vs who owns rails — Visa avoids lending risk. New entrants (Apple Pay, BNPL) ride Visa rails because 65,000-card Fresno experiment proved acceptance density is the product.

## What Makes It Work

> Visa's durable edge is neutral multi-bank orchestration at planetary scale — not proprietary credit underwriting. Fresno drop (65,000 cards, ≈$20M fraud, 22% delinquency) proved mass-market demand only BofA could absorb. Every major bank can issue Visa; merchants display one mark; interchange clears issuer/acquirer economics without Amex closed-loop conflict. Dee Hock's governance prevented banks from selling membership — preserving participation when scale made network valuable. Hock's Jets/NFL analogy: would you rather own a few percent of the default global commerce rail or 100% of BankAmericard? Rules, fraud systems, BIN routing, and brand trust compound over decades. Switching costs are collective: leaving degrades ubiquity for all. Amex competes on premium spend; Mastercard mirrors Visa — duopoly dynamics. PayPal, Apple Pay, Stripe are layers atop Visa because acceptance density is the product. Weaknesses: interchange regulation; merchant fee resentment; fraud arms race. Visa Inc captures network economics without credit losses — among highest operating margins globally. Moat is coordination, not secret tech — Diners Club proved replication difficulty. 2M California cardholders in BankAmericard year one versus Diners taking years to reach 1M affluent members proved mass-market scale after Fresno. 1976 Visa rebrand followed IBANCO international merger; 2008 IPO converted operating company while member banks retained economic participation through non-transferable coop stakes. Visa and Mastercard duopoly persists — new fintech layers ride rails rather than replace acceptance density.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Only BofA could survive the Fresno experiment.
>
> **Q** Why did unsolicited cards work?
>
> **A** 65,000 mailed BankAmericards produced ≈$20M fraud and 22% delinquency versus ≈4% on traditional loans — fatal for smaller banks. BofA's California scale absorbed losses while building fraud systems; 2M cardholders year one proved mass-market credit demand. Diners Club (1950) and Amex stayed affluent niches — Fresno unsolicited experiment required balance-sheet capacity only incumbents possessed.

> **2.** Franchisees revolted over ownership, not product.
>
> **Q** Why did BankAmericard need Dee Hock?
>
> **A** BofA kept brand, rules, and interchange headaches while franchise banks bore losses — classic misaligned platform. 1970 Columbus summit nearly existential; Dee Hock from National Bank of Commerce, Iowa proposed proportional non-transferable ownership matching transaction share (17% share → 17% economics). Without coop structure at Sausalito summit (1977), competing regional BankAmericard/Master Charge networks would have fragmented US acceptance.

> **3.** Interchange is the business model.
>
> **Q** Why not closed-loop like Amex?
>
> **A** When issuer and acquirer split across competing banks, interchange nets fraud/credit/service costs — replaces check discounting and courier fees before electronic authorization existed. Visa Inc does not lend; moves authorized transactions and splits ≈2% era fees between thousands of institutions. Amex closed-loop captures more per user but cannot match open-loop acceptance density — Hock chose ubiquity with globally recognized blue-gold-blue mark over 100% of small closed BankAmericard network.

> **4.** Brand mark equals acceptance signal.
>
> **Q** Why rebrand to Visa?
>
> **A** BankAmericard was English-only; global IBANCO merger needed neutral name translating across languages. Blue-gold-blue stripes recognizable worldwide — early moat was visible universal acceptance decal, not chip technology. Same dynamic persists under Apple Pay tokenization: merchants and consumers still need one trusted mark signaling participation at point of sale globally.

> **5.** Coop governance prevents exit death spirals.
>
> **Q** Why non-transferable membership?
>
> **A** If banks could sell Visa membership stakes, sellers would stop promoting network, collapsing value for remaining members — designed breakeven on fees with profit share matching volume contribution. NFL parallel: share league TV deal proportionally, not solo 1960s Jets contract Hock referenced when asking franchise banks if they wanted few percent of global rail or 100% of BankAmericard. 2008 IPO converted operating company; banks remain economic stakeholders.
