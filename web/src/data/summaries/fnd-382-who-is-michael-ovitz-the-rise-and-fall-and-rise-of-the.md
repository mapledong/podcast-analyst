## Background

> Senra rereads Ovitz's autobiography Who Is Michael Ovitz? six years after his first pass and after a three-hour New York dinner with Ovitz himself. The episode tracks a San Fernando Valley kid who hated his father's boxed-in liquor-store dream, worked 60-hour weeks at Universal at 19, and revolted against William Morris's seven-year mailroom track by betting he could learn agency work in 120 days on $55 a week.
>
> Ovitz co-founded Creative Artists Agency at 27 with folding chairs, shared clients, no turf wars, and a mandate to create opportunities rather than field offers. Senra covers the Sherman-esque march — overhearing rival Ziggy on a car-phone party line, Ghostbusters packaging, Panasonic's MCA Universal deal, Disney's collapse, and Ovitz's later Silicon Valley reinvention — with Ovitz's own regrets on work-life tradeoffs and misplaced trust.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Ovitz left William Morris in 1975 after bringing in $2 million in 1973 while earning $400 a week plus a $7,500 bonus; five partners started CAA with even equity, shared clients, and card-table desks while wives answered phones one day a week. WMA's lawsuit threat backfired when Ovitz bluffed about feeding it to the Justice Department's antitrust probe — forging CAA's siege mentality — and TV's Thursday pay cycle kept cash flowing while film clients remained locked at bigger agencies.

> **F2** Shogun earned CAA a $1 million commission (≈$3 million today) after the project sat at William Morris for three years; Ghostbusters grossed over $295 million with clients taking 30% of gross and CAA earning 10% of that — more than $30 million over TV and VHS life. Ovitz packaged films industry-wide for the first time, orchestrating development so studios avoided headache and expense; by roughly the first decade CAA held an estimated 75% market share, patterned after Lou Wasserman's MCA playbook.

> **F3** Panasonic paid $6.5 billion for MCA Universal; Ovitz received a $135 million fee check and $60 million for CAA after expenses (≈$110 million today). Coca-Cola paid $1 million per month plus costs for ad consulting; after returning a $10 million bonus check he negotiated $31 million. At Disney he lasted 14 months before Eisner fired him by proxy three days before his 50th birthday party; partner Ron Mayer's hidden $5 million and $6.5 million Vegas poker losses and Ovitz's 1980s equity renegotiation planted a 16-year co-founder time bomb.

## Mental Model · *Do the Job Before You're Hired*

> **Components**
>
> Ovitz's career repeats one pattern: accumulate more information than anyone in the room, then behave as if you already hold the role. At Universal he arrived at 7 a.m., stayed until 9 p.m., walked 400 acres, and crammed the studio guide like a final exam. At William Morris he read the file room A-to-Z, served Sam Weisbord unpaid after hours, and bet 120 days against a seven-year track. At CAA he coached clients by assumption — attaching Steve Gordon to a sold script before signing him — and watched every Oscar-winning film from 1929 for a decade to talk directors' language. Car-phone party lines, red-covered manuscripts in beauty parlors, reading all 44 books by Panasonic's founder before a pitch: the edge is preparation volume, not genius.
>
> **Application**
>
> Present yourself as the solution to the buyer's problem — George Clooney's audition reframe and CAA's packaged development both sell finished answers, not pleas for approval. Expand the revenue surface of your profession before competitors define the box: M&A advisory, corporate consulting, advertising. But Ted Ashley's warning stands — working 10% less over 30 years equals three extra years of life with little professional cost once momentum compounds; and emotional equity splits detonate years later even when logic favors the top producer.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Agents win by creating, not waiting.
>
> **Q** How did CAA break into film against locked-up stars?
>
> **A** Ovitz told a skeptical actress to remove her 'actress hat' and run herself as a small corporation — control projects, develop material, increase her bottom line. CAA took books and scripts to prospects, called literary agent Mort Janklow every Thursday at 10 a.m. for a year before getting Chiefs, then flooded 80 agents with red-covered manuscripts planted in beauty parlors and doctors' offices.

> **2.** Intensity flips power but erodes trust.
>
> **Q** Why did Ron Mayer and Michael Eisner betray Ovitz?
>
> **A** Ron hid $11.5 million in poker losses across two episodes while Ovitz prided himself on reading people; Ron later took the MCA COO job Ovitz had negotiated for both of them. Eisner told his biographer Ovitz's hire was the biggest mistake of his life the same night Ovitz told his wife the same thing — yet Ovitz believed friendship made him immune to Eisner's inability to share power.

> **3.** Origins fuel forever.
>
> **Q** What did Ovitz realize in the empty CAA headquarters?
>
> **A** Walking the building Sony leased for 20 years after he left, he saw a valley kid with no stature reshape entertainment — free in daily life and bank account but never in his brain. He missed the camaraderie: 'the passionate way we spent our lives together.' Charlie Munger's seamless web of deserved trust — build life around people you can work with for decades.

## Investment Ideas

> **1. DIS** · 🟡 WATCH · ●●○ Medium
>
> Ovitz's 14-month Disney tenure — hired as Michael Eisner's number two, blocked on innovation, fired by proxy — illustrates the risk when founders who cannot share spotlight hire perceived rivals; Eisner and Ovitz each told confidants the pairing was a mistake before Ovitz accepted.

> **2. CMCSA** · 🟡 WATCH · ●○○ Low
>
> Ovitz's $6.5 billion Panasonic-MCA Universal deal and Lou Wasserman's later mistake passing on Raiders of the Lost Ark over a 50-50 split — while calling a 15-year film-cost bubble inflated — frame legacy media M&A as timing trades where incumbents can sit out growth and cede share.

## Chronology

> *Michael Ovitz · CAA · Disney*
>
> **1960s** Teenage Ovitz works Universal studio tours 7 a.m.–9 p.m., studying power brokers not movie stars.
>
> **1975** Leaves William Morris mailroom; co-founds CAA with five partners at age 27.
>
> **1976** Shogun mini-series earns CAA its first $1 million commission after three years at William Morris.
>
> **1981** Signs Sydney Pollack after two-year courtship; poaches partner Steve Roth and effectively ends rival Ziggy.
>
> **1990** Panasonic acquires MCA Universal for $6.5 billion; CAA earns ≈$60 million in fees.
>
> **1995–1996** Joins Disney as vice chairman; fired after 14 months following rift with Michael Eisner.
>
> **2010s–2020s** Reinvents in Silicon Valley — investing in tech, advising venture firms, scaling new companies.
