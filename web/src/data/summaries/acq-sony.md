## Background

> Season 10 Episode 3 (≈183 minutes) traces Sony from 1945 Tokyo rubble through Akio Morita and Masaru Ibuka's partnership. Morita was 15th-generation heir to a Nagoya sake empire; Ibuka's founding prospectus promised engineers' joy before naming any product. The episode covers transistor licensing from Bell Labs (1952), the TR-63 pocket radio (1.5M units at $25), Morita's Bulova OEM rejection, Walkman, Betamax's ecosystem loss, CBS Records ($2B, 1988) and Columbia Pictures ($3.4B, 1989), the Nintendo-PlayStation betrayal, PS2's 150M+ unit record, PS3's ≈$5B loss, smartphone exit, imaging sensor dominance (~half the market), and Spider-Man's MCU deal where Sony keeps 95% of box office. Ben and David grade conglomerate coherence versus Apple and Samsung pure-plays.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Japan per-capita income ≈$17 in 1946; ≈47% of Tokyo homeless after WWII when Ibuka founded Tokyo Telecommunications Research Institute. Japan had ≈70M citizens vs US ≈130M pre-war — technology gap, not population, drove defeat.

> **F2** Morita family invested ¥190,000 for 17% stake (eventually ≈10% worth ≈$5B by 1999 — more than the 400-year sake empire). TR-63 transistor radio: 1.5M units at $25 each, orders of magnitude above tape-recorder government sales.

> **F3** Akio Morita rejected Bulova OEM order for millions of units unless branded Sony — his single best business decision. Morita was 15th-generation heir to Nagoya sake dynasty; license plate AKM15 referenced lineage.

> **F4** Sony acquired CBS Records for $2B (1988) and Columbia Pictures for $3.4B (1989). Sony Music generates >$2B annual operating cash flow after 30 years — ≈11-12% of company revenue from music segment alone.

> **F5** PlayStation 1 sold >100M units (Super Nintendo 50M); 8000 games vs N64's 400; >1B games sold. PS2: >150M units — most successful console ever. 2021: gaming ≈30% of ¥9.9T revenue (~¥2.9T); ≈50% of profits. PS3 lost ≈$5B first three years. Imaging/sensors: 11% revenue, 14% profits, ~half the market. Spider-Man: No Way Home grossed $1.7B worldwide; Sony keeps 95%. 2014: Sony Financial Group was 63% of operating profit.

## Mental Model · *Content-Hardware Flywheel (Sony)*

> **Components**
>
> Morita/Ibuka pairing: engineer sanctuary (Ibuka's 1945 prospectus promised workplace joy before any product) plus global brand/marketing (Morita's US relocation, Bulova rejection on 1.5M TR-63 units at $25). Hardware breakthroughs fund content bets — Walkman, PlayStation (>100M PS1, >150M PS2, >1B games sold). Betamax failure: Morita ordered $20M US ad campaign Harvey declined — format wars need ecosystem partners. Nintendo CD betrayal converted Sony from supplier to platform owner in a ≈$180B games market versus ≈$50B Hollywood. Conglomerate requires silo profitability: 2014 Sony Financial Group 63% of operating profit; 2021 gaming ≈30% of ¥9.9T revenue/≈50% profits; imaging 11%/14% with ~half sensor market. Spider-Man MCU deal keeps 95% of $1.7B No Way Home gross.
>
> **Application**
>
> When hardware companies buy content, ask if integration creates exclusive distribution or diversifies mediocrity. Sony succeeds when hardware pulls content (PS exclusives, Spider-Man). Struggles when studio economics dominate or smartphone miss erodes brand. Track gaming attach rates, Pictures margins, and sensor share as sum-of-parts indicators. Jobs idolized Sony uniformity; Apple avoided film-studio capital intensity entirely.

## What Makes It Work

> Sony stacks gaming (PlayStation install base exceeding 150M PS2 units, 8000 PS1 titles vs N64's 400), music (CBS Records acquisition generating >$2B annual operating cash flow across 30 years), film (Spider-Man franchise box office exceeding $7.5B cumulative with Sony keeping 95% of No Way Home's $1.7B gross), and imaging (~half the professional camera sensor market at 11% of revenue and 14% of profits). Morita's American relocation and rejection of Bulova white-labeling built direct-to-consumer brand trust Western OEM partners never matched — the TR-63 at $25 across 1.5M units proved premium pricing requires owned brand. PlayStation is the conglomerate's clearest moat: closed hardware plus exclusive content after Nintendo partnership collapse converted Sony from component supplier to platform owner. Video games now represent a ≈$180B industry versus Hollywood's ≈$50B — a market invisible when Sony entered via betrayed Nintendo deal. Weaknesses: Betamax lost to VHS despite superior technology when Morita's ordered $20M US ad campaign was declined; iPod miss; PS3's ≈$5B first-three-year loss; smartphone exit to focus on arms-dealer sensor strategy supplying Apple and competitors. Financial services once contributed 63% of operating profit (2014) — an odd tail showing conglomerate opacity. Versus Apple and Samsung: Sony invents categories (Walkman, CD with Philips) but repeatedly loses platform wars without software ecosystem control. Aibo robots and cinema cameras show Ibuka's engineer-haven R&D culture persists. Markets perennially debate sum-of-parts breakup versus integrated premium; Jobs idolized Sony's product uniformity but Apple avoided film-studio economics entirely. Sony Life insurance in Japan, Philippines, Taiwan, and China contributed 50%+ of operating cash flow when other divisions stumbled — 5% of revenue in 2001 growing to financial services dominance by 2014. Microsoft Xbox Game Pass at 18M subscribers generating $2–3B annually represents the competitive threat Sony faces — a subscription model versus Sony's traditional hardware-plus-software sales. Camera sensor arms-dealer strategy supplies Apple and competitors while PowerShot and pro cinema lines maintain brand in imaging.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Morita's Bulova rejection created global brand.
>
> **Q** Why turn down a huge OEM order?
>
> **A** Bulova wanted millions of TR-63 units with Bulova badge — Morita chose zero revenue over white-label. TR-63 sold 1.5M units at $25. Morita family: 15th-generation sake heirs; license plate AKM15. Direct Sony brand enabled premium US pricing competitors lacked. Morita later lived abroad to learn Western markets firsthand — rare for Japanese executives of his generation.

> **2.** Post-war optimism was irrational but correct.
>
> **Q** Why start a company in 1945 Tokyo?
>
> **A** Ibuka's prospectus: stable workplace for engineers' joy. Per-capita income ≈$17 (1946); 47% Tokyo homeless; 2.5–3M Japanese war dead vs <500K Americans. Morita family invested ¥190,000. Rice cookers failed; transistor bet (1952 Bell Labs) followed. The Morita family eventually owned ≈17% then ≈10% — by 1999 that 10% alone was worth ≈$5B, exceeding the sake empire.

> **3.** PlayStation born from Nintendo humiliation.
>
> **Q** How did Sony enter games?
>
> **A** Nintendo-Sony CD deal collapsed over licensing — Sony kept developing. PS1: >100M units, 8000 games, >1B game sales vs N64's 400 titles. PS2: >150M units, best-selling console ever. Video games now ≈$180B industry vs Hollywood ≈$50B. Microsoft entered gaming with Xbox as DVD-player Trojan horse; Sony lost ≈$5B on PS3 before recovering with PS4.

> **4.** Conglomerate is feature and bug.
>
> **Q** Does Sony coherence exist?
>
> **A** Gaming ≈30% of ¥9.9T revenue, ≈50% profits; imaging 11%/14%. CBS Records >$2B annual cash flow. 2014: financial services 63% of operating profit. Spider-Man MCU deal: Sony keeps 95% of $1.7B No Way Home gross. Jobs idolized Sony but Apple avoided film-studio economics. Sony Pictures ≈$10.2B annual revenue makes each Spider-Man installment material to conglomerate profits.

> **5.** Japan's recovery story personified.
>
> **Q** Why 'engine of Japanese economy'?
>
> **A** Prime Minister eulogy for Morita (1999). Morita family 10% stake worth ≈$5B — more than sake empire. Exported quality brand globally while rebuilding domestic industry. Morita's physics background informed atomic-bomb understanding, speeding surrender acceptance. Emperor's August 15, 1945 radio broadcast was first time most Japanese heard imperial voice — context for Morita's surrender acceptance.
