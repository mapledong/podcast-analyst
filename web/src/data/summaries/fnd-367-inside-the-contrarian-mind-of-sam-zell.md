## Background

> Senra rereads Ben Johnson's Money Talks, Bullshit Walks (2009), published after Zell's Tribune Media bankruptcy, alongside Zell's autobiography (Founders #269) and a two-hour lunch Zell requested after hearing episode 269. The episode distills Zell's contrarian operating system: partner Bob rummaging trash cans for paper clips, the eleventh commandment — thou shalt not take oneself seriously — delivered to 19,000 Tribune employees, and decades of buying bankrupt properties while shunning development bubbles.
>
> Senra weaves in Steve Schwarzman's account of Zell arriving at Blackstone's bare 1979 office in leather pants, Chris Powers' five lessons from Zell (no-BS communication, buy don't develop, supply and demand, irreplaceable assets, Class B industrial pivot), and Rick's stories of Zell flying to Mongolia, Egypt, and Iraq purely to learn.

## Key Facts

> **F1** In February 2007 Zell sold Equity Office Properties Trust — the world's largest office REIT — to Blackstone for $39 billion, the largest leveraged buyout in corporate history at the time. Less than a year later Tribune Media, which he had acquired, filed for bankruptcy — his greatest career failure — yet he told Johnson he does not reminisce: 'My head only functions looking forward.' Tribune had lost $50 million of cash flow the prior year while employees debated his profanity instead of collapsing revenue.

> **F2** Zell and partner Bob avoided high-debt development through the ugly mid-1970s recession, then raised over $1 billion across two opportunity funds in 13 months starting in 1989 as developers overbuilt — soon there was a 10-year supply of empty glass-and-steel office towers. He pocketed an $11.5 million breakup fee negotiating for Rockefeller Center and used the media coverage to build Chicago notoriety that led to a Chicago Bulls stake two years before Michael Jordan's draft; Zell told Senra the annual dividend eventually exceeded his initial investment with equity up roughly 400x.

> **F3** Zell spends roughly 1,200 hours per year on his Gulfstream combing the world for deals at age 68 when the book closes — he had 13 years of life left. Real estate entrepreneur Chris Powers credits Zell with inspiring a 2016 pivot into Class B industrial; Zell's autobiography line — 'Frankly, there's no substitute for limited competition' — drove decades of avoiding crowded trades, from distressed real estate in the 1970s to corporate turnarounds in the 1980s when synergies were deliberately absent across portfolio companies.

## Mental Model · *Professional Opportunist With Limited Competition*

> **Components**
>
> Zell describes himself as a professional opportunist, not a real estate investor per se: buy assets in growth industries that are distressed, manage them to market leadership, sell when competition erodes margins. Supply, demand, and cash flow are the only drivers — 'I make money. That is my talent.' He avoids development ('when you buy, it's a function of cost of purchase and costs to lease'), refuses emotional bidding wars (Rockefeller Center), and repeats samisms for decades — unless you're the lead dog the scenery never changes, liquidity equals value, conventional wisdom is only a reference point. Reputation beats squeezing partners: on a $150 million loan he offered 7.75% when 7.25% was quoted because an extra ≈$2 million was irrelevant versus the next deal.
>
> **Application**
>
> Enter early when competition is scarce and exit when copycats arrive — Zell called real estate 'a really shitty business' once his strategy got crowded. Briefings stay to one page; conviction means shutting out noise. Fear sits close to the surface — he won't hire anyone without it. Design for freedom: control what you work on, work on what you love, get good over decades, and let money follow.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Forward-only beats self-flagellation.
>
> **Q** How does Zell process Tribune and other failures?
>
> **A** He told Johnson he made the right decision when he made it and that 'this too shall pass.' Senra pairs that with Dominic Cruz after a UFC loss — 'Loss is a part of life. If you don't have loss, you don't grow' — and Michael Jordan's line that losing must hurt enough to force change. Zell's version: failures are inevitable, learn, do not look back.

> **2.** Curiosity compounds into unexpected deals.
>
> **Q** Why did Zell fly to Mongolia with no deal in mind?
>
> **A** Rick introduced him to Robert Friedland's copper project; Zell wanted to learn about culture, government, and markets with a beginner's mindset despite massive wealth. He treated the world as a classroom — sitting on Blackstone's floor asking Schwarzman to teach him finance, peppering Senra with podcast questions at lunch. Business is a puzzle to solve, not a battle.

> **3.** Attention beats politeness in crisis.
>
> **Q** Why did Zell deliberately offend Tribune staff?
>
> **A** He counted 14 signatures to use the bathroom and 10 to approve a $10,000 purchase in York, Pennsylvania, while revenue plunged unnoticed. He went 'over the line on purpose' because polite requests failed — survival required revenue focus, not language policing. The eleventh commandment: thou shalt not take oneself seriously.

>
> Blackstone's $39 billion EOP acquisition — the largest LBO in history when closed — validated Zell's timing selling peak office assets; Schwarzman's 1979 floor meeting with leather-jacketed Zell foreshadowed decades of repeat business between the firms.

> **2. BXP** · 🟡 WATCH · ●○○ Low
>
> Zell's refusal to join the 1980s development frenzy and his 2007 exit from the largest office portfolio frame office REITs as cyclical supply-demand trades — buy distress when ten-year gluts clear, avoid when 'build it today because it'll cost more tomorrow' dominates developer psychology.
