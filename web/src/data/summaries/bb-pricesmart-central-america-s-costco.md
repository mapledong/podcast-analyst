## Background

> Matt Reustle breaks down PriceSmart with Marcus Hansen, returning from his Casey's General Stores episode. PriceSmart is the third-generation Price family business — grandson-led, San Diego–headquartered, and operating entirely in Central America, the Caribbean, and South America. Sol Price invented the modern membership club at FedMart in the 1950s ($2 annual subscription for federal employees), then Price Club (2,000–3,000 SKUs versus ≈25,000 at Walmart), which merged with Costco in the 1990s.
>
> When Costco deprioritized Central America, the Price family took two to three stores private as Price Enterprises (1993), relaunching PriceSmart in 1996 with the first club in Panama. Sam Walton credited Sol Price; Arthur Blank consulted him before Home Depot; Amazon Prime traces to the same membership logic. PriceSmart still buys Kirkland private label from Costco (≈33% of Costco sales) while developing its own Members Selection label at 25–30% discounts.

## Key Facts

> **F1** PriceSmart runs ≈61 warehouse clubs across ≈12 markets with ≈$5.5 billion revenue forecast, ≈$350 million EBITDA, and ≈$250 million EBIT at a ≈$5 billion market cap — roughly one-fifth the size of a typical U.S. Costco box. Membership tiers are ≈$45 (standard) and ≈$90 (Platinum with ≈2–3% cashback); Platinum members rose from ≈12% to ≈20% of the base in five years. About 40% of operating earnings come from upfront membership fees, giving strong annual visibility.

> **F2** Roughly 50% of revenue is in dollar-linked Caribbean and Central American economies; the rest faces local FX volatility that PriceSmart largely does not hedge — yet customers keep returning even when Colombia's peso swings wildly across 11 stores. Food is ≈45% of sales (fresh chicken a standout), general merchandise ≈55%; about half of products are locally sourced including ≈25% fresh, with U.S. imports for Members Selection staples and Asian goods for apparel and toys.

> **F3** New clubs pay back in two to three years; adding a distribution center after four to five stores in a market lifts operating leverage. Colombia (≈11 stores today) could reach ≈25 against GDP and middle-class penetration; Chile is the next expansion market after ≈5 years of regulatory groundwork. The company adds ≈3–4 stores per year, targets mid-single-digit same-club growth, and converts cash at ≈90%+ with store-level returns ramping over a couple of years when DCs come online.

## Mental Model · *Controlled Club-Store Replication Abroad*

> **Components**
>
> PriceSmart copies Costco's limited-SKU, bulk-purchasing flywheel for Latin America's top 10–15% income cohort — expats, returning U.S. educated professionals, and tourists accustomed to American retail. Sol Price DNA emphasizes employee investment, community philanthropy, sustainable supplier relationships (not margin-maximizing squeeze), and logistics ownership: Miami export hub, regional DCs after scale, hurricane-hardened construction in the Caribbean, and long-term land ownership enabling pick-and-collect omnichannel without landlord friction.
>
> **Application**
>
> Emerging-market retailers win by importing trust — safe parking, air conditioning, vision and dental perks that can pay back the membership alone — not by racing Sam's Club or Costco on home turf. FX gates in Trinidad and political risk in Venezuela explain slow expansion; Chile's rules-based economy fits the playbook. Investors underwrite membership-fee floor plus 3–4 annual club adds, not U.S.-style hypergrowth.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Membership fees fund resilience through macro shocks.
>
> **Q** How does PriceSmart handle FX volatility?
>
> **A** About 40% of EBIT is prepaid membership income; ≈50% of revenue is dollar-based. In Colombia and other volatile markets, PriceSmart often holds pricing through currency swings because affluent members value U.S.-quality assortment — local supermarkets and SMEs cannot match the club format, so revenue impact stays muted despite wild peso moves.

> **2.** Logistics control is the moat, not store count.
>
> **Q** Why expand slowly?
>
> **A** Consultants urge faster rollout, but PriceSmart opens ≈3–4 clubs per year, owns real estate where possible, and builds a DC after four to five stores — Sam Walton's lesson that logistics wins wars. Jamaica clubs survived hurricanes while competitors failed; owned boxes allow in-store pick-and-collect without lease renegotiation.

> **3.** Colombia and Chile frame the next decade.
>
> **Q** Where is growth headed?
>
> **A** Colombia's ≈11 clubs could roughly double to ≈25 on middle-class density alone; Chile — second-largest regional GDP, strong institutions — is entering after years of site work and regulator relationship-building. B2B demand from small hotels and restaurants buying American-standard product adds a Platinum-tier tailwind.
