## Key Facts

> **F1** Atlassian IPO'd December 10, 2015 at $21 per share — approximately $4.4 billion market capitalization (TEAM on NASDAQ); first trading day closed $27.48, nearly $6 billion market cap. At Episode 47 recording (Nov 2017) market cap approached $11 billion after 25% post-earnings jump and ≈50% year-over-year revenue growth on latest earnings call — first in-person Acquired episode of 2017.

> **F2** Founders Mike Cannon-Brookes and Scott Farquhar never sold primary shares to investors — Excel bought $60 million secondary at ≈$400 million valuation (2010, 15.21% ownership); T. Rowe Price bought ≈6% at implied >$3 billion valuation (2014). Zero dollars reached company balance sheet from VC — only founders and employees sold on secondary market. First Acquired bootstrapped company to lasting independent public scale without primary venture capital funding.

> **F3** Revenue scaled from $1 million (2002, Jira launch year, founders paid themselves $15K each) to $15 million (FY2006) to $150 million (FY2013), $215 million (FY2014), and $320 million (FY2015 pre-IPO). Fiscal year ends June 30 matching Australian convention used by most local public companies. FY2015 generated almost $100 million operating cash flow while profitable — rare among 2015 unicorns burning negative $100M annually on operations.

> **F4** Sales and marketing consumed only ≈20% of revenue at IPO versus 50–100% typical for SaaS peers trying to grow slower. Jira plus Confluence drove most revenue from engineering and product teams inside large enterprises like Cisco. Ben as Cisco intern (2008) chose paid Confluence over free MediaWiki — purchase below procurement thresholds, direct manager approved without CIO involvement.

> **F5** IPO raised $462 million selling ≈10% of company; January 2017 acquired Trello for $425 million from IPO proceeds plus ongoing cash generation. Founders retained ≈39% each at IPO. Excel's $60M secondary at $400M valuation marked up to $4.4B+ IPO and ≈$11B at recording — incredible secondary-only return for one of Silicon Valley's top venture firms.

## Mental Model · *Zero-Touch Enterprise PLG*

> **Components**
>
> Developer tools spread bottom-up — credit-card purchase on website, viral team expansion inside accounts, land-before-expand without steak-dinner CIO sales. Bootstrapping forced product quality: founders in Sydney could not afford salespeople to sell Ford/Boeing/Tesla — product had to sell itself over the internet. Secondary liquidity (Excel $60M, T. Rowe ≈6%) gave employee/founder liquidity and VC credibility without primary dilution. BYOD era aligned enterprise buyer with user — bad product could no longer survive via CIO relationships alone. Atlassian competes with open source (MediaWiki vs Confluence at Cisco 2008) by being enterprise-grade enough to justify payment.
>
> **Application**
>
> B2B founders: prove product-led motion before hiring enterprise sales — Atlassian reached $320M revenue on 20% S&M. Secondary programs retain talent when staying private longer. Public currency ($462M IPO proceeds) enables M&A (Trello $425M) while ongoing cash generation preserves optionality. Saturation risk: Jira/Confluence may peak in engineering orgs; suite expansion (Service Desk, Stride) requires more sales effort and faces Slack competition.

---

## What Makes It Work

> Atlassian moat: Jira workflow embedding in dev teams — switching costs high once sprints and docs integrated; Confluence bundle increases stickiness. PLG distribution from Sydney: global download model, low CAC vs Oracle/Salesforce enterprise motion — S&M 20% vs industry 50–100%. Profitable growth rare in 2015 unicorn era — $100M operating cash flow on $320M revenue. Weaknesses: no sales force limits early upmarket; HipChat lost to Slack despite rebrand to Stride (task assignment, Jira heritage insufficient vs nine-Slack switching cost); Bitbucket vs GitHub; post-Jira products (Service Desk) face Zendesk/Help Scout with more competition than Jira. Versus Salesforce: opposite GTM — product pulls vs sales pushes. Bootstrapped origin = founder control (39% each) plus capital-efficiency legend. Hosts warn margins may compress as non-Jira products require more selling.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Bootstrap to IPO is rare not impossible.
>
> **Q** No VC primary?
>
> **A** Credit card and website sales funded Jira from 2002 — $1M year-one revenue on $15K founder salaries each. Excel $60M secondary (2010) and T. Rowe ≈6% (2014) bought from founders/employees only — company never issued primary shares. First Acquired bootstrapped company to lasting public independence. Contrasts 'steroid era' startups raising every 18–24 months; Atlassian ≈$11B at recording with zero primary VC.

> **2.** Developers buy without sales calls.
>
> **Q** No sales team?
>
> **A** Jira sold online to engineers — bottom-up adoption inside Cisco and similar enterprises below procurement thresholds. Steve Jobs (all-things-D ≈2009): consumer quality wins because buyer equals user; enterprise sold via CIO steak dinners where users stuck with bad software. Atlassian latched onto BYOD shift — 20% S&M vs 50–100% SaaS benchmark. Engineers today: 'if I have to call a sales rep, I'm not buying.'

> **3.** IPO was liquidity not rescue.
>
> **Q** Why go public 2015?
>
> **A** Profitable with $100M operating cash flow on $320M revenue — not cash-burn desperation. Public currency for acquisitions: Trello $425M (Jan 2017) paid from $462M IPO raise plus ongoing cash. Founders became paper billionaires at $4.4B IPO; day-one close ≈$6B. S-1 included voluntary full cohort analysis — transparent vs Blue Apron-era opacity; narrative: Australian underdog beating Silicon Valley VC-backed peers.

> **4.** Australian founders global day-one.
>
> **Q** Location disadvantage?
>
> **A** Download distribution bypassed Sydney geography — US market accessible without SF HQ initially. UNSW classmates (≈40-person program) built Jira 2002, Confluence 2004; fiscal June year-end matches Australian convention. In-person Episode 47 recorded in Seattle — David moved to SF earlier 2017. Mike Cannon-Brookes later climate activism separate from ops; earnings calls in Australian accents noted fondly by hosts.

> **5.** Post-IPO M&A fills product gaps.
>
> **Q** Growth after IPO?
>
> **A** Bitbucket (2010, GitHub competitor), HipChat (2012, Slack predecessor — rebranded Stride 2017 with tasks/project management), Service Desk (2013, Zendesk competitor). Trello $425M extends beyond dev tools. HipChat lost to Slack — hosts won't run nine Strides alongside nine Slacks. 50% YoY growth at recording but Jira/Confluence saturation in engineering orgs may force harder sells on suite products with lower margins.
