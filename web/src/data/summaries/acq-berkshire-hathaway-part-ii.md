## Background

> David opens with Charlie Munger's biography — Michigan math, Harvard Law without an undergraduate degree, the 1959 Omaha dinner with Warren — then reunites the pair through Blue Chip Stamps float, the 1972 See's acquisition at ≈8× earnings on $25 million, and Charlie's painful 1973–74 partnership drawdowns (-31.9% and -31.5% two years running). Parallel threads cover GEICO's near-death and Jack Byrne's state-by-state repricing war (shrinking to seven states plus DC), Buffett's media empire (Washington Post, Cap Cities), and the Salomon Brothers treasury-auction scandal netting ≈$4 million illicit profit that made Warren interim chairman. Ben and David apply Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers to Berkshire's dual engine — wholly owned operating companies plus public equities — and grade the 1970–1992 period an A against the S&P. Tom Murphy and Cap Cities/ABC illustrate owner-operator media culture; part two ends at Berkshire's 1992 $11,750 A-share apex.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Blue Chip Stamps bought See's Candies for $25 million in 1972 (≈8× trailing pre-tax profit on ≈$4 million earnings growing ≈12% annually); hard assets were only ≈$5 million against a competing ≈$30 million offer. The candy business later delivered over $2 billion in cumulative free cash flow to Berkshire with minimal reinvestment required.

> **F2** Warren spent ≈$47 million from 1976–1980 to accumulate ≈33% of GEICO at prices near $2/share; by the mid-1990s buybacks lifted Berkshire to ≈50% ownership without additional capital. Kay Graham introduced Warren to Jack Byrne, who shrank GEICO to seven states plus DC to reprice underwriting after regulators threatened liquidation.

> **F3** From January 1970 ($45/share) through year-end 1992 ($11,750/share), Berkshire A shares returned ≈27.4% annualized — compounding a $45 investment into five figures while Warren and Charlie built the modern conglomerate through Blue Chip float and insurance acquisitions.

> **F4** Berkshire invested $700 million in Salomon convertible preferred paying a 15% coupon in September 1987 during the Perelman raid scare; after 1991 treasury-auction fraud (≈$4 million illicit profit), Berkshire's stake recovered to ≈$1.7 billion when Citigroup bought Salomon in 1998 — poor risk-adjusted return versus reputational bet.

> **F5** Charlie Munger's partnership averaged ≈28.3% IRR its first decade but fell 31.9% and 31.5% in 1973–74 — scarring him into merging with Berkshire. Cap Cities/ABC deal required Berkshire's $517 million equity check in 1986; Tom Murphy's owner-operator media culture illustrated the 'great businesses at fair prices' shift from Graham cigar butts.

## Mental Model · *Quality Purchased with Float*

> **Components**
>
> Munger's insight — 'I just like great businesses' — reframes Berkshire: See's proved brands and pricing power not visible on balance sheets could justify multiples Graham would reject. Blue Chip and GEICO supply float (stamp prepayments, unearned premiums from ≈$2/share accumulation) that funds acquisitions without dilution or covenant debt. Warren still applies probabilistic margin-of-safety thinking (AmEx, GEICO distress) but stops liquidating gems. Salomon adds reputational capital as balance-sheet asset: only Warren's phone call to the Fed reversed a death sentence after ≈$4 million treasury-auction fraud. Counter-positioning versus LBO-era leverage: Berkshire buys with equity and float, refuses debt that raises bankruptcy risk, and holds forever for tax deferral — compounding from $45 to $11,750 A-shares across 1970–1992.
>
> **Application**
>
> Look for businesses where customer trust allows price increases without proportional capital (See's boxed chocolates, later Coca-Cola). Pair them with low-cost sticky funding (insurance float from GEICO's ≈$2/share accumulation, Blue Chip stamp prepayments). In crises, credible operator plus backer beats spreadsheet cheapness — but verify governance before joining boards; Salomon showed even Buffett can prop up rotten cultures at franchise risk. Part two ends at 1992's $11,750 A-share apex before technology and scale complicate part three.

## What Makes It Work

> By 1992 Berkshire stacks multiple Helmer powers across insurance float and operating companies. National Indemnity, GEICO (≈33% owned by 1980, ≈50% by mid-1990s buybacks at ≈$2/share), and later Gen Re create billions in negative-cost funding when underwriting breaks even. Scale lets Berkshire write large policies competitors avoid and weather catastrophes. Warren Buffett's name makes Berkshire capital worth more than equal dollars from LBO shops — visible in GEICO's Salomon-backed $76 million convert, Cap Cities' $517 million equity check for ABC, and the $700 million Salomon rescue. 'Lose money for the firm and I will be understanding; lose a shred of reputation and I will be ruthless' codifies reputational moat. Counter-positioning: permanent capital, no 2-and-20 fees, refusal to lever acquisitions — while KKR raided with junk bonds, Berkshire supplied equity to family-owned businesses. Weaknesses: Blue Chip cross-holdings drew SEC scrutiny; emotional Salomon investment; by early 1990s deals must move billions to matter, shrinking investable universe per Buffett himself.

## Key Insights

> **1.** See's was the hinge from Graham to Munger.
>
> **Q** Why pay $25 million for a candy company at 6× book value?
>
> **A** Hard assets were ≈$5 million; competing offer already sat at $30 million. Charlie argued $4 million pre-tax profit growing ≈12% with minimal reinvestment made ≈8× earnings rational — pricing power on boxed chocolates mattered more than liquidation value. Warren hems, offers $25 million, receives $2 billion+ subsequent cash flow — proof brand moats belong in intrinsic value, shifting Berkshire from Graham cigar butts permanently.

> **2.** GEICO's rescue combines underwriting discipline with Buffett's credibility.
>
> **Q** How did GEICO survive its 1970s crisis?
>
> **A** After mispricing left regulators threatening liquidation, Jack Byrne brokered industry reinsurance support, vacated states blocking rate increases (shrinking to seven states plus DC), and fired management onstage. Warren — introduced via Kay Graham — bought $4 million at $2/share, backstopped Salomon's $76 million convert, rode float as GEICO repurchased shares. Missing GEICO for two decades cost float compounding; ≈$47 million for ≈33% by 1980 partially compensated.

> **3.** Salomon tested whether Berkshire's reputation could substitute for governance.
>
> **Q** Why did Warren become interim Salomon chairman?
>
> **A** Paul Mozer's treasury-auction violations netting ≈$4 million illicit profit threatened Salomon's license and Berkshire's $700 million preferred at 15% coupon. Walking away would torch Warren's reputation; staying meant wrestling traders he detested. He secured Fed reprieve, forced cooperation via Munger's legal structure, told Congress reputation beats quarterly P&L — but Charlie later wished they had let the stake go to zero despite ≈$1.7 billion 1998 recovery.

> **4.** Debt-fueled 1980s LBO culture was value-destructive — and Berkshire counter-positioned.
>
> **Q** Why fight the efficient-market hypothesis crowd?
>
> **A** Academic finance equated volatility with risk, encouraging leverage to amplify beta. Buffett and Munger argued at Columbia's 1985 Graham-Dodd 50th anniversary that risk is permanent capital loss — bankruptcy from coupons, not stock swings. While KKR raided with junk bonds in the 1980s, Berkshire supplied $517 million equity to Cap Cities/ABC and float-backed insurers, earning durable cash flows without forcing breakups — counter-positioning that compounded from 1970's $45 A-share to 1992's $11,750.

> **5.** Letting winners run remains the lesson Warren keeps re-learning.
>
> **Q** What mistake rhymes with selling Disney after Mary Poppins?
>
> **A** Warren bought Disney around an $80 million market cap after Mary Poppins earned ≈$30 million at box office, then sold within a year or two when price met hurdle. David ties it to Sequoia's Apple sale — approximately right on price, massively wrong on compounding. Part two's winners (See's $25M → $2B+ cash, GEICO at $2/share, Washington Post) work because Munger finally convinces Warren that quality times time beats one-time arbitrage across ≈27.4% annualized returns 1970–1992.
