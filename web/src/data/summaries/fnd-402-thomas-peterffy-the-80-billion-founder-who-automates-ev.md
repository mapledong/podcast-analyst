## Background

> Senra profiles Colossus writer Dom Cook's Thomas Peterffy piece — an 81-year-old worth ≈$80 billion who built Interactive Brokers from $120 in capital. Budapest wartime scrappage (dragging bathtubs for steel at 70% bomb damage) taught trade. In 1965 he landed in New York, automated $65/week drafting with the Programma 101, then followed Hungarian Janos Aranyi into finance.
>
> Dr. Henry Jarecki at Mocatta Metals taught commodities automation; Peterffy left in 1977 with $200,000, bought an NYSE seat for $36,000, and wore computer-printed quotes in pockets. Knee injuries led to oscilloscope hacking, 1983 handheld traders, Nasdaq keyboard spiders, and 1987 fully automated trading. IPO May 2007 retained 80%; Dutch auction saved ≈$80 million versus bank fees.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Exchanges are puzzles, not mysticism.
>
> **Q** How did Peterffy beat floor traders?
>
> **A** He decoded Quotron pulses with an oscilloscope, hired tall women traders floor specialists noticed, built 1983 handheld quote devices, and wired mechanical spiders to type on banned keyboards — automating while humans vacationed per 1971 Barron's profile.

> **2.** Partnerships expire when ambition caps.
>
> **Q** Why leave Mocatta Metals?
>
> **A** Jarecki refused options expansion; Peterffy watched seven years, then exited 1977 with $200,000. Successful people remove growth impediments — he bought an NYSE seat for $36,000 and folded quote printouts into pockets.

> **3.** Low price is the strategy.
>
> **Q** What links IBKR to Costco?
>
> **A** Peterffy charges less than rivals who stopped competing on price; Dutch auction IPO avoided ≈$80 million banker fees. At 81 he checks live quotes during interviews — automation ethos never retired.
