## Background

> Jens Grede runs Popular Culture and co-founded SKIMS with Kim Kardashian. He frames modern consumer building through media fragmentation, the decline of easy social-media community building, the return of physical retail, and the need for big platforms that can afford stores and inventory. The conversation also covers Abercrombie, Victoria's Secret, Nike, Beats, and the shift from futurism to nostalgia. Grede grew up in Sweden shaped by 1990s U.S. pop culture — Beverly Hills 90210, Baywatch — and later named his firm Popular Culture because culture is the remaining hack to reach a critical mass of U.S. consumers. He argues that even a billion dollars spent on TV, billboards, and Meta could not match Dwayne Johnson's brand recognition for Teremana tequila; personality and culture are how consumers learn what a company stands for when feeds are algorithmically fragmented. SKIMS launched in 2019 and is approaching six years old — "kindergarten age" — yet Grede says he doubts he could replicate the breakthrough today. Disruption in retail is more often tied to supply-chain shifts and consumer taste than a brand single-handedly moving the world; luck is opportunity meeting preparation.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** Grede says U.S. retail has returned to 2019 post-COVID shopping levels and remains roughly 80% physical stores versus 20% e-commerce — which is why SKIMS wants to be in the 80% business despite the capital cost of opening stores in good locations.

> **F2** Early SKIMS demand overwhelmed supply: about one or two months after launch, Grede says there were roughly 2 million customers on the waitlist with no product left and none coming quickly — a moment when interest felt asymmetric, like 1+1+1 equaled 100.

> **F3** He told anyone who would listen five years earlier to buy Abercrombie when it was worth about $700–$800 million; he contrasts consumer purchasing behavior with Wall Street narratives, noting Victoria's Secret and Abercrombie scandals did not stop younger consumers from buying when product and timing aligned.

## Mental Model · *Culture Plus Distribution*

> **Components**
>
> Use a cultural platform to create demand, then meet it with product franchises, supply-chain competence, retail access, and customer feedback loops. Pop culture and sport are the two forces that still cross political, racial, religious, and algorithmic boundaries. Social media shifted from follower communities to interest-based algorithms — devastating for young brands that could freely build communities five to nine years earlier. Big retailers win because stores consume capital ordinary founders cannot finance.
>
> **Application**
>
> SKIMS started with Kim's influence but Grede compares her to Michael Jordan for the influencer generation — a kid wearing Jordans and Beats without ever watching Jordan play or listening to Dr. Dre. The task is transferring source energy to products, athletes, creators, and stores without becoming only celebrity merch. Grede is laser-focused on product strength — "product is omnipotent" — and building a platform, not a single-person brand. For investors, check whether cultural awareness is backed by supply chain, waitlist conversion, and physical rollout before sizing consumer bets.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Pop culture is one of the few remaining mass-reach channels.
>
> **Q** Why does Grede care so much about culture?
>
> **A** Fragmented media means two feeds can show different worlds — fly-fishing enthusiasts can live entirely inside one algorithmic bubble. Culture and sport still cross society's boundaries, which is why a personality like Kim can move consumer demand faster than paid media. Grede's vodka thought experiment: a billion dollars on TV, billboards, and Meta would not deliver half the recognition Dwayne Johnson has for Teremana. Building awareness plus knowing what a brand stands for now requires personality and culture, not institutional advertising alone.

> **2.** Big brands win because retail and inventory require capital.
>
> **Q** Why is SKIMS opening stores?
>
> **A** If 80% of U.S. retail is physical, staying digital-only caps the opportunity. Grede says he does not want SKIMS to be only the 20% e-commerce business — he wants access to the 80%. But opening stores is incredibly expensive and consumes capital; if he and Patrick tried to start a retailer today they would struggle to finance a good location. That is why large retailers and restaurant chains with big footprints are hard to recreate — and why SKIMS' cultural scale matters for funding the physical expansion.

> **3.** Supply-chain shifts create brand moments as much as taste shifts do.
>
> **Q** What made SKIMS possible?
>
> **A** Grede traces American mall retail to Les Wexner building Victoria's Secret, Abercrombie, and related concepts as malls exploded in the 1980s — timing beyond any founder's control. SKIMS caught whitespace in underwear and lounge, better supply chains, social reach at launch, and Kim's aesthetic clarity. About a month in, 2 million waitlisted customers with empty shelves proved timing and product hit a nerve. Six years on he says SKIMS is culturally bigger than its revenue — still a small company relative to a very big brand — and he would not bet on repeating the same timeline if starting today.
