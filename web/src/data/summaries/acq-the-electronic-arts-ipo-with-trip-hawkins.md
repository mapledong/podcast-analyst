## Background

> Season 4 Episode 7 recorded in Santa Barbara with founder Trip Hawkins — throwback to when IPO meant profitable industrial growth, not cash-burning unicorns. Trip: Southern California sports kid, D&D, Harvard football + late-night coding, early Apple (Lisa team, VisiCalc era), Don Valentine lunch ('bring fire back'). EA incorporated as Amazin' Software, renamed Electronic Arts — 'we're about the arts but not the artists.' EA Sports: John Madden insisted on 11 players per side; Trip flew Madden to EA HQ. Sega alliance after Nintendo licensing knife-fight. IPO ≈1989 after seven profitable years; Trip later founded 3DO (cautionary). Contrasts with 2019 A+ IPO cohort on capital intensity and profitability.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** Trip Hawkins raised $2 million from Sequoia's Don Valentine with $200,000 personal co-investment — the only private capital Electronic Arts took before its ≈1989 IPO, per Trip's Santa Barbara interview. Don Valentine told Trip: 'I'm going to bring the fire and you've got to bring the fire right back.'

> **F2** Dr. J vs Larry Bird (1983) is cited as the first celebrity/athlete-endorsed video game — sports simulation thread predates the EA Sports label and set the template for licensed franchises.

> **F3** John Madden Football shipped in 1988 after years negotiating 11-on-11 authenticity when competitors shipped 7-on-7 '80/20 football'; Trip flew Madden to EA HQ. Ben cites Madden franchise surpassing ≈$4 billion lifetime revenue by 2013.

> **F4** Steve Wozniak joined EA's board in 1983 alongside Trip and Don Valentine — independent credibility reinforcing developer-friendly publisher positioning versus Nintendo's vendor model.

> **F5** EA was profitable from company inception; IPO ≈1989 (≈7 years post-1982 founding) provided public stock Trip cites as acquisition currency for roll-ups that built modern EA — contrasting 2019 Uber raising ≈$8B while losing money.

## Mental Model · *Treat Developers Like Recording Artists*

> **Components**
>
> Trip imported record-industry packaging and royalties to games when publishers treated devs as vendors. EA's name reflects 'we're about the arts but we're not the artists' — publishers enable creators. Platform power shifts: Nintendo's NES lockout (no published SDK, ≈$10/unit licensing) forced EA to reverse-engineer Sega Genesis; Madden plus Joe Montana titles drove 16-bit share. Artist autonomy plus distribution scale equals EA flywheel; Woz's 1983 board seat signaled independence from platform gatekeepers.
>
> **Application**
>
> In creative tech, publisher value is credibly committing to talent brand and retail/distribution — until digital storefronts disintermediate. Modern analog: platforms extracting 30% versus Trip's developer advocacy — Fortnite/App Store fights echo EA-Nintendo negotiations. Trip's later 3DO hardware bet shows risk when publishers try to own platforms directly.

---

## What Makes It Work

> EA's early moat: Trip's Apple/Valentine network, sports licenses (Madden exclusivity became decades-long annuity), and technical guts (Genesis reverse-engineering, 11-player Madden when tech 'couldn't'). EA Sports broadcast camera angle (Madden) defined genre visual language copied in real NFL games. Annual release cadence exploits sports seasons for recurring revenue. Weaknesses: Console platform owners extract rent; licensed sports IP costly today; Trip's later 3DO burn shows risk of hardware bets. Nintendo relationship adversarial — power ultimately with platform gatekeepers.

## Key Insights

> **1.** EA invented developer dignity as strategy.
>
> **Q** Why 'Electronic Arts'?
>
> **A** Trip modeled Hollywood and records — developers as artists on album covers, not anonymous vendors. Don Valentine started EA in Sequoia's office to 'control' a young founder but Trip stood up to Valentine's fire with his own. Woz's 1983 board seat reinforced indie credibility versus Nintendo's lockout model where publishers were mere vendors paying ≈$10/unit.

> **2.** Madden was a multi-year authenticity bet.
>
> **Q** Why did Madden take so long?
>
> **A** John Madden demanded 11 vs 11 when competitors shipped 7-on-7 simplified football. Trip flew Madden to EA HQ; the multi-year authenticity bet created a franchise Ben cites at ≈$4 billion cumulative revenue by 2013. EA Sports broadcast camera angle in Madden defined genre visual language later copied in real NFL broadcasts — annual cadence exploits sports seasons for recurring SKUs.

> **3.** Genesis alliance was calculated platform politics.
>
> **Q** Why reverse-engineer Sega?
>
> **A** Nintendo refused favorable licensing terms (≈$10/unit, limited releases). EA reverse-engineered a Genesis dev kit and bluffed Sega into a favorable deal — Madden became Genesis killer app alongside Joe Montana titles. Platform choke points determine publisher economics; Trip's Apple employee #68 background taught him platform politics before founding EA in 1982.

> **4.** 1989 IPO ≠ 2019 IPO.
>
> **Q** Why go public profitable in seven years?
>
> **A** Trip: shareholders wanted liquidity after ≈7 profitable years; public stock became acquisition currency for roll-ups building modern EA. Contrast Uber 2019 — raise ≈$8B while losing money. EA needed no growth capital from IPO, only M&A flexibility. Only $2M Sequoia plus Trip's $200K preceded IPO — capital efficiency foreign to 2019 A+ IPO cohort.

> **5.** Sports games are perfect recurring SKUs.
>
> **Q** Why annual Madden?
>
> **A** Real rosters, rules, and broadcast presentation refresh yearly — natural sequel engine before live-service. Dr. J vs Larry Bird (1983) proved athlete-licensed games; Trip's jock-plus-gamer identity merged in EA Sports. Category looked obvious retrospectively but was uncertain before first sports titles — Madden's 11-on-11 bet took until 1988 to ship.
