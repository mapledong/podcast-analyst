## Background

> Matt Reustle and Zack Fuss break down Salesforce with Matt Garrett, General Partner at CRV and former head of Salesforce Ventures, who led investments in Snowflake, Twilio, and Zoom. Garrett opens with CRM as a customer database plus workflow spanning sales automation, service tickets, marketing outreach, and commerce — essentially every enterprise touchpoint in the customer journey.
>
> He quantifies a ≈$100 billion CRM category where Salesforce holds ≈20% share, walks Benioff's 1999 founding after Oracle and the "no software" campaign against on-prem incumbents like Siebel, and explains how force.com, AppExchange, and early SOAP APIs created platform stickiness. The conversation covers the shift from builder to buyer via ExactTarget, MuleSoft, Tableau, and Slack; Dreamforce as culture engine; Hawaiian ohana values; V2MOM planning; Ventures scaling from ≈$10–20 million to ≈$1 billion annually; and where SMB challengers plus Snowflake-style data gravity could nibble at the edges.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** At recording, Salesforce guided ≈$26 billion revenue for the fiscal year on Q2 revenue of ≈$6.3 billion (≈23% year-over-year growth), with Sales Cloud and Service Cloud each roughly $5 billion businesses — Service Cloud larger and growing faster. The company served 150,000+ customers including 90% of the Fortune 500, jumped 53 spots to #137 on the Fortune 500, traded near a $270 billion market cap, and forecast ≈$50 billion revenue by fiscal 2026 at still-impressive enterprise scale.

> **F2** Garrett describes the SaaS economics: ≈90% gross retention expanding to 100%+ net dollar retention as customers add seats and products; ≈73% gross margins with ≈18% operating margins and ≈16% cash flow margins because bookings are recognized ratably while cash is collected upfront. Sales and marketing exceeds 30% of expenses — the largest line item — funding perpetual bookings where each dollar of S&M ideally yields a dollar of recurring revenue that expands over time.

> **F3** Platform and developer network metrics anchor the moat: force.com and Apex let enterprises build custom workflows; AppExchange integrated third parties like DocuSign without bespoke API work; Garrett cites ≈$6 of system-integrator and ISV revenue generated for every $1 of Salesforce revenue (circa his tenure). Salesforce Ventures grew from ≈$10–20 million deployed annually when Garrett joined to ≈$1 billion per year with ≈$4 billion on the balance sheet across 300–400 investments that informed but rarely fed M&A.

## Mental Model · *Land-Expand-Platform Perpetuity*

> **Components**
>
> Salesforce landed with cheap sales-force automation against Siebel's million-dollar on-prem contracts — tens of thousands of dollars per year for SMBs Siebel ignored. Once inside, expansion runs on two rails: more seats priced per user and attach of Service, Marketing, and Commerce clouds on 73%+ gross margin software. The platform layer — force.com, Apex, AppExchange, and API-first integration — converts single-app wins into enterprise-wide workflow lock-in. Garrett's shorthand: customers came for the application and stayed for the platform.
>
> **Application**
>
> Garrett says the near-term five-year path needs little heroics — installed SaaS bookings and a large sales force carry growth — but long-term risk mirrors Salesforce's own Siebel method: asymmetric attackers that bypass the admin buyer (developer-led Twilio-style stacks) or decouple data gravity via Snowflake and Databricks warehouses. Operators should copy the disciplined front-office focus Benioff maintained — resisting Workday-style detours — while investors weigh whether platform/developer network multiples already price the compounding engine.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Cheap beachhead beat feature lists.
>
> **Q** Why did Salesforce win against Siebel?
>
> **A** Garrett says early reps targeted Siebel customers and led with price — tens of thousands per year versus $1–2 million on-prem deals — not continuous updates or hosted infrastructure. That single clear message let an unknown cloud vendor displace the CRM category leader during the post-dot-com recession.

> **2.** Platform turns apps into OS.
>
> **Q** What makes Salesforce hard to rip out?
>
> **A** force.com covers ≈80% of needs out of the box; Apex and AppExchange fill the rest with one-click integrations. Garrett notes nCino built commercial lending on the platform and Veeva built pharma workflows — custom data models on shared trust/security — while early SOAP and REST APIs prioritized connectivity over UI polish.

> **3.** Culture scales through ritual.
>
> **Q** How does Dreamforce reinforce the business?
>
> **A** Garrett describes company-wide previews at Moscone, live demos to 100,000 attendees (streamed to ≈1 million), and an annual "hack the V2MOM" offsite where hundreds of executives publicly debate what to stop, start, or do more of — including unscripted customer microphones at the first 250-person Dreamforce where Benioff demanded honest feedback, not scripts.

## Investment Ideas

> **1. CRM** · 🟢 LONG · ●●○ Medium
>
> Garrett argues Salesforce can hit its numbers for five years on SaaS retention, multi-cloud attach, and a $270 billion-scale platform with ≈$6 of developer network revenue per $1 of CRM sales — but SMB ease-of-use gaps, cloud-warehouse data decoupling (Snowflake), and developer-bottom-up adoption (Twilio, open source) are credible medium-term share threats; valuation already embeds pioneer status.

---

## Golden Quotes

> "Parker Harris refused lucrative on-prem deals from large financial customers, telling Benioff that accepting would mean Salesforce is no longer a cloud company — Garrett cites this as the archetypal short-term pain for long-term model integrity."

> "Benioff coined integrated philanthropy at launch: 1% of product, 1% of equity, and 1% of employee time to charity — Garrett calls it a "company with a soul" that creates purpose beyond enterprise software and is hard for rivals to replicate."

> "Garrett warns the disruptive pattern Salesforce used on Siebel — cheaper delivery plus an incumbent business model that cannot self-disrupt — may reappear via engineers adopting Twilio or open-source stacks bottom-up until renewal season when the line of business asks why they still pay $10 million for 30% utilization."

## Chronology

> *Salesforce*
>
> **1999** Mark Benioff co-founds Salesforce with Parker Harris after an Oracle sales career, launching the first cloud-delivered CRM against on-prem incumbents.
>
> **Early 2000s** "No software" campaign and Siebel displacement: SMB beachhead priced at tens of thousands per year versus million-dollar on-prem contracts.
>
> **2000s** force.com, Apex, and AppExchange launch; SOAP APIs and third-party integrations (DocuSign, Zoom) create platform stickiness.
>
> **2013** ExactTarget acquired for $2.5 billion, anchoring Marketing Cloud and large-scale M&A for new front-office categories.
>
> **2010s–2020s** Acquisitions expand analytics and integration (Tableau, MuleSoft), collaboration (Quip, Slack), and AI (Einstein via tuck-ins); Service Cloud becomes largest product line.
>
> **Ongoing** Dreamforce scales from ≈250–300 attendees at a San Francisco hotel to 100,000+ in-person with ≈1 million streaming; V2MOM planning cascades company-wide twice yearly.
>
> **2021-10** Garrett discusses Salesforce on Business Breakdowns EP.29 at ≈$270 billion market cap with ≈$26 billion revenue guidance and ≈20% CRM share of a ≈$100 billion market.
