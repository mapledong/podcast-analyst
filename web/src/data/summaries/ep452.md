## Background

> Henry Ellenbogen built his philosophy by studying long-run public market winners and the mistakes of selling too early. He argues that over rolling 10-year periods, about 40 stocks out of roughly 4,000 public companies compound at 20% a year and produce more than 6x returns. The conversation covers Walmart, Domino's, Duolingo, Figma, DoorDash, Affirm, Toast, Colliers, Workday, Shopify, AI's impact on old-economy leaders, and why human judgment still matters when machines dominate short-term signals.

## Mental Model · *Do Less So You Can Do More*

> **Components**
>
> The market's biggest winners are rare enough to justify obsession. If only about 1% of public stocks create the exceptional compounding, the job is not broad diversification. It is maximizing the odds of identifying and holding the 40 companies that matter over each decade. Machines win short-term repeatable patterns; humans still win change and people judgment. Ellenbogen says quants are better when the problem is known data and repeat behavior. Durable doubles down where the data is messy: whether a founder can do an act two, whether a culture can allocate capital, and whether AI changes a physical-world business. Market structure creates opportunity by shortening others' horizons. He estimates 80-90% of institutional flow is driven by one-month/three-month pod incentives or quants reacting to price signals. That can make earnings seasons more volatile than fundamentals justify and create chances for owners with longer horizons.
