## Background

> Ben and David split biographies — Alice Schroeder's The Snowball versus Roger Lowenstein's Buffett — to narrate Warren from child entrepreneur (paper routes earning $175/month in high school) through Columbia with Ben Graham, the Buffett Partnerships, and the 1965 Berkshire coup. Sources include Graham's Security Analysis lineage, the Cities Service lesson with his sister Doris, and formative stakes in GEICO, American Express, and the textile mill he came to despise. The episode ends with Warren "retiring" in 1969 after his best year ever, distributing Berkshire, Blue Chip Stamps, and Diversified Retailing stakes to partners — while privately doubling down. Charlie Munger appears only in preview; the Intel seed-round pass via Grinnell ($100,000 into Noyce's company that Warren approved for the endowment but never touched personally) foreshadows a 45-year technology blind spot.

## Key Facts

> **F1** From 1957 through 1969 the Buffett Partnerships compounded at 2,795% versus 153% for the Dow — a ≈28× return with positive absolute returns and index outperformance every year; annualized IRR was 29.5% over the 12-year fund life.

> **F2** After the 1929 crash the Dow lost roughly 90% over three years and did not regain its pre-crash high until 1954 — a 25-year recovery that shaped Graham's depression-era cigar-butt framework Warren initially copied wholesale.

> **F3** In 1963–1965 Warren put up to $13 million of partnership capital into American Express after the Salad Oil scandal cut the stock over 50%; he judged AmEx could absorb $150 million+ in fraud exposure against $200 million cash and $500 million+ travelers-check float.

> **F4** Warren's 1965 board coup at Berkshire Hathaway followed Seabury Stanton reneging on an ≈$11.50/share tender; Buffett later estimated buying the textile mill cost ≈$200 billion in foregone compounding had he instead bought a standalone insurer outright.

> **F5** National Indemnity — acquired for Berkshire in 1967 — introduced the float model: long-tail policies where claims rarely cashed let premium dollars fund operating acquisitions, giving Buffett a lower effective cost of capital than any cigar-butt raider.

## Mental Model · *Float-Funded Compounding*

> **Components**
>
> Warren's arc in part one is three stacked realizations: (1) Graham's margin-of-safety cigar butts work until scale and bull markets make them scarce; (2) controlling other people's money requires structural independence — blind pools, annual liquidity windows, and partners who won't force sales at the wrong time; (3) insurance float is neither debt nor equity — it is other people's money with probabilistic repayment dates, ideal for a savant allocator. National Indemnity plus Berkshire's dying mills preview a holding company where capital flows to the center and operators run decentralized businesses. The Intel/Grinnell miss shows circle-of-competence rigidity: Warren approved $100,000 for Grinnell's endowment into Intel's seed round but barred the partnership from technology, a rule he mostly kept until Apple in 2016.
>
> **Application**
>
> When evaluating capital allocators, ask whether their structure matches their edge. Hedge funds with quarterly redemptions cannot run Warren's playbook; insurers and permanent-capital vehicles can. Float quality matters as much as investment skill — short-tail, commoditized insurance is not the same as GEICO's predictable auto book. Finally, the biggest mistakes may be structural (Berkshire textile) rather than analytical; sunk identity in a bad asset can still produce a great company if the allocator learns fast enough. Part two will show Munger pushing Warren toward quality businesses whose economics float can fund.

## What Makes It Work

> Part one covers the investor, not yet the conglomerate — but Warren's emerging edge is already defensible. Information and temperament: While Wall Street still treated stocks as gambling in the 1950s, Warren applied Graham-Dodd discounted cash flows and probabilistic thinking (AmEx salad-oil crisis) when others panic-sold. His partnership ground rules — value not popularity, minimize permanent capital loss, skin in the game — aligned incentives better than commission-driven brokers. Structural counter-positioning: Closed-end partnerships with secret portfolios and once-a-year liquidity let him avoid fire sales; corporate raiders and mutual funds could not copy the patience or the opacity. National Indemnity's long-tail float gave cost-of-capital advantages Graham-Newman never had. Reputation as moat (early): The Stanton tender dispute showed Warren would punish bad faith even at economic cost — building the trust that later let him backstop GEICO converts and join boards. Weaknesses appear simultaneously: selling GEICO and Disney too early, Intel omission, and emotional entanglement with Berkshire's mills. The episode frames Buffett not as a folk hero but as a complex optimizer whose singular focus on compounding — "money creates more money" understood at age 10 — produced extraordinary returns before Berkshire existed in its modern form.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Shutting the partnership after a 59% year was principle over greed — and possibly a mistake in isolation.
>
> **Q** Why wind down Buffett Partnership Limited in 1969 after the best year ever?
>
> **A** Warren wrote he was "out of step with present conditions" — cigar butts scarce, go-go markets rising, and scale making 50%+ returns harder. He closed to new capital in 1966 and quit entirely in 1969 rather than chase styles he did not understand. Ben calls it the purest illustration of "be fearful when others are greedy." David argues that in a vacuum he should have kept going — but the wind-down forced concentration into Berkshire, Blue Chip, and See's, seeding the next 50 years.

> **2.** The Berkshire textile purchase was economically irrational but strategically generative.
>
> **Q** Was buying Berkshire Hathaway Buffett's biggest mistake?
>
> **A** By 2010 Warren claimed the 1965 purchase cost ≈$200 billion in opportunity cost versus buying a pure insurer. The mills employed ≈15,000 people, burned cash, and could not be liquidated at book. Yet without Berkshire as a public vehicle he might never have bought National Indemnity or learned to run operating companies. The "soggy cigar butt" became the shell for everything — a mistake that compounded into an empire.

> **3.** Insurance float is the hidden engine — not stock-picking alone.
>
> **Q** Why does National Indemnity matter more than Sanborn Map?
>
> **A** Sanborn Map was a one-off corporate raid: 35% of capital, board control, 50% quick profit. National Indemnity supplied durable float from long-tail policies Jet Jack Ringwalt wrote — money Warren could deploy for years with probabilistic, not fixed, repayment. Marrying float to acquisitions lowered his cost of capital below any cigar-butt peer and ended the need to break up companies for cash.

> **4.** Warren's technology ban started as a explicit 1967 partnership rule.
>
> **Q** How close did Buffett get to Intel?
>
> **A** As Grinnell College endowment chair in 1967–68, Warren approved a $100,000 investment in Intel's seed round pitched by board colleague Robert Noyce — then wrote partners he would not touch semiconductors because technology was "way over my head." Grinnell invested; the partnership did not. He held that line for decades until Todd Combs/Ted Weschler's Apple position in 2016.

> **5.** Selling winners early is Warren's recurring sin of omission.
>
> **Q** What pattern do GEICO, AmEx, and Disney share in part one?
>
> **A** Warren bought wonderful businesses at panic prices — GEICO after Graham, AmEx after salad-oil fraud, Disney at an ≈$80 million market cap after Mary Poppins — then sold when price met his hurdle. David parallels Sequoia selling Apple for a $6 million profit. The partnership returns would have been even more extraordinary had he let great businesses compound instead of treating them as mispriced assets to exit.
