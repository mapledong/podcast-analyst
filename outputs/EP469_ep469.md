## Background

> Dylan Patel uses SemiAnalysis as a live case study of AI economics under real operating pressure. He says the firm moved from feeling like a heavy user to spending at a pace that reached about $5M run-rate and then about $7M in roughly a week, against salary expense around $25M. He describes that as already north of 25% of salary, with a path to 100% if the trajectory kept climbing. The conversation then shifts from headline spend to concrete workflow changes: analysts using Claude to accelerate reverse engineering, evaluate chips, and build tools that previously required broader teams. Patel cites examples where one person can produce output comparable to five, 10, or 15 people, changing hiring and org design decisions. He also references economics research built on the BLS task framework of roughly 2,000 tasks, with an estimate that about 3% are currently AI-doable. A separate example is a US power-grid mapping sprint he says ran at roughly $6,000 per day in token cost and delivered in weeks what would have taken much longer before. The throughline is simple: demand for useful tokens is effectively infinite at the right price-performance point, while supply, access, and managerial judgment stay scarce.

## Mental Model · *Token ROI Flywheel*

> **Components**
>
> Component 1 is access: secure reliable frontier-model capacity before demand spikes. Component 2 is allocation: route tokens to high-value tasks with measurable business impact rather than broad, undirected experimentation. Component 3 is compounding: recycle productivity gains into more ambitious projects, which increases demand for additional tokens and better tooling. Component 4 is capture: defend value through customer relationships, proprietary workflows, and execution speed before competitors copy the output.
>
> **Application**
>
> Apply this model by tracking four weekly metrics: token spend, equivalent labor replaced (for example five to 10 to 15 people of output), cycle-time reduction, and downstream value captured. If spend rises from $5M to $7M run-rate while value capture also rises, keep allocating. If spend rises without cycle-time or output gains, reallocate fast. The framework is useful for investors and operators comparing companies that all claim AI adoption but differ radically in token discipline.

## Key Insights

> **1.** The AI budget can become a labor budget substitute.
>
> **Q** What happens when one person can do the work of 5-15 people?
>
> **A** Patel argues that AI spend can substitute for labor growth in companies where frontier models produce output equivalent to five to 10 to 15 people for selected workflows. In that setup, the right comparison is not token bill versus prior SaaS bill; it is token bill versus incremental hiring and slower execution. He suggests faster companies may choose to keep headcount tighter while redeploying budget into model access, whereas slower movers could face both margin pressure and talent displacement.

> **2.** Ideas are cheap again, but implementation changed.
>
> **Q** What is different about AI-coded projects?
>
> **A** He describes a new implementation regime where idea quality again dominates because build friction has fallen. The episode example is a power-grid mapping effort run at roughly $6,000 per day of token usage, completed on a timeline that would historically require much longer setup and a larger team. Patel's point is that execution bottlenecks now shift from coding capacity to judgment: which projects deserve scarce frontier tokens and which should not be funded.

> **3.** Access to the best tokens may stratify the economy.
>
> **Q** Why might frontier models be less broadly deployed?
>
> **A** Patel expects token access to become a stratifying variable across firms and possibly across labor markets. If the best models are supply constrained, vendors will prioritize customers who can pay and who generate durable value. He links that to a potential underclass dynamic: organizations with limited access may not catch up even if they understand the opportunity, because the productivity gap compounds over time while leaders iterate faster.
