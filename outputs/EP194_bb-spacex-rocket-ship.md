## Background

> Matt Reustle breaks down SpaceX with Luke Ward of Baillie Gifford, which first invested in 2018 after building a relationship from 2016. Ward argues SpaceX is misunderstood as pure technology — the economic flywheel matters: cheaper launches expand addressable demand, which funds faster iteration on rockets and satellites.
>
> The conversation traces post-Apollo ossification (customers refusing risk on billion-dollar payloads), the graveyard of pre-SpaceX ventures lacking reusability and cost feedback loops, and how Falcon 9 development cost roughly one-tenth NASA's $4 billion estimate. Ward separates capex savings from operating leverage of boosters reflown 20+ times, explains Starlink as a Moore's Law-scaling connectivity factory (≈6,000 satellites, ≈$120/month US pricing), and positions Starship as the cost curve that could make orbital manufacturing and point-to-point cargo economically plausible.

## Key Facts

> **F1** NASA estimated Falcon 9 development at ≈$4 billion; SpaceX spent roughly one-tenth that. Falcon 9 lists ≈$70 million per launch — about 20× cheaper per kilogram than the space shuttle. Of ≈400 Falcon 9 flights, ≈320 reflights spread booster capex across an average ≈5 missions, with some boosters reused 20+ times; two-thirds of current launch capacity serves internal Starlink deployment.

> **F2** Starlink operates ≈6,000 low-Earth-orbit satellites delivering laptop-sized terminal broadband at roughly $120/month in the US. Ward cites ≈11 million US households lacking full broadband and hundreds of millions to billions of potential global users; incremental connectivity cost is factory-based (semiconductors, terminals) rather than civil-engineering trenching. Starlink revenue is expected to eclipse Falcon 9 and fund Starship.

> **F3** Starship targets ≈100–200 ton payloads with variable costs around $10 million per launch at scale (Musk has cited ≈$2 million), implying ≈$10/kg — over 100× cheaper than Falcon 9 and comparable to postal shipping economics. Ward notes ISS assembly took ≈36 shuttle flights over 10 years versus a Starship bay that could launch equivalent mass in one afternoon for under 0.1% of the cost; DOD low-Earth-orbit connectivity budgets grew from ≈$900 million to a projected ≈$13 billion.

## Mental Model · *Cost-Curve Flywheel Funding Frontier Tech*

> **Components**
>
> Slash launch capex through vertical integration, then convert disposable hardware into depreciable assets via partial (Falcon 9) and full (Starship) reusability. Use internal Starlink demand to keep launch pads supply-constrained and cash-generative, reinvesting into the next cost curve. Mars ambition acts as a forcing function attracting talent and pushing Earth-orbit economics — most cargo cost is escaping Earth's gravity well, not interplanetary distance.
>
> **Application**
>
> Ward says valuation hinges on extrapolating cost curves (rockets, satellites, terminals) and believing scale improves economics rather than degrades it. Investors should track Starship catch-and-refuel milestones, Starlink subscriber ramp, launch-pad throughput, and whether $10/kg materializes — that threshold could unlock semiconductor fabs, pharma crystallization, and data centers in orbit the way cheap shipping enabled e-commerce.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Reusability converts capex into operating leverage.
>
> **Q** Where do Falcon 9 savings actually come from?
>
> **A** Ward splits savings: development cost roughly one-tenth NASA's estimate, plus partial reusability spreading booster manufacturing over 5–20+ flights instead of one. Variable fuel and expendable upper-stage costs remain, but reflights drove ≈320 of ≈400 launches and made the industry supply-constrained on affordable access.

> **2.** Starlink subsidizes the Starship bet.
>
> **Q** Why is satellite internet central to SpaceX economics?
>
> **A** Starlink's ≈6,000-satellite mesh avoids terrestrial trenching costs and scales with semiconductor deflation. At ≈$120/month and millions of underserved households globally, Ward expects Starlink revenue to fund Starship development — aligning satellite production pace with the rocket needed to deploy them at hundreds per launch.

> **3.** Mars mission improves Earth-orbit economics.
>
> **Q** Should investors dismiss the Mars ambition?
>
> **A** Ward argues Mars is a talent and innovation forcing function: once in orbit, gravitational economics favor deep-space destinations, so Starship R&D that cuts Earth-to-orbit cost to ≈$10/kg also enables space stations, lunar Artemis cargo, and orbital manufacturing — Gwen Shotwell has said Starship could eventually exceed Starlink's value.
