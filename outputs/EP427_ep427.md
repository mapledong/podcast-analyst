## Background

> Bill Gurley returns for a state-of-venture conversation in summer 2025. His frame is systems thinking: founders, mega funds, LPs, secondary markets, banks, regulators, and public buyers are all acting rationally, but their combined behavior has changed the venture cycle. The discussion centers on the rise of multi-billion-dollar venture platforms, the backlog of private unicorns created around 2021, weak IPO/M&A exits despite a strong NASDAQ, and new late-stage private markets that can look like public markets without public disclosure.

## Mental Model · *Incentive Pile-Up In Private Markets*

> **Components**
>
> The private-company backlog is not just a valuation problem; it is a governance and incentive problem. Founders anchor on peak valuation, liquidation preferences can consume much of a lower-priced sale, and many LP gatekeepers have little incentive to force markdowns. A company once marked at $2B may be worth $400M, but the cap table can make that truth painful for everyone. The IPO market is losing its role as the default growth handoff. Large private rounds and organized secondary liquidity let companies provide capital and employee liquidity without accepting IPO underpricing, seven-point fees, or quarterly public scrutiny. The best late-stage private investors can also buy 30% of a round rather than 1-2% of an IPO allocation. Public investors may be losing the most attractive compounding years. Amazon went public below $1B and public investors captured much of the trillion-dollar journey. If future Amazons remain private until hundreds of billions or even $1T, public-market savers inherit later, more mature economics.

## Key Insights

> **1.** The private-company backlog is not just a valuation problem; it is a governance and incentive problem.
>
> **Q** Why don't stale unicorn marks correct quickly?
>
> **A** Founders anchor on peak valuation, liquidation preferences can consume much of a lower-priced sale, and many LP gatekeepers have little incentive to force markdowns. A company once marked at $2B may be worth $400M, but the cap table can make that truth painful for everyone. Branded VC firms that once raised roughly $500M every three or four years may now manage commitments closer to $5B, while $300M checks can go into 12-month-old AI companies that are not really 'late stage' except by check size.

> **2.** The IPO market is losing its role as the default growth handoff.
>
> **Q** Why stay private when public markets are strong?
>
> **A** Large private rounds and organized secondary liquidity let companies provide capital and employee liquidity without accepting IPO underpricing, seven-point fees, or quarterly public scrutiny.
