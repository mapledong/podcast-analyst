## Key Facts

> **F1** Tesla filed to go public in summer 2010 and priced June 29, raising $226 million; the stock jumped 41% on opening day. Acquired highlights this as the first IPO of an American car company since Ford in 1956.

> **F2** By early 2009 Tesla had shipped roughly 2,500 Roadsters at about $100,000 each — roughly $250 million in cumulative sales that could have sustained a niche EV maker. Survival also depended on a $465 million interest-bearing U.S. DOE loan requiring U.S. EV production, which anchored the Fremont strategy.

> **F3** Elon led the 2004 Series A with $6.5 million of the ≈$7 million prototype budget after prior backers offered only $500,000. By fall 2008 he was about $55 million personally invested while Tesla burned ≈$4 million per month; early 2007 board review pegged Roadster build cost near $200,000 versus an intended $85,000 sale price.

> **F4** The March 2016 Model 3 reveal drew 325,000 reservations within a week at $1,000 deposits each — more demand than all prior Tesla vehicles sold combined. By summer 2018 most reservation holders still lacked cars; hosts likened the deposit pool to roughly $325 million in interest-free financing.

> **F5** Tesla bought the abandoned NUMMI Fremont plant for $42 million in 2010 — a facility that cost at least $1 billion to build and once produced up to 500,000 Toyota and GM vehicles annually. In 2012 Model S output ran to dozens per week, illustrating the gap between acquiring capacity and scaling into it.

## Mental Model · *Miracle-Stacked Automotive Startup*

> **Components**
>
> Building cars demands manufacturing learning curves, working capital, and regulatory compliance far heavier than software. Tesla sequenced proof points — Roadster (≈2,500 units), Model S (brand and factory), Model 3 (mass demand) — while repeatedly raising on the best story available (private rounds, IPO, DOE loan, near-Google $6B deal in early 2013). OTA updates and in-house software give partial tech economics inside a hardware shell, but hosts stress Toyota pioneered the lean manufacturing discipline Tesla still lacked in 2012.
>
> **Application**
>
> EV entrants must survive capital winters and production ramps, not just win spec sheets. At episode time (27% short ratio, junk-bond trading below par) investors split between tech-multiple optimism (Supercharger, batteries, brand) and auto-multiple skepticism (Model 3 at 5,000–6,000/week as break-even, 2018 guidance of 500,000 cars requiring ≈325,000 in Q4 alone). Pricing Tesla requires judging whether miracle-threading continues or timelines slip into dilutive equity raises.

## What Makes It Work

> Tesla's edge is product desirability plus tech-native design: over-the-air updates, touchscreen-centric UX, direct online sales, and Supercharger infrastructure incumbents lacked. Volunteer owners staffing Texas showrooms where employees cannot sell illustrate Apple-level fan intensity. Outsourced auto supply chains since the 1960s–90s let Tesla source batteries and components without a single incumbent's parts bin — unlike the DeLorean. Weaknesses dominate the 2018 half: Fremont assembly (not Gigafactory) is the bottleneck; SolarCity added $3.4B long-term debt and $500M operating burn; Musk's board ties (Kimball Musk, SolarCity/SpaceX-linked directors) blur governance; SEC whistleblower risk and expensive short interest complicate capital raises when equity is the escape hatch.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Zip2 shaped Elon's control obsession.
>
> **Q** Why did Musk lead the Series A personally?
>
> **A** After VCs pushed him from CEO to CTO at Zip2 (≈$300M Compaq sale, ≈$22M to Elon), he vowed never to be housed again. He put $12M into x.com/PayPal, netted ≈$180M after taxes in summer 2002, then wrote $6.5M of Tesla's first $7M round in 2004 — an individual leading a Series A was almost unheard of. That control instinct later kept a near-done 2013 Google acquisition ($6B, up to $5B capex commitment) from closing once Q1 sales surged.

> **2.** Roadster economics nearly killed the company twice.
>
> **Q** What broke in 2007–2008?
>
> **A** Interim CEO Zev Drori demanded 24/7 work; Lehman's collapse froze luxury-car credit just as Tesla burned $4M/month. Board review found ≈$200K build cost on an $85K price target. Christmas Eve 2008 financing (≈$40M round, Sergey Brin in for $500K) followed layoffs of 25% of staff. Iron Man premiered the same crisis — Downey modeled Stark on Elon and put a Roadster nearest Stark's workbench, linking pop-culture hope to a company weeks from bankruptcy.

> **3.** Model S revolutionized product, not throughput.
>
> **Q** Why call it the iPhone of cars?
>
> **A** Unanimous 2012 Motor Trend Car of the Year, seven-seat configs, and OTA feature drops (Autopilot) rewrote expectations — but Fremont ran dozens of Model S units weekly while the former NUMMI line had built 500,000 cars/year. Henrik Fisker–era design talent and Aston Martin's chief designer raised aesthetics; capital to reach even high-single-digit billions of equipment spend remained the constraint. Early 2013 near-death saw Musk reassign hundreds of employees to cold-call Model S buyers, posting $562M Q1 sales and $11M net income that saved the Google deal.

> **4.** SolarCity was a balance-sheet merger, not a moat.
>
> **Q** What did the $2.6B 2016 deal add?
>
> **A** Elon's cousins (Rive brothers) founded SolarCity after Burning Man 2004 advice; EverDream proceeds had bailed Tesla once before. Ben Rush's breakdown: ≈$5.8B leased solar systems, ≈$1B PP&E, ≈$3.4B long-term debt, and ≈$500M prior-year operating burn — not a cash cow. SolarCity's finance-first model succeeded until it pivoted to making panels (≈2014–2015), mirroring Tesla's own manufacturing struggles. Powerwall synergies were framed as 10–15-year vision, not near-term profit.

> **5.** 2018 is a capital-and-ramp crucible.
>
> **Q** Bull vs bear at episode air date?
>
> **A** Bears: 2018 delivery guidance of 500,000 cars needs ≈150,000 in Q3 and ≈325,000 in Q4 after 53,000 in Q2 — tripling then doubling again on a manual Fremont line (tents in parking lots). Junk bonds trade below par; equity raises may face SEC friction. Bulls: Model 3 at 5,000–6,000/week hits break-even production; P/S ≈4.9x (≈18x Ford) prices in software/energy optionality; a $2.5B raise at ≈$250/share could be ≈6% dilution if the story holds. Hosts note shorting is crowded at 27% short ratio — expensive either direction.

## Investment Ideas

> **1. TSLA** · 🔴 SHORT · ●○○ Low
>
