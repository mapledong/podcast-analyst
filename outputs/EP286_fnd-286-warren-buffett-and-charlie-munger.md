## Background

> Senra reads Peter Bevelin's All I Want to Know Is Where I'm Going to Die, So I'll Never Go There — a conversational compilation of Buffett and Munger quotes from Berkshire meetings and biographies. Recommended by Eric Jorgenson, it presents four characters in a library of wisdom. Themes repeat: mistakes are normal, prevention beats cure, focus beats multitasking, inversion finds what to avoid, and compound interest needs time and a good wave.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** Munger: wisdom is prevention; Buffett: it wasn't raining when Noah built the ark. They schedule time to think — Munger says most people schedule like dentists and pay a huge price — while Buffett claims three good decisions a year suffice at Berkshire, which has no meetings or committees.

> **F2** Inversion runs the book: study how businesses die, collect instances of bad judgment, engineer failure out. Buffett on Singleton and Teledyne: get facts then think, don't read others' opinions. Compound interest is a snowball — Berkshire was small once; surfing Microsoft or Intel beats jumping off the wave.

> **F3** Debt kills smart people via leverage zeros; cash is oxygen 99% ignored until absent. Envy not greed drives much misery — Munger's line that someone will always get richer faster. Sol Price designed Costco-style membership by deciding which businesses to avoid; passion not money is the first hiring filter.

## Mental Model · *Invert, Read, Endure*

> **Components**
>
> Structure life and businesses to survive bad decisions because stupidity is inevitable. Gain wisdom by sitting and reading, mute outside opinions, and decide quickly only after long preparation. Avoid assholes, debt, and envy; partner with first-class people in good businesses and let time compound.
>
> **Application**
>
> Keep calendars open like Buffett — say no firmly to requests forwarded from 80 subsidiaries. Practice inversion on hiring and strategy: list what you refuse to do, as Sol Price excluded bad-check customers. Hire missionaries who love the business; culture once set is nearly impossible to change.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Multitasking taxes cognition.
>
> **Q** Why do Buffett and Munger reject busy schedules?
>
> **A** Munger succeeded because of long attention span, not IQ edge. Multitasking crowds out deep thought — Edwin Land and Jobs agreed intense concentration releases hidden ability. Bezos needs eight hours sleep for three high-quality decisions daily; Buffett targets three per year.

> **2.** Most disasters repeat because people ignore history.
>
> **Q** How should founders use biographies?
>
> **A** Buffett: learn most lessons from others' mistakes. Munger notes little originality in common disasters — drunk driving, cults, repeated business failures. Senra hears direct endorsement of studying effective individuals and financial history, the Founders podcast thesis.

> **3.** Cash and frugality enable survival.
>
> **Q** What kills rich people?
>
> **A** Munger: liquor, ladies, leverage. Buffett: bright people go broke on borrowed money; life snaps at the weakest link. Berkshire hoards Treasury bills — more money lost reaching for yield than at gunpoint — to stay in the game when others get foolish.
