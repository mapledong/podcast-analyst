## Background

> Season 2 Episode 7 (≈81 min, May 2018) covers pre-PowerPoint culture (35mm slides, overhead transparencies) and Gaskins' Bell Northern Research product discipline. Forethought founders Rob Campbell and Taylor Poole left Apple 1982 raising $700K then $2.5M for a GUI OS — Microsoft Windows ended that. Gaskins joined spring 1984 with 5% equity; publishing arm launched FileMaker. Apple invested January 1987; February launch event; Microsoft bid escalated from $5.3M to $14M cash after $1M first-month sales. Team stayed on Sand Hill Road as Microsoft's graphics business unit. Primary source: Gaskins' book Sweating Bullets.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Microsoft acquired Forethought Inc. in July 1987 for $14 million cash — Microsoft's first corporate acquisition and Apple's first strategic investment in another company occurred within three months. VCs received $12 million of the $14 million price; ≈$2 million went to common shareholders. Board rejected a $12 million Microsoft stock offer because MSFT was up 6× in the prior year — venture capitalists demanded cash, forfeiting billions in eventual upside.

> **F2** PowerPoint 1.0 shipped mid-March 1987 for Macintosh; project codename 'Presenter' renamed PowerPoint for trademark clearance before the February 1987 launch event. Initial product produced black-and-white overhead transparency printouts — color slides and digital projector video output arrived in PowerPoint 3.0 for Windows and Mac in 1992. Apple invested January 1987 only after Forethought literally began printing floppy disks for the first production run.

> **F3** First production run: 10,000 copies at roughly $100 each sold out immediately — $1 million revenue in the first month on Mac only, a rate hosts call 'literally sold out, sorry you can't get the bits anymore.' By the last six months of 1992, standalone PowerPoint revenue exceeded $100 million annually inside Microsoft. Over PowerPoint's first decade (1987-96) cumulative revenue surpassed $1 billion; by 1997 over 20 million copies were in use.

> **F4** Robert Gaskins led development starting with Dennis Austin and a tiny team over roughly three years while Bob pitched bridge rounds and laid off staff — 'laying off people, pitching for money, or building PowerPoint probably in that order.' Forethought raised $700K then $2.5M for its failed Foundation OS, burning ≈$2 million of just over $3 million raised before the spring 1984 pivot when Gaskins joined with 5% equity.

> **F5** Microsoft's initial March 1987 cash offer was $5.3 million pre-ship; board rolled dice, shipped, then sold for $14 million after $1M first-month sales. Jeff Raikes convinced Bill Gates presentations were 'a whole genre,' not a Word outline feature. Forethought stayed on Sand Hill Road as Microsoft's graphics business unit. Hosts cite PowerPoint owning ≈95% of today's digital presentation market and compare $14M to ≈$100 billion Office value over 25-30 years.

## Mental Model · *Category Creation on New UX Paradigm*

> **Components**
>
> GUI Macintosh enabled WYSIWYG slides versus 35mm production overhead and handwritten transparencies. Gaskins defined 'presentation' as distinct software category — storyline, slide sorter, speaker notes — before market existed. Forethought's OS pivot failed ($2M burned, demo day showed nothing) but VC Dick Kramlich let Gaskins restart with 5% equity. First-mover Mac excellence caught Gates' application strategy: own every desktop job versus Lotus/WordPerfect. Microsoft initially offered $5.3M cash pre-ship; post-$1M month returned at $12M stock then $14M cash. $14M for ≈10-person team cheap versus building internally; Jeff Raikes overruled Gates' 'feature of Word' framing.
>
> **Application**
>
> When new platforms emerge, watch small teams defining interaction patterns incumbents dismiss as features — Gates said put outlines in Word; Raikes proved presentations are a genre. Microsoft's Forethought buy mirrors later category-leader acquisitions — purchase distribution-ready product when Windows amplifies. Enterprise investors should track bundle attach: PowerPoint rarely sold alone; Office 365 switching costs and .pptx format lock-in compound decades. Forethought irony: company that could barely raise bridge rounds built the vehicle through which more capital would be raised than any prior human endeavor.

## What Makes It Work

> PowerPoint's moat became Office integration — Excel chart paste, shared fonts, Outlook meeting hooks, enterprise IT deployment — plus .pptx standard creating billions of archived decks. Forethought's Mac-first shipping gave Microsoft GUI credibility; independent Sand Hill Road business unit avoided smothering product while receiving Gates/Maple strategic guidance during buggy pre-Windows 3.0 era. PowerPoint group refused shipping on unstable Windows 2.0 — Word/Excel arrived first; convergence waited until PowerPoint 7.0 (labeled 95) matched Word/Excel version numbers. Network effects weak per-user but enormous via format lock-in — hosts admit using PowerPoint over Keynote because collaborators expect it. ≈95% market share versus Google Slides/Keynote. Weaknesses: 'death by PowerPoint' cultural backlash; cloud collaboration challengers; AI slide generators may commoditize templates. Versus Aldus Persuasion and Dave Winer's MORE (Microsoft withdrew LOI same day as Forethought offer): Microsoft won distribution via Windows bundle — same playbook crushing Lotus. FileMaker publishing arm became long-running Apple subsidiary — another Forethought-era asset.

## Key Insights

> **1.** First acquisition set Microsoft's M&A playbook.
>
> **Q** Why did Gates buy instead of build?
>
> **A** Forethought shipped working Mac product months ahead of internal alternatives — black-and-white transparencies first, but category-defining UX. $14M bought working code after $1M first-month sales on 10,000 disks; ≈10 engineers could not be hired fast enough to beat Lotus in applications war. Microsoft escalated from $5.3M pre-ship to $14M cash after board rejected $12M stock. Pattern: buy leaders when Windows distribution amplifies — 'original Facebook-style acquisition' left team independent on Sand Hill Road.

> **2.** Mac-first was strategic, not religious.
>
> **Q** Why launch on Macintosh?
>
> **A** GUI essential for WYSIWYG slides; Windows 1.0-2.0 too buggy for external developers — PowerPoint group refused shipping until Windows 3.0 stability. Forethought proved concept on Mac February 1987; Apple invested January 1987 once disks printed. Windows port followed 1990 post-Office bundle. Platform sequencing: ship where UX works, expand where volume lives — same debate as modern mobile-first products.

> **3.** Bundle economics dwarf standalone pricing.
>
> **Q** How did $14M become billions?
>
> **A** PowerPoint rarely sold separately — bundled Word/Excel Mac 1989, Windows 1990. Standalone run-rate exceeded $100M annually by late 1992; first decade revenue topped $1 billion; hosts compare to $100B Office value over 25-30 years. VCs' cash preference at sale forfeited Microsoft stock up 6× — classic venture timing error. Acquisition ROI measured on suite retention and .pptx lock-in, not shrink-wrap price.

> **4.** Gaskins invented job-to-be-done, not just software.
>
> **Q** What problem did PowerPoint solve?
>
> **A** Business presenters iterated slowly on 35mm slides and overhead transparencies — Gaskins mapped presentation as distinct category with storyline view, slide sorter, speaker notes. Bell Northern Research product rigor plus Berkeley interdisciplinary PhD (English, linguistics, CS) produced spec competitors copied decades. Market research and feature prioritization notes in Sweating Bullets document paradigm-shift product planning.

> **5.** Cultural backlash didn't kill adoption.
>
> **Q** Why does everyone hate PowerPoint but use it?
>
> **A** Deck culture enables middle-manager communication at scale — military, enterprise, investment banking ('10,000 hours' joke). ≈95% market share despite Keynote/Google Slides UX praise; collaborators expect editable .pptx files. PowerPoint 1992 added digital projector video output, replacing physical media. Habit plus IT deployment beats UX purity — irony that presentation software company could barely raise money yet enabled more capital formation than any prior institution.
