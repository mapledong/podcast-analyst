## Background

> Patrick O'Shaughnessy breaks down Formula One with Armand Gokgol-Klein (Ruane, Cunniff & Goldfarb) and F1 CEO Stefano Domenicali (appointed late 2020). F1 is one of the world's largest sports leagues by fan base (≈400 million unique fans vs. NFL ≈100 million, Premier League ≈300 million), racing in 21–23 countries per season. Revenue (≈$2 billion) splits across promoter fees (≈33%), broadcast (≈33%), sponsorship/advertising (≈15%), and other (≈15%, mainly Paddock Club VIP). Liberty Media acquired the series in 2017 from Bernie Ecclestone's decades-long commercial control; the episode pairs investor framing with Domenicali's insider view on fans, Netflix's Drive to Survive, and the landmark 2021 Concord Agreement.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** F1 generates roughly $2 billion in annual revenue from ≈400 million unique global fans — about $5 monetization per fan versus the NFL's ≈$16 billion / ≈100 million fans (≈$150 each) and the Premier League's ≈$6 billion / ≈300 million (≈$20). Three primary buckets each contribute ≈33% (promoter fees), ≈33% (broadcast), and ≈15% (sponsorship); promoter fees range from near-zero (Monaco) to $10–20 million (core Europe) to $30 million+ (flyaway/government-backed races).

> **F2** The league is asset-light: it does not own tracks or teams. The largest cost is team revenue share — historically ≈65% of pre-team-share EBITDA paid to constructors. Top teams employ ≈1,500 people; pit stops take ≈1.9 seconds. Mercedes cites ≈$1 billion in advertising-equivalent brand value from F1 while spending $300–500 million annually; Ferrari treats F1 as its primary marketing budget.

> **F3** Liberty expanded the calendar from ≈19 races under Bernie to 23 (cap ≈25 in current agreements). The 2021 Concord Agreement introduced technical spending caps, guaranteed revenue floors for smaller teams through 2025, and triggered financial-market interest in team ownership. Sponsorship was historically a simple menu (placard fees across races) with no global soft-drink or tech partner for years — Gokgol-Klein sees clear opportunity to double or more via regional and local brand lending.

## Mental Model · *Global Luxury Brand Licensing on an Ecosystem*

> **Components**
>
> Bernie Ecclestone built F1 as a global spectacle — glamour, VIP events, Monaco — not just racing, running the business from his London home with minimal sales staff and maximal cash extraction. Liberty inherited under-monetized rights: broadcast concentrated in ≈5 Western European pay-TV deals, sponsorship unsliced, and adversarial team-league dynamics (zero-sum competition, top teams spending $300–500M/year). The Concord Agreement aligns constituents: sprint races approved even when suboptimal for individual teams because ecosystem value rises.
>
> **Application**
>
> Gokgol-Klein compares to Ferrari post-Fiat spin — singular halo product professionalized into multi-revenue streams — and Hermès scarcity management (limit races to preserve specialness; NASCAR oversaturation caution). Investment levers: grow sponsorship white space, OTT/F1 TV Pro and streaming competition for broadcast rights (Amazon NFL bid as precedent), direct fan monetization (gaming, virtual race data feeds, social media openness), and promoter-fee growth by helping hosts monetize better. Change is cultural and slow — patience required.

## Key Insights

> **1.** F1 monetizes fans at a fraction of peer leagues — room to grow without adding races.
>
> **Q** Where is the biggest revenue upside?
>
> **A** At ≈$5 per fan vs. NFL ≈$150, the gap is execution not audience. Sponsorship has the most white space (could double+) via regional/local partners and brand-lending deals; broadcast may inflect if global streamers bid against linear incumbents; promoter fees grow when F1 helps hosts monetize concerts and tiered ticketing — best promoters pay the highest fees and still profit.

> **2.** The 2021 Concord Agreement turned teams into investable assets.
>
> **Q** Why did the new Concord matter?
>
> **A** Domenicali says it guarantees revenue visibility for all teams through 2025, caps technical spending, and clarified cost structures — triggering financial-market acquisition interest. Budget caps compress the gap between Mercedes/Ferrari ($400M+ spend, $1B brand value) and backmarkers who lacked revenue to compete; Porsche and VW Group negotiations hinge on financial sustainability plus stable engine rules post-2025.

> **3.** Fan engagement is becoming direct and multi-layered.
>
> **Q** How is Liberty growing the fan base?
>
> **A** Netflix Drive to Survive brought casual U.S. fans; drivers now use social media freely; F1 TV Pro offers super-fan camera angles; esports envisions virtual cars fed live race data. Domenicali wants complementary content — technical DNA for avid fans, human stories for newcomers — and Miami 2022 as a U.S. beachhead while avoiding oversaturation (23–25 race ceiling due to logistics and team strain).

## Chronology

> *Formula One*
>
> **1950s–1970s** F1 begins as weekend racing gatherings; Bernie Ecclestone sees commercial opportunity in broadcast and promoter rights.
>
> **1980s–2016** Bernie centralizes commercial rights, concentrates Western European pay-TV (≈5 key contracts), pushes costs onto teams; business run from his London home.
>
> **2017** Liberty Media acquires F1; calendar ≈19 races; begins OTT (F1 TV Pro), social media openness, and Drive to Survive partnership.
>
> **2020** Stefano Domenicali (Ferrari 1991–2014, Imola-born) appointed CEO after teams agree to latest Concord framework.
>
> **2021** New Concord Agreement: revenue-sharing transparency through 2025, technical budget caps, sprint-race experiments; ≈23 races, ≈$2B league revenue.
>
> **2022+** Miami Grand Prix targets U.S. growth; Porsche/VW rumored entry pending engine Concord; Lawrence Stroll merges Racing Point with Aston Martin OEM brand.
