## Background

> Ben and David narrate 170 years of The New York Times — from Republican co-founder Henry Raymond and the 1863 draft-riot Gatling gun defense through Adolph Ochs's 1896 turnaround. Ochs bought the bankrupt paper with $75K borrowed, seller notes, a Grover Cleveland endorsement letter, and a fake New York bank balance wire; he cut price from 3¢ to 1¢ undercutting Pulitzer and Hearst yellow journalism with the motto 'All the news that's fit to print.' Times Square and the New Year's Eve ball followed. The Sulzberger era brought Punch's TV transition, Holocaust coverage failure, 1990s diversification madness (Red Sox, about.com for $410M, $3B debt-financed buybacks), and Carlos Slim's 2009 $250M rescue at ≈14% interest. The 2011 metered paywall cut monthly uniques from 160M to 80M while subscriptions flipped the revenue mix. AG Sulzberger's leaked 2014 Innovation Report shifted power toward distribution inside the newsroom. By 2021: debt-free, BuzzFeed/Vox/Recode editors hired as columnists, 130 Pulitzer prizes (2× peers), trading ≈4.8× revenue versus Netflix ≈10× despite faster subscriber growth — Trump-era 'failing NYT' irony as the business boomed.

## Key Facts

> **F1** By 2021 The New York Times reached roughly 7.5 million digital subscribers — more than The Wall Street Journal, The Washington Post, and 250 Gannett local papers combined — while the closest peer (WaPo) had roughly 2 million. Management set a 10 million subscriber goal for 2025 and expected to exceed it.

> **F2** Subscription revenue is now roughly three times advertising revenue, reversing the 20th-century newspaper model where print ads exceeded subscriptions by about 3× as recently as the 1950s–1960s. Digital growth hit 47% in 2016 — tech-company pace for a 170-year-old institution.

> **F3** Carlos Slim's January 2009 rescue injected $250 million of debt at roughly 14% interest plus warrants for about 10% more equity when junk bonds traded near 6–7%; Slim became roughly 13–17% owner after exercising warrants. Company became completely debt-free by 2017 while peers still struggled with print collapse.

> **F4** The Daily podcast (launched February 2017) receives roughly 4 million downloads per recent episode; roughly 75% of listeners are under 40; the podcast ad business generates roughly $36 million revenue with roughly $7 million year-over-year growth. The Daily hit 100 million downloads in its first year after February 2017 launch.

> **F5** Wirecutter (acquired for roughly $30 million) generates roughly $50 million in annual affiliate revenue; the crossword app produces roughly $30 million per year; other digital lines grow roughly 60% year-over-year at near-zero marginal cost. Times trades near 4.8× revenue versus Netflix near 10× despite faster subscriber growth.

## Mental Model · *Franchise Journalism as Subscription Utility*

> **Components**
>
> Historic local newspaper monopolies paired paid circulation with print advertising until Craigslist, Google/Facebook ads, and smartphones destroyed the dual-revenue model. The Times survived by substituting a global English-language brand for geographic monopoly — a subscription utility for serious news. The 2011 metered paywall traded reach (160M to 80M monthly uniques) for ARPU; the Trump news cycle accelerated trust-based subscriptions independent of traffic volume.
>
> **Application**
>
> In subscription media, brand plus scale beats aggregation arbitrage — BuzzFeed rewrote headlines but could not replicate 130 Pulitzer prizes. Metered free tiers balance SEO discovery with conversion. Family trust ownership enables long journalism bets (Innovation Report, cooking, Wirecutter) that public markets might reject, though it also tolerated 1990s debt buybacks and the about.com mistake.

## What Makes It Work

> The Times stacks durable powers rare in media. Brand power: identical article under NYT masthead versus unknown blog commands different willingness to pay; 130 Pulitzers reinforce trust Ben calls 'print things I wouldn't believe elsewhere.' Scale economies: ≈7.5M subscribers amortize fixed reporting costs — $100K+ average journalist starting salary (≈2× industry) affordable only at scale; Netflix analogy for content spend. Process power (tentative): 170-year newsroom machine — daily paper/web cycle insiders can't fully articulate but executes (Gay Talese); family stewardship preserves ethos. Switching costs moderate for readers (habit, crossword, cooking bundles). Weaknesses: Not a tech company organizationally — journalists retain power; intro $1/week trials face churn at full price; partisan perception risk; video/streaming experiments underwhelming; Fox News proved partisan cable ≈$6B revenue at 50% EBITDA — neutral positioning may cap TAM versus outrage economics. The Times trades ≈4.8× revenue while Netflix trades ≈10× despite 40%+ digital subscriber growth — bear case cites smaller TAM (management estimates half of Netflix's 200M subs); bull case cites in-house content creation versus Netflix licensing.

## Key Insights

> **1.** The paywall worked because brand trust exceeded free alternatives.
>
> **Q** Why did traffic halve yet subs soar?
>
> **A** Monthly uniques fell ≈160M to ≈80M after the 2011 metered paywall — outrage at charging online. David Perpich (family member) led the launch; free articles dropped from 20 to 10. Simultaneously digital subs exploded: 400K year one, subscriptions surpassed ads for first time in decades by 2013, 47% growth in 2016, 300K added in Q1 2017 when Trump took office, 2.2M by 2017, 7.5M by 2021. Revenue mix flipped to subscriptions 3× ads — opposite of 20th-century newspapers where print ads were triple subscriptions. David Perpich led the 2011 paywall launch as a family member and potential future publisher.

> **2.** Ochs's 1896 turnaround is the template — cheap, sober, entrepreneurial.
>
> **Q** How did a Chattanooga publisher save The Times?
>
> **A** Bought bankrupt Times with $75K borrowed, $600K seller notes, Grover Cleveland endorsement letter, and fake New York bank balance wire. Dropped price from 3¢ to 1¢ while Pulitzer and Hearst colluded toward 5¢ sensationalism. Motto 'All the news that's fit to print' plus impartiality credo rebuilt prestige against yellow journalism. Times Square ball marketing shows never waste distribution moments. Ochs used seller notes again — 'the yokel from Tennessee' bought The Times using none of his own money. Ochs used $600,000 in seller notes and a Grover Cleveland letter in the 1896 takeover financing.

> **3.** Family trust cuts both ways — mission preserved, capital blunders tolerated.
>
> **Q** Why dual-class shares in 1969?
>
> **A** Ochs trust ensures descendants keep ownership only by maintaining independent journalism mission — not selling. Prevented Sulzbergers from cashing out but also enabled 1990s debt-funded $3B buybacks and about.com ($410M) while family couldn't sell — value trapped illiquidly. AG Sulzberger's Innovation Report reversed culture toward product/growth inside newsroom: 'winning at journalism but falling behind at getting journalism to readers.' The 1969 dual-class Ochs trust blocks sale unless journalism mission is maintained.

> **4.** Economics look like Netflix; governance looks like historic newsroom.
>
> **Q** Is The New York Times a tech company?
>
> **A** Ben: no — journalists hold power like Apple designers or Google engineers hold theirs; digital transformation serious (Reforge training, 85% ad staff turnover) but core is fearless reporting per trust. Bull: creates content in-house vs Netflix licensing; bear: can't move at startup speed; trades 4.8× revenue vs Netflix 10× despite 40%+ sub growth. Management goal 10M subs by 2025 likely blown past. Ben argues journalists hold power the way Apple designers do — not a tech-company governance model.

> **5.** Winner-take-most in English digital news — middle hollows out.
>
> **Q** Who loses when Times wins?
>
> **A** More digital subs than WSJ, WaPo, Gannett 250 papers combined; hires BuzzFeed/Vox/Recode EICs as columnists. Indie Substacks occupy long tail; middle-tier metros shrink. Scale lets Times pay 2× industry salaries — flywheel competitors can't match without similar global sub base. LA Times ≈500K subs versus Times 7.5M. LA Times has roughly 500,000 subscribers versus the Times at 7.5 million — middle tier hollows out.

## Chronology

> *The New York Times Company*
>
> **1851** New-York Daily Times founded by Henry Raymond and George Jones; Morgan family among financiers
>
> **1863** Draft riots — publisher defends Times building; Civil War era Republican ties
>
> **1896** Adolph Ochs buys bankrupt Times — $75K borrowed, 1¢ price, impartiality credo
>
> **1904–1907** Times Tower opens Times Square; New Year's Eve ball tradition begins
>
> **1935–1963** Sulzberger succession — Arthur, Dryfoos, Punch; WWII Manhattan Project access and Holocaust undercoverage
>
> **1969** Company goes public with Ochs trust dual-class control — model for later tech founders
>
> **2005–2008** about.com $410M; debt-financed buybacks; revenue peaks ≈$3.2B+
>
> **Jan 2009** Carlos Slim $250M debt at ≈14%; traffic and ad collapse
>

>
> **2014** Innovation Report leak — newsroom must own distribution; cooking app success
>
> **Feb 2017** The Daily podcast launches — 100M downloads year one
>
> **2016–2020** Trump-era subscriber surge; debt paid off; Wirecutter and games scale
>
