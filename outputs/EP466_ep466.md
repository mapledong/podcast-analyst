## Background

> Alan Waxman runs Sixth Street and frames the last century of American finance as three systems: Glass-Steagall stability from 1933 to 1999, deregulated leverage into the GFC, and the post-2010 Basel III/Dodd-Frank system where safer commercial banks sit beside private capital. The episode is really about how a good System Three can become fragile when private capital adopts public-company-style fee growth incentives. He is especially worried about perpetual private BDCs, wealth-channel money, SMAs, narrow direct-lending vehicles, and the pressure created when FRE multiples move from 10-15x to 25-30x+. Sixth Street's answer is wide-aperture private capital, matched liabilities, and saying no when capital is too easy.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** System One ran roughly 1933-1999 after Glass-Steagall and the FDIC; it followed the 1929 crash and about 9,000 bank failures. System Two followed the 1999 repeal, with investment bank and commercial bank leverage reaching 20-30x or more before the GFC.

> **F2** Post-GFC private capital grew from about $2T to $14-15T; private credit grew from roughly $500B to about $2T. Waxman says that matched assets and liabilities made this healthy until behavior changed around 2018 and accelerated after COVID.

> **F3** Fee-related earnings multiples in asset management moved from roughly 10-15x in the early 2010s to 15-20x around 2018 and then 25-30x+. Perpetual private BDC redemptions have exceeded typical 5% quarterly limits, exposing the liquidity mismatch.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Private credit is not automatically systemic, but some structures have recreated the classic crisis ingredient: illiquid assets funded by impatient liabilities.
>
> **Q** Is today's private credit issue another GFC?
>
> **A** Waxman says not yet: the problem is only about five years old and the economy is still healthy. But the setup is recognizable. Wealth-channel investors got quarterly liquidity against narrow direct-lending and private-market portfolios, and redemption requests are already breaching 5% gates.

> **2.** The factory model starts on the liability side, not the asset side.
>
> **Q** Why do underwriting standards fall?
>
> **A** Because firms first industrialize fundraising, then need to industrialize deployment. If hit rates rise from about 0.5% to 2-3%, capital goes out faster, but capped-upside credit investors may accept terms that can move a 50% LTV to 120% LTV for only a 10% return.

> **3.** The better answer is not necessarily more regulation; it is better market discipline and better product design.
>
> **Q** What would responsible private capital look like?
>
> **A** Govern inflows, widen the aperture beyond one narrow strategy, tell investors to assume they cannot get money back in a 1929 or 2008 environment, and keep clarity of purpose. Sixth Street could have launched perpetual private BDCs and says it chose zero.

## Chronology

> *American Finance Systems*
>
> **1929-1933** Crash, roughly 9,000 bank failures, Glass-Steagall and FDIC create System One
>
> **1998-1999** Deutsche Bank buys Bankers Trust; Glass-Steagall repeal starts System Two
>
> **2008-2010** GFC leads to Basel III, Dodd-Frank, and tighter bank leverage/liquidity rules
>
> **2010s** Private capital fills the gap left by constrained banks
>
> **2018** SMAs and factory-model behavior begin to show up more clearly
>
> **2020-2022** Post-COVID fundraising and deployment accelerate; many assets bought at high prices
>
> **2026** Perpetual private BDC redemptions force an industry recalibration
