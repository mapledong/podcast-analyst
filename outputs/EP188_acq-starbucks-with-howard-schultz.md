## Key Facts

> **F1** Howard Schultz acquired the six-store Starbucks chain for $3.8 million in 1987 after Il Giornale raised roughly $1.6 million; Bill Gates Sr. helped remove blocking investor Sam and the Gates family invested in the round — Il Giornale then rebranded as Starbucks Corporation while the original company became Peet's. Coffee Connection deal: $23 million for 23 stores — Frappuccino became 7% of revenue by 1996.

> **F2** Starbucks store economics featured roughly 80% gross margins on high-frequency, low-ticket visits (sub-1% of household income per trip) with a roughly two-year payback — Howard doubled store count year-over-year in the late 1980s, a pace unheard of in traditional retail. Howard stepped down CEO 2000, returned 2008, interim again 2022 — succession remains unsettled.

> **F3** In 1988 at a 33-store company, Howard introduced health insurance for part-time workers including domestic partners plus Bean Stock grants making employees 'partners' — turnover ran roughly half the industry average, enabling customization culture competitors struggle to replicate at scale. Retraining 135,000 partners afternoon-long in 2008 turnaround banned thousands of efficiency cuts.

> **F4** The 1992 IPO carried roughly a $250 million market cap on $93 million in revenue — the first publicly traded pure-play coffee company; the 1994 Coffee Connection acquisition ($23 million, 23 stores) brought the Frappuccino trademark that became 7% of revenue by 1996. United Airlines and Costco beans distributed Starbucks before store ubiquity in many markets.

> **F5** Today Starbucks operates roughly 39,000 stores in 86 countries with roughly 18% of revenue from China, roughly 33% of orders via mobile, and roughly $1.8 billion in customer prepaid balances on roughly $14 billion loaded per year; in 2008 market cap fell from $30 billion to under $7 billion with Howard citing roughly seven months from insolvency. Howard argues scaling humanity is the answer — not just caffeine commodity extraction.

## Mental Model · *Humanity at Scale via Partner Economics*

> **Components**
>
> Starbucks combines addictive caffeine, ritualized third place, and barista respect — virtuous work perception reduces turnover versus burger chains, enabling customization at thousands of units. Roughly 80% gross margin on frequent small tickets beats traditional restaurant traffic limits; names on cups and drink mods increase loyalty and margin simultaneously.
>
> **Application**
>
> Retail founders sell experience plus substance — affiliation, not just coffee. Scale humanity via tenure economics before automation. Prepaid mobile float (≈$1.8B on ≈$14B loaded) funds expansion if breakage is managed. The 2000–2008 succession gap proved operators must be developed before founders step away.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Milan trip created the company Howard bought.
>
> **Q** Why not stay a bean retailer?
>
> **A** 1983 Italy showed espresso bars as community hubs — founders resisted restaurant stigma; Howard built Il Giornale ($1.6M raised), then acquired Starbucks name and six stores for $3.8M when original owners debt-loaded from Peet's deal (6:1 debt-to-equity). Without café model, no third place, no Frappuccino platform, no global density. Original Starbucks became Peet's; Il Giornale became Starbucks Corporation. Ben spent $23,000 at Starbucks since 2011 — opening credibility with Howard as co-host.

> **2.** Partners are the product moat.
>
> **Q** Why health insurance at 33 stores?
>
> **A** 1988 part-time benefits (including domestic partners) and Bean Stock align partners with experience quality — turnover ~half industry; training cost dominates wage savings. Barista respect is tailwind burger chains lack — humanity scales when tenure scales (Costco rhyme Howard cites). ≈80% gross margin on high-frequency small tickets enables investment competitors can't match at scale. Original Starbucks founders resisted café model due to restaurant business stigma among investors.

> **3.** 2008 was insolvency, not slow growth.
>
> **Q** How bad was the crisis?
>
> **A** Market cap $30B to <$7B; ≈7 months from insolvency; same-store sales negative; Howard closed stores, retrained 135,000 partners afternoon-long, banned thousands of efficiency cuts hurting experience — profits $315M (2008) to $945M (2010). Howard: 'We were seven months from being insolvent.' Proof model is experiential, not just real estate. Closed US stores during crisis but invested China leadership. Howard closed stores during 2008 but continued investing in China under Belinda Wong's leadership.

> **4.** Mobile is treasury plus UX tradeoff.
>
> **Q** Why push gift cards and app?
>
> **A** ≈$14B/year loaded, ≈$1.8B float interest-free; reduces ≈$0.30 Visa swipe on $6 tickets when customers preload $25. ≈33% orders mobile — throughput win but risks depersonalization and 'mobile order unavailable' frustration; Howard acknowledges re-architecture choices. Gift card float works like insurance premium float. Mobile order reduces roughly $0.30 Visa swipe when customers preload $25 gift cards.

> **5.** China required Belinda Wong, not copy-paste.
>
> **Q** How did China become 18% of revenue?
>
> **A** Tea culture market — Howard closed US stores during 2008 but invested China leadership; Belinda Wong built thousands of stores (new every 15 hours peak 2017); JV discipline versus franchise. Japan 1996 echoed Microsoft Kay Nishi cold-call international playbook. Today ≈39,000 stores in 86 countries; China ≈18% of revenue. Japan 1996 launch echoed Microsoft Kay Nishi cold-call international expansion playbook.
