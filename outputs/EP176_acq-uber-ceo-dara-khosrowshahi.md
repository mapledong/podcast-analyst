## Background

> Ben and David frame the conversation as checking in post-2019 IPO coverage. Uber now runs two complementary businesses (mobility and Eats), shed hardware, international experiments, and far-future bets, and reached break-even or slight profitability on several metrics — a stark contrast to ≈$3B losses the year before IPO. Dara's pre-Uber path: Allen & Company introduction to Barry Diller, IAC's Expedia acquisition (term sheet signed pre-9/11; IAC honored deal despite MAC clause), and 13 years as Expedia CEO competing with Booking.com — an 'execution machine' Ben and David say Expedia couldn't match. Recruitment lore: headhunter contact while happy in Seattle; Meg Whitman and Jeff Immelt were public candidates; Dara kept process secret with Barry Diller's loyalty. Turnaround context: Uber still had ≈$10B revenue and hypergrowth when Dara arrived — boardroom and C-suite catastrophe, not demand collapse. Shareholder base largely turned over at ≈$70–80B market cap; ≈15% float hitting market at once moved supply-demand dynamics.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Uber revenue exceeded ≈$30B in the past 12 months vs ≈$10B two years ago (Ben's opening stat — revenue, not GMV) — with mobility and Eats as complementary core businesses after divestitures.

> **F2** Pre-IPO Uber lost close to ≈$3B the year before going public — described as the most capital burned before an IPO in history at the time; Dara's tenure shifted toward break-even/slightly profitable depending on metric.

> **F3** Dara was Expedia CEO from 2004–2017; IAC signed to buy Expedia before September 11, 2001, and did not invoke material adverse change — a formative loyalty/trust moment with Barry Diller.

> **F4** SoftBank-led round described as ≈$80B prisoner's dilemma — university endowments, fund-of-funds, sovereigns tied to Uber private mark; ≈15% of stock hitting market at once moves supply/demand.

> **F5** Dara prefers owning mobility (≈20% take rate discussion) over Eats (≈30%) — citing ecosystem durability and Bill Gurley's 'rake too far'; ride price increases driven by demand exceeding supply plus inflation, not deliberate margin extraction; Lyft's comeback illustrates 'never over till it's over.'

## Mental Model · *Execution Machine on a Two-Sided Flywheel*

> **Components**
>
> Dara imports Booking.com lessons: build geographic density, then expand verticals — Uber cross-markets rides and Eats in ways single-category competitors cannot. Marketplace economics self-regulate supply via the 'invisible hand' (driver hourly earnings chart in earnings); Uber doesn't set ride prices directly. Profitability unlocks after shedding non-core bets and surviving shareholder turnover (≈15% supply shock). Take-rate discipline matters: lower rake on mobility preserves driver/restaurant ecosystem vs maximizing short-term margin (Gurley thesis). Turnaround ≠ shrink — Uber grew fast with ≈$10B revenue while fixing governance. CEO recruitment under crisis: reverse diligence limited; Travis-era momentum and brand-as-verb mattered. Public persona restraint (contrast with Kalanick tweeting) reflects 'head of state' CEO style.
>
> **Application**
>
> Marketplace operators should measure cross-sell between demand pools (rides → Eats) before chasing standalone CAC in each vertical. When evaluating gig platforms, compare take rates against partner retention and supply quality — high rake invites regulatory and competitive backlash. Leaders taking over hypergrowth messes need loyalty from prior boards (Diller/Expedia) and willingness to inherit $80B prisoner-dilemma cap tables (SoftBank ≈$10B stake context). Investors: profitability plus ≈3× revenue growth in two years suggests operating leverage if supply-demand balance holds.

## What Makes It Work

> Uber's durable edge is global two-sided density plus cross-vertical demand aggregation — mobility feeds Eats acquisition cheaper than DoorDash can replicate rides cross-sell, and internal ads monetize attention without third-party media buys. Post-Dara divestitures sharpen focus: no distraction from autonomous hardware bets, failed international experiments, or speculative freight-first strategies (freight remains but deprioritized in interview). US rideshare competition weakened vs 2016 peak; Lyft execution-machine comparisons persist — Dara acknowledges formidable food-delivery rival (DoorDash implied). Marketplace pricing transparency: expensive rides reflect supply shortage, not monopolistic pricing — airport SF example showed demand-driven cheap fares. Driver earnings data (≈5-year chart referenced) supports supply gating philosophy — platform protects hourly economics vs flooding supply. Weaknesses: food delivery still more contested; autonomous disruption timeline still uncertain (2023 SF robotaxi visibility); prior shareholder overhang and endowment-dependent private marks created systemic risk during transition. Dara's Expedia experience didn't fully prepare for operating physical service quality vs pure marketplace layer. Moat is execution + bundle, not IP — Booking parallels show copyable strategy if capital and density match. Dara’s Expedia lessons: Booking.com as execution machine — geography-first hotel density, vertical expansion. Uber cross-markets rides→Eats (largest Eats acquisition channel); advertising on own app treated as internal media. Supply self-regulates via earnings — platform does not set prices but tunes incentives. Post-IPO ≈$3B loss year vs break-even now; shareholder base fully turned over (≈15% float events move stock). SoftBank $80B prisoner’s-dilemma round. NYT board: family control enabled subscription bet. Self-driving finally visible in SF. Mobility preferred over Eats on rake — Bill Gurley ‘rake too far’; leaving margin for ecosystem partners builds durability. Near-death: 2014 Taylor Swift exit, COVID, Travis era — yet revenue ≈$10B→$30B in two years pre-interview.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Uber's turnaround happened during hypergrowth.
>
> **Q** How is this a 'turnaround'?
>
> **A** Ben notes it may be history's only turnaround with ≈$10B revenue, explosive growth, top talent, and a verb-level brand — while burning cash and suffering boardroom/C-suite catastrophe. Dara fixed governance, capital discipline, and portfolio focus without shrinking the core marketplace. Revenue then scaled ≈$10B to ≈$30B in two years — operational maturity catching up to scale.

> **2.** Booking.com shaped Uber's playbook.
>
> **Q** What did Expedia teach Dara?
>
> **A** Booking built geographic hotel markets then expanded verticals — an execution machine Expedia couldn't match in the States where Booking was unknown. Dara applies the same frame: Uber's rides density cross-markets Eats; competitors lack both sides. Geographic market-building beats one-off growth hacks.

> **3.** Prices follow supply-demand, not greed.
>
> **Q** Why are rides more expensive?
>
> **A** Dara pushes back on 'good business now' as the sole explanation: inflation plus demand exceeding supply moved prices — not Uber setting fares (marketplace clears dynamically). SF airport example showed cheap fares when supply abundant. Economists theorize; Uber has one of history's largest work-consumption datasets.

> **4.** Mobility beats Eats on unit economics philosophy.
>
> **Q** Which business would Dara rather own?
>
> **A** Mobility — despite lower ≈20% take rate vs ≈30% for Eats — citing ecosystem durability and Gurley's rake-too-far warning. Higher rake increases vulnerability to competitors and partners. NZS Capital-style long-term thinking favors leaving margin on the table for network stability.

> **5.** CEO transitions under a microscope.
>
> **Q** How did Dara get the job?
>
> **A** Headhunter outreach while content in Seattle; secret process vs public Meg Whitman/Jeff Immelt candidacies; Barry Diller personal loyalty (no Expedia board conflict like Microsoft/Disney transitions). Limited reverse diligence — Travis contact uncertain in transcript — but ≈10-year Uber momentum and verb brand convinced him. Shareholder base turnover (≈15% float event) followed SoftBank-era cap table unwind.
