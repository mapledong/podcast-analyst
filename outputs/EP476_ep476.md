## Background

> Dara took over Uber in August 2017 after thirteen years running Expedia for Barry Diller. Daniel Ek pushed him for the job with a line he still uses: life is about impact, not happiness. In COVID he drove and delivered in San Francisco himself to see what drivers deal with. The conversation is where Uber goes from $10B+ of free cash flow to robotaxis, drones, and a single app for local life. His instinct is supply-first — nail the cars and restaurants, demand follows — the opposite of how he built Expedia.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** Uber throws off $10B+ free cash flow on 10B+ trips a year. Eats went from under $1B to $100B+ gross bookings since 2017. Uber One has 50M members growing ≈50% YoY; ≈13% of Eats orders start in the ride-hailing app. Reserve (scheduled rides) is a $5B+ run-rate business at 99%+ reliability.

> **F2** 30+ AV partners including Waymo, Nvidia, Lucid, Nuro, Pony.ai. Robotaxis on Uber's network run 30%+ more trips and revenue per car per day than off-network — that matters when the hardware costs $60–70K. Santander put up a $1B fleet financing line; Uber is building depots, charging, and AV insurance itself.

> **F3** They burned through the year's AI budget in one quarter. Engineers in India are posting ≈10x the code commits using agents. Feed and search models are ≈10,000× bigger than a few years ago; Uber gets the destination right ≈75% of the time before you type. Dara is holding headcount flat as output rises.

## Mental Model · *Supply-First Marketplace + Vector Decomposition*

> **Components**
>
> Get supply before you chase demand — drivers, restaurants, AV partners. That's the flip of Expedia, where you built hotel inventory after the traffic. When things are messy, break the problem into separate vectors (board, ops, product) and fix one at a time. Optimize for the driver who's on the app six hours, not the rider who taps once for thirty seconds.
>
> **Application**
>
> On AVs: partners build the driver, Uber builds the ecosystem around it. In 2017 he fixed the board and went on listening tours. In thin markets he seeds supply in the next 50–200 cities before marketing. Uber One loses money in year one, pays back in years two to four — like a bundle, not a discount gimmick.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Uber won't manufacture robotaxis. It wants to be the layer AV companies plug into — depots, insurance, financing, and riders waiting on day one.
>
> **Q** Why are AVs on Uber's network 30% busier?
>
> **A** Utilization is everything on a $60–70K car. On his network they run '30% or more' trips and revenue per vehicle per day versus going solo. He announced a $1B Santander line to help fleets finance the hardware.

> **2.** He thinks AV opens another trillion-dollar market — Lucid's midsize target is $60–70K, and hardware costs drop ≈30–40% each generation.
>
> **Q** How does cheaper AV grow beyond the taxi market?
>
> **A** Wall Street sized Uber on taxis; they're multiples bigger now. Cheaper rides pull in new use cases. Drones could cut delivery from 25–30 minutes to 10–15. He says the wow factor on AV wears off by minute three — then it's just transport.

> **3.** AI inside Uber is already real — bigger models, agents writing code, and a full-year budget gone in a quarter.
>
> **Q** How do you handle AI cost after blowing the budget in three months?
>
> **A** Go hard on frontier models to experiment, switch to cheaper ones at scale. Meter hiring as engineers get more productive. In ≈7 years he thinks you'll talk to book a ride; you'll still open the app to watch the car on a map.
