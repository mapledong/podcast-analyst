## Background

> First public podcast content sourced from a venture firm CEO Summit — Emergence Capital facilitated the session. Peter Gassner (Veeva, ≈$2B revenue life-sciences cloud) and Eric Yuan (Zoom) compare fundraising histories, board dynamics when Zoom raised after profitability, hiring/compensation philosophy, pricing, and defensibility. Veeva's Pfizer bake-off invoice funded product development; multimillion-dollar upfront pharma contracts contrast Zoom's self-serve credit-card motion — yet both achieved capital efficiency. Marketing discipline: Zoom's viral growth from a TechCrunch article (≈50,000 users) not paid ads; Eric's Super Bowl ad had strict payback criteria (~one month). Peter delayed second product years after CRM success to preserve focus — counter to bundling instinct.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Veeva Systems raised approximately $4 million total and did not consume all of it; Peter Gassner reports ≈$2 billion in revenue at time of interview with strong margins.

> **F2** Zoom raised about $130 million ($30 million Emergence-led round plus ≈$100 million later) but Eric Yuan states none of that venture capital was spent building the operating business.

> **F3** Zoom started with roughly 25 employees scaling quickly to ≈40 — 39 engineers and Yuan, with no product managers, marketing, or sales initially.

> **F4** A TechCrunch article drove approximately 50,000 early Zoom users — kernel of product-led virality without billboard marketing.

> **F5** Veeva was among the first public companies to convert to B-corporation status (2019 conversion cited in grading segment).

## Mental Model · *Earn the Customer Every Year*

> **Components**
>
> Capital efficiency emerges from business model plus culture: Veeva's upfront pharma cash funds development but Peter avoids multi-year lock-ins that discount 30% annually and trap pricing. Zoom raises VC after profitability for strategic reasons, not need. Hiring homegrown leaders keeps hunger; importing Salesforce/HubSpot stars adds cost without guaranteed fit. Marketing spend demands short CAC payback (Eric ≈6 months; Super Bowl ≈1 month). Product must be non-consensus but correct — not random contrarianism. Parallel: annual contract discipline mirrors internal promotion discipline.
>
> **Application**
>
> Founders should ask if customer revenue can fund MVP before raising (Pfizer invoice model). Default to annual SaaS contracts even when multi-year cash is tempting. Keep early headcount engineer-heavy; defer GTM hires until product pull exists. Before big marketing bets, define payback window in months not years.

## What Makes It Work

> Zoom's moat combines product quality, viral PLG, and enterprise reliability at lower price than WebEx/Cisco. Eric cites happiness, uptime, and cost — operational efficiency converts to margin and cash flow. Network effects are moderate (Zoom meetings need other party on Zoom) but brand became default during COVID. Veeva owns life-sciences vertical CRM with regulatory workflow depth competitors lack — high switching costs once validated in pharma QA environments. Second product (content/CMS) launched years after CRM to avoid diluting focus; revenue split discussed but CRM anchor persisted. Both resist capital as moat — efficiency is cultural. Weaknesses: Zoom faces Microsoft Teams bundling; Veeva TAM is vertical-limited though expandable product-by-product. Zoom's pandemic stock swing irrelevant to Yuan — he ignores ticker, focuses customers. Peter's second product (Vault CMS) launched years after CRM — deliberate delay that contravenes conventional land-expand bundling but preserved engineering focus and customer trust in regulated pharma environments.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Venture rounds can be optional, not operational fuel.
>
> **Q** Did Zoom and Veeva need their VC money?
>
> **A** Peter never needed beyond ≈$4M; Eric raised ≈$130M but did not spend it on building Zoom — angels funded early team. Eric's later round (2017, Peter on board) occurred despite profitability — strategic capital, not survival. Misconception that great outcomes require burning VC on ops is disproved by both.

> **2.** Customer-funded product beats speculative build.
>
> **Q** What is Peter's formula?
>
> **A** Ask customer if they want product; if no, ask what they use; build better than incumbent workaround. Pfizer sent invoice during bake-off — cash financed development. Works when buyer pain is acute and budgets exist (enterprise pharma), harder in greenfield consumer.

> **3.** Lean early teams force focus.
>
> **Q** How did Zoom start on people?
>
> **A** ≈39 engineers, no PM/marketing/sales — Yuan plus engineers from WebEx diaspora. No billboards; TechCrunch drove ≈50k users and word-of-mouth retention. Capital efficiency is headcount discipline plus product that spreads.

> **4.** Multi-year deals and superstar hires add hidden costs.
>
> **Q** Why avoid 3-year SaaS contracts?
>
> **A** Peter argues multi-year deals embed ≈30% annual discount and lock pricing — product must re-earn customer yearly. Same philosophy for promoting internal leaders versus importing VP-of-Sales from marquee SaaS — hunger and fit beat resume prestige.

> **5.** Marketing must pay back fast or skip it.
>
> **Q** How fast should CAC return?
>
> **A** Eric targets ≈6-month payback generally; Super Bowl spot required ≈1-month return — validating decision not brand vanity. Most founders use 12-month LTV/CAC; Zoom's bar is stricter, enabling profitability at scale.
