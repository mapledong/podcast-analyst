## Background

> Alex Sacerdote left Fidelity about twenty years ago — Peter Lynch was a mentor — and built Whale Rock into a $17B Boston shop that's compounded roughly 44% over the last three years. Ten people, thousands of meetings a year, many with private companies. Patrick has him walk through the same S-curve lens he used for internet, mobile, and cloud, applied to AI. Anthropic is the name he keeps coming back to. The through-line: adoption feels tiny even when every CIO says they're 'doing AI,' and Whale Rock entered 2026 net short traditional application software.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Whale Rock bought into Anthropic in August 2025 at ≈$180B; revenue had gone from ≈$100M to ≈$1B with ≈$9B the next target. Power users were spending up to ≈$100/day on tokens (≈$20–30K/year). With ≈20M coders globally, Sacerdote puts the coding market alone at $500B+. Compute is sold out worldwide.

> **F2** Anthropic has ≈14–15M daily users. Sundar Pichai said ≈10 bps of knowledge workers use AI in a deep way. Enterprise app-layer AI is under 1% penetrated. Anthropic still has only ~half the compute it wants. A 90-page internal deck on Claude Code helped seal the August investment.

> **F3** Whale Rock bought Nvidia in 2023 at ≈4x earnings, Tesla in 2019 at ≈5x, Apple at ≈4x. Software was ≈40–50% of the book five years ago; entering 2026 they were net short app software. Of ≈60 model startups at a 2023 industry webinar, almost all are gone. On chips he scores names by AI sales mix × share — 30% × 30% = 60 is the bar.

## Mental Model · *S-Curve × Competitive Advantage × Underappreciated Earnings Power*

> **Components**
>
> Find the adoption curve — sometimes an 'L-curve' when friction drops fast. Ask who has a real moat: network effects, scale, IP, lock-in. Then model earnings a few years out while the market still thinks linearly. On chips he multiplies AI revenue mix by AI market share; 60+ is the sweet spot.
>
> **Application**
>
> This cycle he went chips first (2023), then Anthropic after the 2025 coding inflection, sold SaaS when AI SKUs didn't move revenue, and dug into hardware via scuttlebutt — Celestica at ≈8x as sole Google TPU box supplier. He tries to sell around 30–40% penetration; says he sold Apple too early at ≈50% US smartphone share in 2012.

## Key Insights

> **1.** At an April 2023 webinar he counted ≈60 model startups; most are dead now, and it looks like a three-way race — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google — the way cloud did.
>
> **Q** Why wouldn't the model layer just commoditize?
>
> **A** Quality at the frontier still matters — 80% to 85% on benchmarks is a big jump, not a rounding error. Open source trails without compute. Revenue really clicked when coding took off; Claude Code in mid-2025 was the moment they leaned in.

> **2.** Salesforce does ≈$40B a year; its AI ARR is maybe $500–700M. That's the gap he's watching — AI is 1–2% of sales for most software names, not the 20%+ the old 'rule of 40' math would need.
>
> **Q** Can incumbents like Salesforce actually make money on AI?
>
> **A** Not much evidence yet — AI products barely register on the P&L. They went net short app software entering 2026. He leaves room for a bull case: plug Claude into Slack, let agents live inside CRM. But he said that's still half-baked.

> **3.** Data-center hardware was a commodity for forty years under Intel x86. AI workloads growing ≈10x a year are forcing real engineering again — HBM, boards, liquid cooling.
>
> **Q** Why are suppliers like Celestica suddenly interesting?
>
> **A** An AI server runs $200–300K versus $5K for the old kind — and it can't break. Celestica was the sole Google TPU supplier at ≈8x earnings when they found it. Sequoia's Shawn Maguire told him he wished he could run a hardware fund again.
