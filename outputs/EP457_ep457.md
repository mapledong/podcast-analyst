## Background

> Ben Horowitz co-founded Andreessen Horowitz after building and running companies himself. Here he explains why a16z was designed to market itself, hire former founders as investors, and support companies long after the old venture handoff point. The conversation ranges from AI startup physics to American competitiveness, public safety technology in Las Vegas, and the culture systems that keep a large venture firm from drifting. Horowitz contrasts his own era — going public at 18 months with roughly $2M trailing revenue — with today's norm where companies may need roughly $1B revenue before IPO, forcing private platforms to support founders at $100M–$300M scale with product lines, channels, and international expansion that used to happen after listing.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** Horowitz says AI companies can now reach revenue levels that once took 12–15 years in a fraction of the time; he cites Cursor going past $1B of revenue quickly and frames some AI markets as potentially $5T rather than $50B — forcing investors to ask both market-size and incumbent-catch-up questions simultaneously.

> **F2** The old IPO path changed: he went public at 18 months old with $2M trailing revenue, companies once went public at roughly $50M revenue, and now many need roughly $1B revenue — so private investors must help at $100M, $200M, and $300M revenue stages.

> **F3** In Las Vegas, Horowitz says the murder clearance rate was roughly 94% versus roughly 75% in San Francisco, 30s in Chicago, and below 60% nationally; after deploying public safety technology, he says crime fell over 50% and police shootings of suspects fell close to 75%.
