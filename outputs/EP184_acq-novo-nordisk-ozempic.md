## Key Facts

> **F1** Insulin discovered 1921 in Toronto; August Krogh (Nobel 1920) licensed for Denmark after wife Marie's diagnosis — Nordisk founded 1923; Pedersen brothers' Novo 1925 two blocks away, sparking rivalry that drove protamine insulin, pens, and pumps.

> **F2** WW2: Novo supplied insulin across Nazi-occupied Europe; Nordisk stayed smaller in Denmark. By 1984 Nordisk reached 20% global insulin share while Novo fell to 30% — rivalry accelerated both firms before 1989 merger.

> **F3** 1989 merger united ≈$1B insulin revenue and ≈50% global share (Lilly 45%, Hoechst 5%); insulin market grew from ≈$500M (1980) to ≈$2B (1989) — GLP-1 obesity TAM today dwarfs both figures.

> **F4** Novo Nordisk Foundation controls ≈77% voting / ≈28% economic shares — blocking hostile bids (≈25–30% premium would force sale in public-company land). Novo Holdings endowment ≈$120B, largest charitable foundation globally (2× Gates).

> **F5** Semaglutide first trial 2008; by 2024, 69% of revenue from GLP-1 drugs (51% diabetes, 18% obesity) vs 22% insulin. Market cap climbed $100B (2013 Saxenda hype) → $250B (2021) → $300B (2022) during rising rates; 84% gross margins, ≈$10B net income.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Ozempic is 30 years in the making.
>
> **Q** Why did GLP-1 explode in 2023?
>
> **A** Semaglutide's first diabetes trial was 2008; GLP-1 research began in the early 1990s. Obesity indication and social media visibility lagged science by decades. Public feared insulin companies would be disrupted — instead Novo leveraged delivery and metabolic expertise into a vastly larger TAM. Pre-2020 obesity drug market was only ≈$744M; Saxenda (2013) failed the magical 10% weight-loss threshold physicians demanded. Pre-2020 obesity drug market only ≈$744M — Saxenda on market 6 years before semaglutide obesity label. Marie Krogh's diabetes motivated August's 1920 Nobel-laureate Denmark license.

> **2.** Foundation control is strategic asset.
>
> **Q** Why not acquired by Big Pharma?
>
> **A** Novo Nordisk Foundation holds ≈77% voting / ≈28% economic — in public-company land, a 25–30% premium offer forces sale. Without foundation control, hosts argue GLP-1 pipeline might have been harvested for short-term cash during 2016's 40% stock drawdown. Novo Holdings endowment ≈$120B makes it the world's largest charitable foundation, 2× the Gates Foundation. 2016 stock fell 40% on pricing pressure when market cap was well under $100B. Novo enzyme side business crashed before merger forcing Copenhagen street partnership.

> **3.** Rivalry accelerated both firms.
>
> **Q** Why merge Novo and Nordisk?
>
> **A** Two-block Copenhagen rivalry produced protamine insulin, pens vs pumps, and patent fights. WW2 scaled Novo across occupied Europe; Nordisk compounded at 30% annually in the 1970s. Recombinant DNA capex and Novo's enzyme crash forced 1989 merger when global insulin market was still only ≈$500M — combined entity reached ≈50% share (Lilly 45%). Nordisk compounded sales 30% annually in 1970s before passing Novo's 30% global share. 1984 Nordisk 20% share passed Novo 30% before 1989 combination.

> **4.** Delivery mechanism is the moat layer.
>
> **Q** Why pens and pumps matter for Ozempic?
>
> **A** Novo invented the insulin pen; Nordisk pushed pumps — decades tuning injection pharmacokinetics translate directly to once-weekly GLP-1 dosing patients tolerate. Semaglutide's longer half-life vs liraglutide (daily) drove 15%+ BMI reduction vs sub-10%. Molecule innovation without delivery excellence fails — Saxenda proved that. Switching costs high: patients stay on working drugs 15+ years (hosts' allergy medicine example). Saxenda record 2015 $16B revenue year yet stock flatlined before CEO change.

> **5.** Obesity TAM reframes diabetes company.
>
> **Q** Did Ozempic kill insulin?
>
> **A** No — expanded into weight loss (Wegovy) while diabetes demand persists. By 2024, 69% of revenue from GLP-1 (51% diabetes, 18% obesity) vs 22% insulin. Market cap went vertical: $100B (2013) → $250B (2021) → $300B (2022) even as rates rose. Lilly's tirzepatide competes — duopoly dynamics echo insulin's three-player era. Only ≈8% of approved drugs earn back R&D; 10% of winners provide 50% of industry profits. CRISPR and gene therapies noted as future disruption outside last 30-year returns.
