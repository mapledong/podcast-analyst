## Background

> Neil Mehta links his childhood exposure to his grandfather's gun collection and craftsmanship to how GreenOaks studies companies. The firm sees a great company as an artist's work: hard to build, easy to imitate badly, and obvious only after the customer experience breaks old trade-offs. The episode covers Coupang, Wiz, Stripe, Robinhood, Uber, Tencent, and the mistake of passing on SpaceX. Mehta keeps returning to the idea that very few founders and companies create most of the value, so process should be designed for the rare outlier, not the average meeting.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Founder quality and business quality usually converge.
>
> **Q** Can a great founder build a bad business?
>
> **A** Mehta says it is rare. Truly remarkable founders usually find their way into better markets because they think in customer experience, learn quickly, and refuse mediocre trade-offs. GreenOaks has been involved with Coupang for about 15 years and invested a little under $1B over roughly 10 years, leading 5-8 rounds and continuing to buy some shares after it went public.

> **2.** Great growth investing is not private-equity-style coverage.
>
> **Q** Why not track every Series A, B, and C?
>
> **A** Coverage works for most of the market, but the best founders should not need to explain themselves to a junior process. GreenOaks wants to understand them from the outside in and bring all of GreenOaks, not a small slice. Mehta says GreenOaks usually wants growth companies that can become a meaningful part of the S&P 500. He cites the idea that about 1% of the S&P 500 drives around 90% of the value.

> **3.** Concentration is rational if returns are power-law distributed.
>
> **Q** Why focus so much effort on a few companies?
>
> **A** If a tiny share of companies creates most long-term equity value, then the job is not to be broadly average; it is to find the future Apple, Google, Facebook, Tencent, Coupang, or Wiz early enough to matter. For the rarest founders, Mehta estimates there may be only 10-15 per year worth full-firm focus. He contrasts that with a broader market where tens of thousands of meetings happen and 90%-plus of the industry can operate with coverage processes.
