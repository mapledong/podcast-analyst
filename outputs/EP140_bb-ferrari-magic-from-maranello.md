## Background

> Zack Fuss breaks down Ferrari with Brian Lum of Baillie Gifford, a shareholder since the 2015 IPO (and indirectly via ex-Chrysler). Lum opens with scale contrast: Ferrari delivered 13,000 cars in 2022 while global auto production runs ≈80 million units; BMW sells ≈2 million. New Ferraris start around $250,000 in the U.S.; the company carries a ≈$70 billion market cap exceeding GM or Ford. Lum traces Enzo Ferrari's 1929 racing team origins, first road car in 1947 at age 50, obsessive Maranello factory culture, and Formula One as marketing spend (≈10% of revenue). Three pillars — brand heritage, manufacturing obsession, and managed client relationships — drive a SaaS-like collector ladder where two-thirds of new sales go to existing owners and over half of clients own multiple Ferraris.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Ferrari delivered 13,000 cars in 2022 versus ≈80 million global industry units and BMW's ≈2 million; market cap ≈$70 billion exceeds GM/Ford. Expected revenue ~€6 billion with ≈90% from cars and spare parts; EBIT margins mid-to-high 20s versus single-digit auto peers and in line with luxury goods at 20–30%+. Gross margin mid-40s% with more than half of that consumed by R&D — Ferrari has no parent to subsidize research unlike Mercedes.

> **F2** Client economics: two-thirds of new cars sold to existing Ferrari owners; over half of clients own multiple Ferraris. SP3 Daytona limited to 599 units at $2M-plus was pre-allocated to top collectors before announcement. Purosangue SUV capped below 20% of output despite industry SUV share above 50% at Porsche and Lamborghini. Typical options add $50,000–70,000; Tailor Made customization launched just over 10 years ago with upside Lum calls underexplored.

> **F3** Formula One is the oldest and only ever-present F1 team — integrated at Maranello, ≈10% of revenue, treated as marketing not split profitability. Secondary market: Ferrari dominates ≈20 of the top 30 auction sale prices; cars depreciate 7–10 years then stabilize with company-certified Classiche support. Hybrid 296 GTB/GTS drove >50% of recent-quarter shipments; pure EV committed for 2025; ≈40% of 296 buyers are new-to-Ferrari clients under age 40.

## Mental Model · *Collector Pyramid With Combinatorial Luxury Moat*

> **Components**
>
> Ferrari manages a hierarchy from first-time buyers through collectors to top-tier clients offered limited editions — scarcity enforced by saying no to wealthy buyers. Revenue drivers resemble subscription logic: repeated purchases build profile for allocation rights, not one-off transactions. Brand awareness vastly exceeds client count (Lum cites toddlers singing about Ferraris); F1 supplies global storytelling without traditional ad spend. Manufacturing is artisanal — low automation, V12 in the Purosangue SUV as a deliberate statement — optimizing product over process scalability. Three factors in combination (heritage, craft, relationships) beat peers who excel at one or two.
>
> **Application**
>
> Lum says discard the "car company" label and model lifetime collector value: expand the pyramid base (296 hybrid, Roma, fashion) while stretching the top (Tailor Made, track-only XX programs, F1-car experiences). Watch volume discipline (≈13,000 rising slowly from Montezemolo's 7,000 cap), personalization mix driving margin beats, electrification preserving occasion and sound, and Exor family long-term governance under chairman John Elkann. Porsche is Rolex (volume, precision); Ferrari is Patek Philippe (pinnacle, combinatorial edge).

## Key Insights

> **1.** Ferrari's edge is combinatorial, not a single magic bullet.
>
> **Q** What makes Ferrari different from Lamborghini or Porsche?
>
> **A** Lum says niche makers lack heritage and client depth; Porsche balances volume (SUVs pay for the rest) like Rolex; Ferrari alone combines F1 racing DNA, 75 years of storytelling, artisan manufacturing, and a gated collector base. No one factor is unreplicable — the combination over decades is.

> **2.** Scarcity is an active management choice, not passive supply constraint.
>
> **Q** How does Ferrari choose who buys?
>
> **A** New buyers build profile toward collector tiers; limited editions like the SP3 Daytona (599 units, $2M-plus) go only to top clients with proven ownership history — some collectors resented needing permission to spend $3 million. Purosangue held under 20% of output while peers derive 50%+ from SUVs; exclusivity means saying no to beautiful demand.

> **3.** Electrification may widen Ferrari's contrast, not erase it.
>
> **Q** Does EV threaten the moat?
>
> **A** Lum compares quartz-crisis watches: when utility commoditized timekeeping, luxury doubled down on useless mechanical complexity. Ferrari focuses hybrids (296 >50% of shipments) and a 2025 EV with engineered sound signature — transportation is not the point; low annual mileage means environmental payback on batteries may never arrive, so performance-led electrification fits.

## Chronology

> *Ferrari*
>
> **1929** Enzo Ferrari founds Scuderia Ferrari as a race team; road cars begin after WWII in 1947 when Enzo is nearly 50.
>
> **1947–1980s** Mid-engine lineage and supercars (GTO, F40) build bedroom-wall brand equity; Enzo's marketing genius includes artificial waitlists.
>
> **2015** Ferrari IPO after Fiat Chrysler spin; Baillie Gifford invests directly; Montezemolo-era ≈7,000-unit cap gives way to controlled growth.
>
> **2021–2022** Benedetto Vigna (ex-STMicro physicist, 100+ patents) becomes CEO; Purosangue launches with V12; ≈13,000 deliveries.
>
> **2023** 296 hybrid drives >50% of shipments and ≈40% new clients under 40; Lum breakdown on Business Breakdowns EP.140; EV targeted 2025.
