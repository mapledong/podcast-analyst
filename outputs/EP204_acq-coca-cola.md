## Background

> Ben and David frame Coca-Cola through Charlie Munger's thought experiment: build a non-alcoholic beverage company from $2M to $2T with massive dividends along the way. Episode covers Pemberton's 1886 precursor, Candler's $2,300 full acquisition (1891), mistaken 1899 $1-per-gallon bottling contract sold for $1, Woodruff's WWII expansion (64 portable plants, 5–10B bottles to troops), Pepsi's 1975 Challenge, New Coke (1985, ≈3% share after Classic), and Buffett's ≈$1.3B stake now ≈9.5% worth ≈$28B. Modern Coca-Cola Company: $47B revenue, ≈$10.6B net income (23% margins), 60% gross margins on concentrate. Thirty $1B+ brands serve 2.2B daily servings in 65B global beverage market. US share fell from ≈60% (1948) toward Pepsi parity.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Coca-Cola serves 2.2 billion beverage servings daily from 200 bottling partners operating 950 facilities. Coca-Cola Company reports $47B revenue and ≈$10.6B net income (23% margin) on concentrate; total system generates ≈$175B revenue at ≈$300B market cap — syrup economics, not finished drinks.

> **F2** Asa Candler acquired full Coca-Cola ownership for $2,300 (1891). First official year (1892): ≈$20K production spend, ≈$10K advertising against ≈$250K marketplace gross revenue at soda fountains charging $6.40/gallon — 80% fountain margins drove drugstore adoption before bottling scaled.

> **F3** 1899 $1-per-gallon perpetual bottling contract sold for $1 locked syrup pricing for decades without inflation adjustment — nearly destroyed concentrate economics until renegotiation. By 1948 Coca-Cola held ≈60% US soft drink share; Pepsi's 1975 Challenge eroded share toward competitive parity.

> **F4** WWII: Woodruff pledged Coke at 5¢ for every serviceman; 64 portable bottling plants shipped to Europe, Asia, North Africa; 5–10 billion bottles reached troops who returned with brand loyalty. Diet Coke became #1 diet drink by end of 1983 first full year; New Coke (1985) retained ≈3% share after Classic relaunch despite winning taste tests.

> **F5** Buffett's Berkshire bought ≈$1.3B stake (1988–89), now ≈9.5% worth ≈$28B — 22x gross but ≈8% IRR versus S&P ≈11% with dividends. Berkshire receives ≈$800M–$1B annual dividends (≈60–80% yield on original cost). Munger's $2M-to-$2T thought experiment: dividends flowed but 3–4% post-1998 growth caps reaching $2T by 2036 150th birthday — thirty $1B+ brands serve 2.2B daily servings in 65B global beverage market.

## Mental Model · *Asset-Light Brand Franchise*

> **Components**
>
> Coca-Cola sells branded concentrate, not finished drinks — bottlers provide capital, logistics, and local relationships while the company maintains 60% gross margins on syrup. Fountain sales (80% retailer margins at $6.40/gallon charging drugstores) created initial adoption; bottling scaled distribution but the 1899 $1-per-gallon perpetual contract sold for $1 nearly broke economics until renegotiation. Marketing invests in lifestyle association (happiness, Americana, Santa Claus) not ingredient claims — blind tests favored Pepsi yet Coke retained share for decades. Portfolio: 30 $1B+ brands (15 organic, 3 acquired at scale, 12 grown from small) across 2.2B daily servings in a 65B global beverage market. Munger's $2M-to-$2T thought experiment: dividends flowed but 3–4% post-1998 growth caps Lollapalooza outcome.
>
> **Application**
>
> Separate brand ownership from capital deployment when scaling physical goods globally — franchise terms must inflation-adjust (1899 $1/gallon lesson). For investors, KO is dividend compounder with mature 3–4% growth, not Munger Lollapalooza — brand moat intact but category constrained. US share fell from ≈60% (1948) toward Pepsi parity after 1975 Challenge. Fountain 80% retailer margins aligned early drugstore adoption; bottlers deploy capital while Coca-Cola Company keeps 60% concentrate gross margins on $47B revenue.

> Coca-Cola's moat is brand plus distribution architecture spanning 139 years and ≈$300B market cap on ≈$47B concentrate revenue (≈$175B system-wide). Secret formula mythology, contour bottle (99% American recognition by 1940s), and red identity create habit switching costs — New Coke (1985) failed despite winning taste tests because identity trumped palate (≈3% share retained). Asset-light model: 200 bottling partners, 950 facilities, 60% gross margins on concentrate. Fountain channel earned drugstores 80% margins at $6.40/gallon in 1892. WWII planted global infrastructure via 64 portable plants and 5–10B bottles to troops at 5¢ pledge. Woodruff marketing mastery; Goizueta-era diversification (Minute Maid, Monster 20% stake, $4B Glaceau). Weaknesses: sugar/health headwinds, Pepsi parity, Buffett ≈8% IRR vs S&P ≈11%, plastic environmental concerns. Munger frame captures dividends; reaching $2T by 2036 150th birthday impossible at 3–4% growth. Thirty $1B+ brands reduce single-product sugar exposure — 15 created organically, 3 acquired at scale, 12 grown from small — serving 2.2B daily servings in 65B global beverage market. Diet Coke became #1 diet drink in America by end of 1983 first full year — portfolio diversification beyond core cola reduces single-product sugar exposure in developed markets. PepsiCo Frito-Lay diversification cited as growth edge Coca-Cola lacks in episode investor framing. ## Key Insights

> **1.** The bottling contract almost killed the golden goose.
>
> **Q** What was the $1/gallon mistake?
>
> **A** Candler sold bottling rights 1899 for $1 with perpetual $1/gallon syrup pricing — no inflation adjustment. As costs rose, economics broke until renegotiation. Fountain model was genius: drugstores earned 80% margins at $6.40/gallon charging while Coca-Cola spent ≈$10K advertising against ≈$250K gross marketplace revenue in 1892 — but fixed bottling contract nearly destroyed concentrate profitability for decades.

> **2.** Brand beats taste in blind tests.
>
> **Q** Why did New Coke fail despite winning taste tests?
>
> **A** Pepsi Challenge (1975–85) showed consumers preferred Pepsi in blind tests; Coke share declined annually toward parity from ≈60% US share in 1948. New Coke (1985) tested better but abandoned 100 years of brand identity — Classic relaunch saved company while New Coke retained ≈3% share. Identity and habit trump palate when contour bottle had 99% American recognition by 1940s.

> **3.** WWII was Coca-Cola's global distribution event.
>
> **Q** How did a US soda become worldwide?
>
> **A** Woodruff pledged Coke at 5¢ for every serviceman; 64 portable bottling plants shipped to Europe, Asia, and North Africa. 5–10 billion bottles reached troops who returned home with brand loyalty. The US government welcomed leaving an American symbol worldwide. Seeded bottling infrastructure and consumer habit competitors could not replicate quickly; combined with fountain 80% retailer margins driving early US drugstore adoption.

> **4.** Buffett's KO stake is a dividend miracle, not IRR triumph.
>
> **Q** Was Berkshire's Coca-Cola investment great?
>
> **A** ≈$1.3B invested (1988–89), now ≈9.5% worth ≈$28B — 22x gross but ≈8% IRR versus S&P ≈11% with dividends included. However ≈$800M–$1B annual dividends equal 60–80% yield on original cost — Munger's Lollapalooza frame captures dividend miracle even when IRR frame shows mature-brand limits at 3–4% post-1998 growth on ≈$300B market cap.

> **5.** Munger's $2T thought experiment reveals the ceiling.
>
> **Q** Why isn't Coca-Cola worth $2T?
>
> **A** At ≈$300B market cap with 3–4% post-1998 growth, reaching $2T by 2036 150th birthday requires impossible compounding. Company executed Munger playbook — global brand, 200 bottling partners, 950 facilities, 2.2B daily servings in 65B global beverage market, massive dividends (≈$800M–$1B annually to Berkshire alone) — but category saturation and sugar headwinds cap upside. Pre-1998 history is remarkable era; post-1998 is dividend compounder story, not Lollapalooza Charlie imagined from $2M starting capital.
