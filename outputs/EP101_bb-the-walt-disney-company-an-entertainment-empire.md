## Background

> Jesse Pujji breaks down Disney with Ben Weiss, CIO of 8th & Jackson, on the company's 100th anniversary. FY2022 revenue was $82.7 billion with $12 billion of EBITDA and $3 billion of net income across parks (≈$29 billion revenue, ≈$8 billion segment operating income), linear networks including ABC and ESPN (≈$28 billion, ≈$8 billion profit), streaming (≈$19 billion revenue, ≈$4 billion of losses), and box office (≈$5 billion). Weiss frames Disney as emotional IP compounded through a flywheel — stories feed parks, merchandise, cable carriage, and subscriptions — while warning that managing linear decline and streaming investment simultaneously is uniquely difficult.

## Key Facts

> **F1** FY2022 Disney generated $82.7 billion of revenue, roughly $12 billion of EBITDA, and $3 billion of net income. The two largest profit pools are parks (≈$29 billion revenue, ≈$8 billion segment operating income, ≈$3.4 billion of 2022 CapEx or ≈12% of sales) and linear networks (≈$28 billion revenue, ≈$8 billion segment profit with historical ≈40% margins). ESPN alone commanded an estimated ≈$70 per-subscriber monthly affiliate fee across roughly 100 million U.S. cable households — often bundled whether households wanted sports or not.

> **F2** Streaming is scale without economics yet: ≈$19 billion of revenue and roughly $4 billion of operating losses in FY2022 as Disney invested in tech infrastructure and content ahead of subscribers. Disney+ had ≈46.6 million domestic and ≈160 million global subscribers (including India Hotstar from Fox); combined with Hulu and ESPN+, Disney's streaming footprint exceeds Netflix's ≈231 million subs, but with lower ARPU, lower engagement, and no profit versus Netflix's improving profitability.

> **F3** The Cars (2006) case study illustrates the flywheel: ≈$120 million production plus ≈$120 million marketing (≈$200 million total), $244 million domestic and $460 million global box office, $314 million in DVD/Blu-ray, $1.4 billion cumulative franchise box office, over $10 billion of reported retail merchandise, and roughly $1.1 billion invested in Cars Land at Disneyland. Marvel — acquired in 2009 — has since generated over $20 billion of global box office and anchors both parks attractions and Disney+ programming; Weiss argues it is worth multiples of the purchase price.

## Mental Model · *Storytelling Flywheel, Four Profit Engines*

> **Components**
>
> Disney starts with characters and worlds that build emotional connections transcending geography and generation — a capability Weiss argues is nearly impossible to replicate at scale. That IP feeds four monetization layers: high-margin parks (pricing power, fixed-cost leverage, limited global competitors), licensing and consumer products (royalty collections with strong retail shelf space), linear networks (ESPN and cable bundle economics with ≈40% margins at peak), and theatrical plus downstream licensing. Acquisitions under Michael Eisner (ABC/Cap Cities 1996–97, bringing ESPN) and Bob Iger (Pixar 2006, Marvel 2009, Lucasfilm 2012) bolted new IP onto the same distribution rails rather than diversifying away from storytelling.
>
> **Application**
>
> Separate durable IP from the distribution transition. Parks and merchandise stay high-return if stories resonate — new Star Wars and Avatar lands drive repeat visits. The bear case is organizational: ≈$8 billion of declining cable cash funds streaming losses while activists push near-term profit. Weiss's bull math — 200M households at $15/month equals ≈$36B revenue and ≈$14B EBITDA at cable-like margins, potentially ≈$250B EV for streaming alone — requires investing through the cycle and eventually walling premium IP behind a Disney paywall.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Movies drive the flywheel; parks and cable pay the bills.
>
> **Q** Why are parks and linear networks the largest segments if films define the brand?
>
> **A** Theatrical releases create IP that monetizes everywhere else, but measured revenue concentrates in parks (≈$29 billion) and cable (≈$28 billion) because those businesses scale fixed storytelling investment across tickets, merchandise, affiliate fees, and advertising. Box office (≈$5 billion) is meaningful but the downstream licensing margin is the real economic engine.

> **2.** Streaming wins on IP, loses on unit economics.
>
> **Q** How does Disney+ compare to Netflix?
>
> **A** Disney exceeds Netflix on combined streaming subs but trails on ARPU, engagement, and profit. Losses reflect tech build-out and front-loaded Marvel and Star Wars content. Weiss sees a path to ≈200M households at ≈$15/month but questions whether activists will let management invest long enough.

> **3.** Leadership must serve creatives, shareholders, and governments.
>
> **Q** What makes running Disney uniquely hard?
>
> **A** Weiss compares it to running a small country: CEOs must nurture creative risk-taking ($150–250 million bets per film), balance profitability with growth for dividend-focused shareholders, manage a massive employee base, and navigate political relationships (Florida zoning disputes). Bob Iger's November return after Bob Chapek's tenure reflects board concern over streaming losses and reported creative-community alienation; Iger prioritized refocusing on creative excellence.

## Investment Ideas

> **1. DIS** · 🟢 LONG · ●●○ Medium
>
> Weiss sees more opportunity than risk if Disney commits to profitable Disney+ at ≈200M households × $15/month (≈$36B revenue, ≈$14B EBITDA, ≈$250B EV for streaming alone) while parks and Marvel/IP compound. Medium confidence reflects innovator's-dilemma risk as cable profits decline and activists cap streaming investment.

## Chronology

> *The Walt Disney Company*
>
> **1923** Walt and Roy Disney found the Disney Brothers Cartoon Studio.
>
> **1928** Mickey Mouse debuts, becoming the anchor of the character canon.
>
> **1937** Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs — first full-length animated feature.
>
> **1955** Disneyland opens in Anaheim, launching the parks franchise.
>
> **1996** Michael Eisner's ABC/Cap Cities acquisition brings ESPN into Disney.
>
> **2006** Bob Iger acquires Pixar; Cars releases, spawning a decade-plus franchise.
>
> **2009** Disney buys Marvel Entertainment for comic-book IP that becomes a $20B+ box-office engine.
>
> **2012** Lucasfilm acquisition adds Star Wars to parks, merchandise, and streaming.
>
> **2022** Bob Chapek exits; Bob Iger returns as CEO for a stated two-year transition focused on streaming and creative talent.
