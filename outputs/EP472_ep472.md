## Background

> Krishna Rao explains Anthropic from a CFO perspective where capital planning and technical architecture are inseparable. He repeatedly describes compute as the central planning variable because demand can outstrip supply long before customer willingness to pay disappears. The discussion connects procurement, software stack design, and sales quality: Anthropic uses multiple chip families, then relies on internal orchestration to route workloads across model training, inference, and internal usage. Rao argues this flexibility improved over several years and now acts like a structural margin advantage. ## Key Facts

> **F1** Rao says compute is the business lifeblood and estimates he still spends roughly 30-40% of his time on it. His explicit tradeoff is existential: over-buying compute can impair the balance sheet, while under-buying means Anthropic cannot serve current demand or keep pace at the frontier. He also places current growth in context by citing step-changes in run-rate revenue, from the low hundreds of millions when he joined to around $1B by Series E, then from roughly $9B at the start of the year to above $30B by quarter-end.

> **F2** Anthropic runs across 3 hardware families - Nvidia GPUs, Google TPUs, and Amazon Trainium - and Rao says the abstraction/orchestration layer took multiple years to build. That architecture allows workloads to move across hardware classes as cost and availability shift, which he presents as a direct contributor to better dollar efficiency and resilience during supply volatility.

> **F3** He gives concrete scale markers: around $250M run-rate when he joined, close to $1B by the Series E period, about $9B at the start of the year, and north of $30B by quarter-end. He also cites roughly $75B raised since he joined, plus another ≈$50B of future commitment tied to Amazon and Google relationships. He repeatedly describes planning under a one- to two-year cone of uncertainty, where the cost of being late on capacity can exceed the cost of carrying reserve headroom when demand inflects quickly.

## Mental Model · *Compute Portfolio Under Uncertainty*

> **Components**
>
> First, forecast demand with a one- to two-year cone of uncertainty rather than point estimates. Second, secure heterogeneous supply across at least three chip ecosystems to reduce single-vendor fragility. Third, build orchestration software that can dynamically reallocate workloads between inference, training, and internal usage. Fourth, pair frontier capability with trust signals (safety and interpretability) so enterprise demand is durable, not purely experimental.
>
> **Application**
>
> Rao applies this by treating compute commitments as portfolio decisions: reserve enough capacity for upside scenarios, but preserve flexibility so chips are not stranded if usage mix shifts. Commercial teams then prioritize high-value enterprise deployments, while infrastructure teams continuously improve chip-level efficiency. The model converts technical optionality into financial resilience, helping Anthropic scale from single-digit billions to tens of billions in run-rate revenue without relying on one hardware path.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Compute is a strategic asset, not overhead.
>
> **Q** Why do traditional SaaS lenses miss Anthropic?
>
> **A** Rao says investors often want a clean split between R&D and serving costs, but the same compute pool can serve different purposes during the same planning window. Because allocation shifts with demand and model cycles, returns come from utilization quality, not static accounting buckets. His framing implies that superior scheduling, compiler work, and chip flexibility can matter as much as headline model benchmarks in determining economics.

> **2.** Heterogeneous chips create real option value.
>
> **Q** What is gained from Trainium, TPU, and GPU diversity?
>
> **A** Using three chip families improves procurement leverage and reduces single-supplier dependence during constrained periods. Rao also argues that being forced to run across different architectures pushed Anthropic to build deeper orchestration and compiler capabilities, which improved price-performance over time. In effect, hardware diversity is not only a risk hedge; it becomes an operating advantage when demand surges faster than supply contracts can be rewritten.

> **3.** Safety investments convert into enterprise revenue.
>
> **Q** How do mission and monetization align?
>
> **A** Rao positions safety and interpretability as both mission commitments and commercial differentiators. He points to adoption among nine of the Fortune 10 as evidence that trust matters when customers deploy AI into sensitive workflows. The argument is that frontier performance alone is insufficient at enterprise scale; buyers also underwrite governance, reliability, and partner behavior over multi-year relationships.
