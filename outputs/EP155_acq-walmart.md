## Background

> Season 11 premiere — deliberate precursor to Amazon.com episode because Bezos copied Walton (Made in America marked up, Saturday meetings, competitor shopping, EDLP psychology). Sam born Kingfisher, Oklahoma (1918); Depression foreclosures shaped frugality. Ben Franklin franchise Newport betrayal forced move to Bentonville. First self-service Five and Dime (1950); Walmart name July 2, 1962 Rogers, AR — same year as Kmart, Target, Woolco. Sam flew prop planes sideways over towns to pick store sites before $24M satellite network (1987) linked real-time sales. IPO 1970: 77% first-year post-IPO revenue growth; 1970s 40.1% CAGR, 1980s 32.4% CAGR. Passed Sears 1990; crushed Kmart (bankruptcy 2002). Sam died 1992; Supercenters scaled after Brazil Carrefour trip. Today: 10,500 stores, 24 countries, 230M weekly customers, ≈2.3M employees, largest US grocer by 2×+ over Kroger at 55% of company revenue (≈$300B grocery line).

## Key Facts

> **F1** Walton family retains just over 50% ownership ≈60 years after founding via Walton Enterprises partnership — individual family members cannot sell shares without collective consent, preserving long-term EDLP time horizon versus quarterly margin extraction by public markets.

> **F2** Approximately 90% of Americans live within 10 miles of a Walmart; exceptions include San Francisco, Seattle, Boston, and Manhattan (nearest New Jersey store technically under 10 miles but not 'in spirit' urban). Company operates 10,500 stores across 24 countries serving 230M weekly customers with ≈2.3M employees worldwide.

> **F3** Grocery accounts for 55% of Walmart revenue — over $300 billion annually — with 20%+ US grocery share versus Kroger under 10%. Sam saw Carrefour hypermarkets in Brazil; Supercenter format scaled after his 1992 death, averaging ≈15% grocery savings versus traditional grocers for middle and lower income families.

> **F4** Walmart invested $24 million in proprietary satellite network (1987) linking stores for two-way voice/data and one-way video when commercial bandwidth could not support real-time sales data — Ron Mayer built computerized distribution centers after Sam's 1966 IBM Poughkeepsie seminar at age ≈48.

> **F5** Ecommerce ≈13% of revenue (≈$75 billion) with 37% online growth in 2019 versus ≈2% overall company growth. Kosmix acquisition ≈$300M (2011) brought Amazon/Junglee alumni → Walmart Labs; Flipkart ≈77% for $16B+ (2018). IPO 1970: 77% first-year post-IPO revenue growth; 1970s 40.1% CAGR, 1980s 32.4% CAGR. Sam's Club ≈$75B revenue versus Costco ≈$217B — one in three US households had Sam's membership in 1990s per episode.

## Mental Model · *Everyday Low Prices via Owned Logistics*

> **Components**
>
> Sam's discounting insight: cut markup, triple unit sales, earn more profit dollars (80¢ cost sold at $1.00 beats $1.20 price per Made in America). Eliminate Butler Brothers' 25% markups by owning distribution centers, trucking, forecasting — don't buy anyone else's inefficiency. Small-town counter-positioning: Kmart/Target optimized existing urban supply chains; Walmart built hub-and-spoke DCs one day's drive from store clusters. EDLP psychology removes promotion whipsaw. Walton Enterprises partnership forbids individual share sales — ≈50%+ family control ≈60 years. Supercenter adds low-margin grocery (≈15% cheaper than traditional grocers) feeding hard-goods flywheel — now 55% of revenue (≈$300B grocery line). Sam flew prop planes sideways over towns scouting sites; shopped competitors relentlessly — look at what they do right and steal it.
>
> **Application**
>
> When incumbents inherit infrastructure tuned to old retail model, greenfield builders accepting short-term capital pain can counter-position — DoorDash suburbs parallel in delivery. Technology (IBM 1966 seminar, $24M satellites 1987) is margin weapon when commercial tools do not exist. In mature oligopoly, scale economies make lower prices rational — competitors cannot match without rebuilding logistics. Bezos 'beer tastes better' rule fails when best-in-class logistics does not exist — building it IS the product. Sam's competitor-shopping ethic — look at what they do right and steal it — predates modern customer-obsession rhetoric.

Maturity: scale economies — largest SKU purchaser, hub-and-spoke DC network, private fleet, $24M satellite network (1987). Grocery Supercenters: 55% of revenue (≈$300B+), 20%+ US grocery share versus Kroger under 10%. ≈90% of Americans within 10 miles; Walton family ≈50%+ via Walton Enterprises. Ecommerce ≈13% (≈$75B), 37% online growth 2019 versus ≈2% total; Kosmix $300M (2011), Flipkart $16B+ (2018). IPO 1970: 77% first-year post-IPO growth; 1970s 40.1% CAGR. Weaknesses: Sam's Club (≈$75B) vs Costco (≈$217B) despite parent scale — one in three US households had Sam's membership in 1990s but declined; Costco gross margin ≈13% versus Walmart ≈24% shelf margin. International execution mixed. Growth ≈3% since ≈2013 on world's largest revenue base. Amazon closes revenue gap though AWS distorts comparison — castle largely defended as world's largest employer and grocer Albertsons and Costco tied at 5% each per episode grocery share breakdown. International execution mixed; growth stalled near ≈3% on revenue since ≈2013 on world's largest base. Bezos copied Walton Made in America playbook — episode deliberately precedes Amazon.com history as competitive parallel per hosts.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Inherited logistics looked smart until discount era.
>
> **Q** Why did Kmart lose with head start?
>
> **A** Inherited logistics looked smart until discount era arrived. Kresge/Kmart leveraged existing warehouses tuned to old variety-store model with 33%+ gross margins — worked until Walmart's self-built hub-and-spoke DC network optimized for EDLP in small towns Kmart ignored. Kmart's 1980s acquisition spree (Sports Authority, Borders, OfficeMax) distracted while Walmart compounded same-year 1962 launch cohort. Bankruptcy 2002; Sears merger 2004; combined bankruptcy 2018 — Walmart killed them through logistics counter-positioning, not Amazon alone initially.

> **2.** Supercenter reinvented Walmart after Sam's death.
>
> **Q** Where did 55% grocery revenue come from?
>
> **A** Sam saw Carrefour hypermarkets in Brazil; Hypermart USA (3 stores) too big; Supercenter format succeeded post-1992 death. Grocery lowest margin but highest frequency — ≈15% average savings versus traditional grocers matters hundreds of dollars monthly to middle/lower income families. Grocery now 55% of ≈$600B+ Walmart revenue (≈$300B grocery line alone); Kroger #2 under 10% share — 2×+ gap making Walmart largest US grocer by wide margin.

> **3.** Technology was strategic from 1966 IBM seminar.
>
> **Q** Was Walmart a tech company?
>
> **A** Sam enrolled in IBM Poughkeepsie seminar age ≈48 (1966); Ron Mayer built computerized distribution centers and $24M satellite network (1987) for real-time sales when commercial bandwidth insufficient — hosts call it start of Walmart becoming a technology company. First big US retailer treating computing as core operating paradigm, not back-office cost center — predating modern ecommerce fulfillment center logic by decades.

> **4.** Ecommerce nearly missed but $75B line exists.
>
> **Q** How did Walmart respond to Amazon?
>
> **A** Slow until ≈$300M Kosmix acquisition (2011) brought Amazon/Junglee alumni → Walmart Labs. Mark Lore/Jet ≈$1B pre-launch bet mostly failed and was shut down; Doug McMillon credited Lore for ecommerce jumpstart anyway. 2019 ecommerce +37% vs company +2%; ≈13% revenue mix (≈$75B), profitability debated. Walmart poached Amazon execs while Amazon hired Rick Dalzel from Walmart — talent flowed both directions. Walmart+ membership competes with Amazon Prime as omnichannel integration play.

> **5.** Walton partnership preserved EDLP time horizon.
>
> **Q** Why still family-controlled at 50%+?
>
> **A** Walton Enterprises partnership requires family collective decision to sell — prevented individual liquidity events forcing margin extraction. Enabled decades of EDLP price investment versus public quarterly pressure on world's largest retailer by revenue. ≈50%+ family ownership ≈60 years later via structure blocking individual share sales; third-generation Waltons own sports franchises — tension rises between winning on price versus extracting margin from world's largest grocer and employer with ≈2.3M employees.
