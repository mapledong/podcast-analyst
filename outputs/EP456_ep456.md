## Background

> Gokul Rajaram has built and led core product and revenue systems at Google, Facebook, Square, DoorDash, and Coinbase, and has invested in roughly 700 companies. He uses those operating reps to explain how product work changes when agents can code, test, and execute. This is a practical product conversation: evals, customer behavior, systems of record, North Star metrics, work projects in hiring, and why self-serve distribution can sneak into large enterprises before sales teams even notice.

## Mental Model · *Outcome First Product Judgment*

> **Components**
>
> Start from the customer outcome and define how success will be evaluated before building. Favor products that can onboard and deliver delight without human mediation, because self-serve quality compounds distribution efficiency. Identify where incumbents hold system-of-record advantages and data access control, then decide whether to integrate, displace, or build a new record layer. Keep teams close to real usage evidence and fast iteration. The future-proof skill is judgment, not prompt volume. What matters when anyone can build more software? Rajaram worries about AI slop. In an era when teams can make almost anything, the harder question is which things should be made, what outcome they create, and whether an eval proves the product is good enough to ship.
>
> **Application**
>
> Rajaram applies this in both operating and investing contexts: he values products with explicit North Star metrics tied to customer value, and he pressure-tests whether pricing aligns with delivered outcomes. He also looks for bottom-up adoption patterns where teams adopt tools before executive mandates, as seen in cases like Figma and newer AI developer products entering enterprises through daily utility.

## Key Insights

> **1.** The future-proof skill is judgment, not prompt volume.
>
> **Q** What matters when anyone can build more software?
>
> **A** Rajaram worries about AI slop. In an era when teams can make almost anything, the harder question is which things should be made, what outcome they create, and whether an eval proves the product is good enough to ship. Rajaram says product roles were clearly split 5-10 years ago between PMs, designers, and engineers, but long-horizon agents now let technical and non-technical people build working products in hours.

> **2.** Systems of record can fight back against agents by controlling data.
>
> **Q** Why are incumbents not automatically dead?
>
> **A** Slack, Salesforce, Epic, and other workflow systems hold the data agents need. Rajaram notes Slack cutting off Glean and APIs moving toward prices like $2 per call, which can force agent companies to build systems of record too. He distinguishes exposed seat-based software from more durable workflow systems. A $20-$30 per seat customer-service product may need to move toward $1, $0.50, or $0.20 per resolved ticket as AI changes the unit of value.

> **3.** Self-serve is both a product discipline and a distribution weapon.
>
> **Q** Why does self-serve matter beyond lower CAC?
>
> **A** If a customer can onboard without talking to an employee, the product must deliver a fast moment of delight. It can also infiltrate enterprises from the bottom up, as Figma did after a mid-level design manager brought it in despite a failed top-down push. He cites concrete product lessons: Gmail offered 100x Hotmail's 10MB storage, Square accepted 95% of applicants by moving risk to the transaction level, and self-serve products can reach millions of customers versus ≈10,000 through a 100-person sales force.
