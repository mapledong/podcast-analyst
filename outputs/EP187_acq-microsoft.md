## Key Facts

> **F1** April 4, 1975: Micro-Soft founded in Albuquerque after Popular Electronics Altair cover; Bill and Paul demoed BASIC on Harvard PDP-10 emulator without finished product — second try saved the MITS meeting. MITS exclusive deal capped lifetime BASIC revenue at $180,000; Bill Sr.'s arbitration clause helped win November 1977 release to OEM license broadly.

> **F2** Bill priced 8080 BASIC ≈$30 per machine to OEMs — Apple paid $31,000 for standard versus finishing Woz's incomplete BASIC. 1975 revenue $16,000 from $30-per-copy Altair sales; 1977 ended $381,000 after zero revenue first 11 months post-arbitration; 1978 $1.3M with 13 employees; 1979 $2.4M before Seattle move for UW recruiting.

> **F3** IBM PC (1981): Project Chess team ≈30 people in Boca Raton — smaller than Microsoft's deal team. Gary Kildall/Digital Research missed IBM meeting flying his plane; Microsoft bought QDOS from Seattle Computer Products (≈$50K–$75K), customized PC-DOS, retained non-exclusive MS-DOS OEM rights — IBM marketed PC, Microsoft captured clone wave.

> **F4** Altair sold ≈$397 (≈$2,300 in 2024 dollars) versus DEC mini-computer ≈$120,000; Intel 8080 list price $360, Ed Roberts negotiated volume deal ≈$75 per chip enabling affordable kits. Bill born 1955 same year as Steve Jobs — timing mattered as much as strategy; Lakeside teletype 1968; Paul saw Moore's Law before the phrase existed.

> **F5** IBM antitrust 1968 cracked bundled mainframe software — created opening for independent software vendors. Partnership split 60/40 then 64/36 Bill-led; Kay Nishi cold-called for Japan partnership building ≈50% international revenue early. Open Letter to Hobbyists (1975) argued software labor deserves pay before copyright protection clear (1980) — piracy on unbundled Altair BASIC threatened the model.

## Mental Model · *Positive Spiral Platform Pricing*

> **Components**
>
> Microsoft's early insight: software is zero marginal cost with high fixed R&D — price for adoption, not margin maximization, to become the standard. MITS mistake taught exclusive caps ($180K lifetime) and piracy risk when hardware OEM does not bundle payment. OEM per-machine ≈$30 royalty aligns incentives. IBM deal non-exclusivity lets Microsoft capture clone wave (Compaq) IBM enabled. Languages (BASIC, Fortran, COBOL) plus DOS integrate point of industry control. International early (Kay Nishi Japan ≈50% revenue) expands spiral. Bill's competitive drive plus Paul/Moore insight plus Mary/Bill Sr. legal-network access compound luck (Altair cover timing, Kildall miss). NCR, GE, Control Data approached 1976 with ≈$100K deals each — arbitration unlock changed economics.
>
> **Application**
>
> Platform startups: underprice to become default if marginal cost near zero and switching costs rise with adoption — but avoid exclusive caps that trap you (MITS $180K lesson). Partner with giants (IBM) only if you retain rights to sell same product to their competitors. Bill priced 8080 BASIC ≈$30 flat — Apple paid $31,000 for standard versus finishing Woz's unfinished BASIC. Legal protection for software (1980) lagged invention — business model innovation preceded IP regime.

## Key Insights

> **1.** IBM transferred dominance to Microsoft.
>
> **Q** Why is the IBM deal the splinter?
>
> **A** Project Chess needed 16-bit OS fast; Kildall missed meeting flying his plane; Microsoft bought QDOS cheap (≈$50K–$75K), customized PC-DOS, retained MS-DOS OEM rights — IBM's brand created demand Microsoft captured on all clones including Compaq. Project Chess team ≈30 people; irony: project named Chess while Gates played multi-level game. IBM shipped PC in a year, revolutionized industry, captured little software value — non-exclusive license transferred dominance.

> **2.** MITS taught royalty economics.
>
> **Q** Why an open letter to hobbyists?
>
> **A** 1975 piracy on Altair because software not bundled with hardware — Bill's Open Letter argued labor deserves pay before copyright clear (1980). Exclusive MITS deal capped lifetime BASIC revenue at $180,000 — Ed Roberts had upper hand. Bill Sr. arbitration clause freed OEM model Nov 1977; Microsoft ended 1977 with $381K revenue after zero first 11 months. Per-machine ≈$30 fee aligns payment with each sale.

> **3.** Price to starve rivals, not maximize.
>
> **Q** Why $30 BASIC to Apple?
>
> **A** Bill priced 8080 BASIC ≈$30 flat to every OEM — positive spiral: cheap enough every OEM buys Microsoft standard; volume funds R&D lead. Apple paid $31,000 versus hiring to finish Woz's BASIC. Requires low overhead (Bill/Paul coding) and patience; deliberately underprices versus value-maximizing when marginal cost near zero and switching costs rise with installed base.

> **4.** Timing beat intelligence.
>
> **Q** Was Bill the smartest in the room?
>
> **A** Harvard Math 55 yielded Bill a B — peers one-in-a-million exist. Born 1955; Lakeside teletype 1968; Altair 1975 cover timing. Paul saw exponential chips in spring 1974 Electronics Magazine 8080 article (≈$200). Kay Nishi doubled international to ≈50% revenue early. Wave mattered as much as IQ — Bill shifted to applied math/applied computing when pure math had sharper peers.

> **5.** Software-only was counter-positioning.
>
> **Q** Why no Microsoft hardware?
>
> **A** Bill insisted microprocessor software North Star — Paul tempted by hardware (Traf-O-Data made ≈$20,000). While IBM integrated mainframes, Microsoft licensed bits with no inventory risk. Clone OEMs fought hardware wars at Altair $397 versus DEC $120,000; Microsoft collected DOS toll on all — counter-position versus IBM's integrated model.
