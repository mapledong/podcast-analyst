## Background

> Season 9 finale features Michael Ovitz live — rare long-form appearance — structured in medias res with Jurassic Park, then rewinding to Lew Wasserman's MCA/Universal era. Hosts contrast old Hollywood (studios owned talent as salaried employees; Jimmy Stewart's Winchester '73 pioneered gross participation via agent Lew) with CAA's packaging insight: own the scarce complete package (script + director + stars) and auction to fragmented, capital-constrained studios. Ovitz describes founding CAA from zero with raw ambition, partnership economics (agents eat what they kill at rivals; CAA partners shared upside), and systems supporting team selling (five people per meeting, archived deal memos, mailroom as talent pipeline). Extensions included corporate advisory (Coca-Cola with Roberto Goizueta/Don Keough), investment banking-style M&A (Sony, Matsushita/Universal), and generational sale to "Young Turks" before a brief Disney CEO stint. Hosts compare CAA to Acquired's community "packaging" startups in Slack. Transcript is Q&A-heavy — Ovitz's direct quotes are sparse but packaging math and Wasserman lineage are explicit.

## Mental Model · *Package the Scare Resource*

> **Components**
>
> Pre-CAA: studios controlled development; agencies represented individual talent for 10% of wages. Ovitz realized the bottleneck was the fully configured film product — scarce because stars, directors, and scripts were brokered separately. CAA internalized development: lunch Crichton weekly through block years, attach Spielberg via spouse call ("classic agent trick"), pre-sell before manuscript publication. Studios need fragmented buyers because CAA cannot finance $100M budgets — but sellers consolidate power. Lew Wasserman's arc (agent → MCA buying Universal + pre-1948 TV catalog) showed agents could become studios; Ovitz stayed agent-banker, taking obscene fees on Sony and Matsushita deals instead. Partnership LLC aligned incentives versus commission-only rivals.
>
> **Application**
>
> In two-sided markets where buyers (studios) need financing and sellers (talent) control attention, integrators who assemble bundles capture surplus — same logic as Rolled-up SaaS or investment-banking pitch books. Team-based coverage increases switching costs for clients (five advisors deep) but requires heavy internal coordination (CAA mailroom, archives). Founders selling partnerships should note CAA's value peaked with founder-operators; PE-backed WME scaled differently post-exit. Hosts compare CAA packaging to Acquired Slack community "packaging" startups — aggregation layer before platforms. Disney CEO stint and State Department speculation show Ovitz's dealmaker brand outlived agency role; Matsushita/Universal chain links to later UMG/Tencent Acquired episodes.

## What Makes It Work

> CAA's moat was counter-positioning against lone-wolf agencies (William Morris, ICM) — young, hungry partners sharing economics versus eat-what-you-kill silos. Process power in team packaging: signing Scorsese enabled signing more Scorseses; packaging made zero-sum casting positive-sum ("bigger pie for everybody"). Scale economies in information: deal archives, Coca-Cola's national account, and corporate M&A advisory diversified revenue beyond 10% commissions — cash-flowing through years-long film development gaps competitors couldn't survive. Branding: Ovitz as "most powerful man in Hollywood" for two decades; Variety ads announcing Sean Connery signings. Cornered resource was Ovitz's relationship graph (Spielberg, Crichton, Goizueta, Sony/Matsushita boards) — non-substitutable until exit. Weaknesses: cannot finance films (studio dependency); entrepreneur-to-employee transition failed at Disney; post-Ovitz CAA sold to PE while WME (Ari Emanuel) consolidated. Family-LLC structure (≈90% founder-owned) limited perpetual franchise — sold cheaply to successors relative to lifetime fees. Interview transcript captures mostly host questions — Ovitz's voice is sparse, but packaging economics and Lew Wasserman lineage are clear. Hosts draw line from Matsushita/Universal to later UMG/Tencent episodes and compare CAA's startup "packaging" in Acquired Slack to Ovitz's Hollywood packages. Disney stint and State Department speculation dealmaker reputation outliving agency CEO role. Season 9 finale format: medias res Jurassic Park hook, then rewind to Winchester '73 gross-deal precedent — teaching packaging before biography. WME consolidation (MMA, entertainment roll-up) is the counterfactual if Ovitz had stayed and raised institutional capital instead of ≈$200M generational sale.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Packaging turned Hollywood from studio-led development to agency-led SKUs.
>
> **Q** How did CAA differ from pre-1975 talent agencies?
>
> **A** Studios used to buy scripts, attach directors, cast actors — agencies only represented individuals. Ovitz copied Lew Wasserman's gross-deal innovation but industrialized it: combine elements in-house, sell one package to capital-constrained studios. Jurassic Park started pre-publication with Spielberg attached though he wasn't a client — agency as producer without balance sheet. Hosts compare to "they were the internet" — aggregation layer before platforms.

> **2.** Partnership economics funded patient investment in blocked clients.
>
> **Q** Why spend years lunching Michael Crichton with no output?
>
> **A** Agency model pays 10% only when talent earns — Crichton's writer's block meant zero commission yet Ovitz kept coaching because payoff asymmetry (Jurassic → ≈$1B franchise) justified portfolio approach. Rivals on commission-only systems couldn't cross-subsidize. CAA partners shared profits like an LLC — enabling "overpay to keep" talent and hire teams of five per meeting. Cash-flow challenge: no VC; survived on hit backends and corporate advisory fees.

> **3.** CAA exported Hollywood dealcraft to corporate America.
>
> **Q** Why pitch Coca-Cola with movie talent?
>
> **A** McCann-Erickson held Coke for decades; CAA showed strength by bringing A-list directors/actors to commercials — polar bear campaign at fraction of movie-star cost. Parallel to today’s creator-led brand deals. Ovitz met Roberto Goizueta and Don Keough (Berkshire episode tie-in) — treating ad pitches like studio packages. Staff thought it was hubris; it worked — money for clients, agency, and Coke.

> **4.** Seller power required fragmented buyers — until it didn't.
>
> **Q** What were Sony and Matsushita deals about?
>
> **A** When studios weakened, CAA couldn't write $100M checks — but could orchestrate buyers. Sony courtship became Sony Pictures Entertainment; Matsushita (Panasonic parent) bought Universal (≈10 years before Seagram/Vivendi/NBC chain). Lew Wasserman and Sid Sheinberg resisted being owned — Ovitz earned "Brinks truck" fees without becoming studio employee until Disney CEO experiment. Hosts compare putting iPhone on table (2007) as power-move analogy.

> **5.** CAA apexed at founder exit — classic partnership succession risk.
>
> **Q** Why didn't CAA become WME-scale under Ovitz?
>
> **A** Ovitz sold ≈100% to Young Turks for ≈$200M paid from ≈5 years of firm profits — interest-free financing to buyers. ≈90% founder ownership meant no public equity to fund global roll-up. WME/Ari Emanuel consolidated differently (MMA, entertainment conglomerate). Lesson: relationship moats walk out; mailroom systems don't replace dealmaker graph. Disney CEO stint failed entrepreneur-to-employee transition. Hosts compare WME path to counterfactual if Ovitz had raised institutional capital.
