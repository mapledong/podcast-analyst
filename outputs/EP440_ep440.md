## Background

> Horing co-founded Insight Partners in 1995 and helped build it into one of the defining software growth-equity firms. The episode covers sourcing, cold outreach, software margins, subscription models, ARR scale, board work, fund sizing, AI, and how Insight has changed as private software markets grew from niche to enormous. The conversation is data-rich because it ties firm design to market structure. Insight built specialized sourcing, a large operating group, and a culture focused on software long before growth equity became crowded. He also discusses how market structure changed after the 2016 Vision Fund era and why fund size alone is not edge. His framing is that the firm keeps winning when domain knowledge, relationship data, and operating support improve together as scale rises. A recurring subtheme is that insight quality compounds with time in market: long-tenured teams can interpret new software cycles faster because they have seen similar adoption, pricing, and distribution patterns before.

---

## Key Facts

> **F1** Insight Partners was founded in 1995, and Horing argues the software focus worked because recurring revenue, high gross margins, and low marginal distribution costs let winners compound for decades instead of single product cycles. He notes that this structure kept working across multiple market regimes, from early private software markets to today's larger growth rounds, because recurring contracts and renewal behavior gave better visibility than many cyclical sectors.

> **F2** Horing gives a concrete magnitude example from software M&A: EMC reportedly spent about $650 million for VMware and later sold around $60 billion of value, which captures why owning software optionality can matter far more than initial check size. The example is used to show outcome asymmetry: one enduring software asset can return multiples of an entire early fund, which is why Insight spends heavily on identifying compounding products before they become obvious.

> **F3** Scale changed Insight's toolkit over time: Patrick references the industry shift to 10+ billion and 20 billion funds, Horing says Insight's current marginal fund is about $12 billion, and the firm pairs that capital with a repeatable sourcing-and-operations machine. He ties this to organization design, saying bigger funds only help if paired with specialist teams, repeat founder relationships, and operating resources that can materially improve go-to-market and product execution after investment.

In his framing, software investing maturity progresses from pattern collection to institutional pattern application, and scale only matters when it raises decision quality.

>
> **A** By calling and tracking software companies long before they needed money, Insight created relationship and market data that competitors could not easily buy at the moment of a deal.
