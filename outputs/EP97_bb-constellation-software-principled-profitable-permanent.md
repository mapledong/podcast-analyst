## Background

> Zack Fuss breaks down Constellation Software with Chris Cerrone, partner and portfolio manager at Allspring Capital Management, tracing a Toronto-listed serial acquirer of 500-plus vertical market software (VMS) businesses founded by Mark Leonard in 1995. Cerrone's firm has owned shares since 2014 after reading Leonard's shareholder letters — transparent, humble, and unusually honest about capital allocation and operations.
>
> The conversation covers Leonard's path from Barclays banking intern to venture capital dissatisfaction to a permanent-capital vehicle for sticky, small-market software; why VMS moats come from mission-critical customization and mid-90s-percent customer retention; a decentralized M&A engine doing upward of 100 deals per year; compensation with zero stock-based pay and mandatory insider share purchases; spinoffs Topicus and Lumine; and the tension between 4–5% organic growth and 20% returns on invested capital as the company deploys ≈$1 billion-plus of annual free cash flow against a ≈$6 billion cumulative acquisition spend since 2005.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Constellation generates roughly $6.5 billion of revenue — compounded ≈24% since the 2006 IPO and ≈22% over the prior decade — with ≈70% recurring from maintenance and subscriptions, ≈20% free cash flow margins on just over $1 billion of clean FCF (no stock-based compensation), and ≈20% returns on invested capital matching 20–30% equity IRR hurdles on acquisitions mostly under $10 million at ≈1–2× revenue. Geographic mix is ≈40% U.S., ≈40% Europe and UK, ≈10% Canada, remainder elsewhere across 32,000 employees and 100-plus niche verticals where no single end market is material. Since IPO the share price compounded ≈34% annually over 17 years to ≈130× the listing price.

> **F2** VMS economics drive the model: products cost less than 1% of customer revenue yet run day-to-day operations for transit agencies, utilities, home builders, tennis clubs, and similar niches — yielding mid-to-high-single-digit pricing when disclosed, mid-90s-percent annual customer retention implying 20–30-year relationships, and small addressable markets (often tens of millions of dollars) that deter Salesforce-, Oracle-, and SAP-scale entrants. Constellation tracks 50,000-plus potential targets; healthy ≈$5 million VMS assets with ≈30% EBITDA margins often go to private equity, but break-even or declining businesses play to Constellation's proprietary base-rate database from hundreds of prior deals.

> **F3** Capital structure reflects Leonard's dilution allergy: after a regretted 1999 secondary raise on top of $25 million of 1995 seed capital, Constellation has issued no equity, pays cash bonuses with 75% of after-tax proceeds mandatorily reinvested in shares held ≈4 years, and compensates Leonard solely through his ≈7% family stake — no salary since 2015. Organic recurring revenue growth averaged ≈4% since 2016 (negative in only one pandemic quarter); a large healthcare acquisition group drags consolidated organic growth by ≈100 basis points by design. A Canadian bank study of 73 software peers found median FCF margin of 15% excluding stock-based comp versus 1% including it — reframing Constellation's ≈25% EBIT margin plus ≈4–5% organic growth as exceeding a cash-adjusted Rule of 40.

## Mental Model · *Decentralized VMS Compounding with Magnetic Hurdle Rates*

> **Components**
>
> Three legs support the acquisition engine: discipline (20–30% IRR hurdles that Leonard calls magnetic — lowering them once erodes all returns), data (proprietary base rates from ≈1,000 business units benchmark messy targets private equity cannot underwrite), and preferred-acquirer status (permanent ownership for founders who fear financial sponsors). Operations and M&A decentralize through six operating groups approving nearly all deals up to $20 million without head-office sign-off, scaling toward dozens of delegated approvers. Leonard combines operator and allocator roles with trustee-ship — special dividends and disclosed intrinsic-value sales when he believed shares ran hot, personal backstop offers during 2011 sponsor exits, and refusal to buy back shares without disclosing trade secrets.
>
> **Application**
>
> Generalist software investors misread Constellation through a Rule-of-40 lens optimized for stock-comp-heavy growth SaaS. The relevant algorithm is redeploy ≈$1 billion-plus of annual FCF at ≈20% ROIC for mid-teens-plus per-share compounding — but scale is the crux: ≈$6 billion invested since 2005 must be matched in the next four to five years just to reinvest one year's cash flow. Jack Henry's contrast is instructive: strategic vertical focus and higher multiples bought dominance but lower ROIC and slower per-share compounding. Constellation may lower hurdles on large deals or buy outside VMS (Leonard nearly bought a ≈$1 billion thermal-oil asset in 2022) while spinning verticals like Lumine to compete with private equity's second bite at the apple.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Return on capital beats organic growth as the north star.
>
> **Q** Why accept ≈4% organic growth?
>
> **A** Cerrone argues Leonard prioritizes IRR over headline growth — occasionally buying declining 'cigar butt' VMS at prices that still clear hurdles, with a healthcare platform estimated to weigh consolidated organic growth down ≈100 bps by design. Internal studies found many organic-growth initiatives earned poor returns on time and capital deployed.

> **2.** Zero dilution aligns everyone with owners.
>
> **Q** How does compensation differ from peers?
>
> **A** No public equity issuance since a 1999 round Leonard calls a multi-billion-dollar mistake; zero stock-based comp; executives invest 75% of after-tax bonuses into escrowed shares for ≈4 years; directors buy stock with all fees; Leonard takes no salary and holds ≈7% of shares while flying economy until he paid his own travel.

> **3.** Decentralized M&A scales what centralized buyers cannot.
>
> **Q** Why can't copycats replicate the model?
>
> **A** After Mark Leonard and CIO Bernie Anzeruth centralized M&A for the first decade, operating-group heads gained authority on deals up to $20 million across a 50,000-company database — combining local sourcing with head-office hurdle discipline and proprietary post-acquisition playbooks private equity lacks on its 25th VMS deal.

## Investment Ideas

> **1. CSU** · 🟢 LONG · ●●○ Medium
>
> Allspring has held since 2014 on a three-legged stool — durable ≈20% ROIC VMS, skilled capital allocation, and massive reinvestment runway — with ≈$6.5 billion revenue, ≈70% recurring mix, ≈20% clean FCF margins, and a proven 100-deal-per-year engine; Cerrone expects mid-teens-plus per-share compounding if FCF keeps redeploying at ≈20% returns even as absolute deal size rises.

## Chronology

> *Constellation Software*
>
> **1995** Mark Leonard raises ≈$25 million to launch Constellation as a permanent owner of vertical market software businesses.
>
> **1999** Second capital raise diversifies investors but permanently dilutes shareholders — Leonard's last equity issuance.
>
> **2000** Steve Scotchmer joins the board; mentor relationship with Leonard spans 35-plus years.
>
> **2006** IPO on the Toronto Stock Exchange; share price later compounds ≈34% annually to ≈130× over 17 years.
>
> **2011** Leonard offers personal share backstop if Constellation fails to compound at least 10% annually for PE sponsors seeking exit — ultimately unused.
>
> **2014** Allspring Capital initiates a client position after discovering Leonard's shareholder letters.
>
> **2015** Leonard stops salary, bonus, and expensed travel to align with employee shareholders.
>
> **2019–2022** Leonard discloses automatic sales above intrinsic value and pays a special dividend; spins Topicus and prepares Lumine telecom/media spin to pair cash and equity in large deals.
>
> **2023** Cerrone breakdown records Constellation at ≈$6.5 billion revenue, ≈$1 billion-plus FCF, 500-plus VMS businesses, and 100-plus acquisitions per year.
