## Background

> Decagon builds AI agents for customer support. Zhang describes the market from the trenches: customer-service volume, messy enterprise knowledge, integrations with systems of record, human escalation, and the difference between a model that answers questions and a system a large company can trust with customers. The conversation also covers founder judgment in the AI era. Speed matters, but so do technical taste, customer obsession, and choosing a wedge where AI creates visible ROI rather than just novelty. He repeatedly emphasizes that enterprise adoption is less about flashy model output and more about safe operation under real ticket volume, with clear controls for escalation, auditing, and customer experience. The broader message is that AI support is an operations problem as much as a model problem: value comes from safe deployment, measurable outcomes, and reliable integration depth. He also notes that enterprise buyers usually start with narrow workflows precisely because support quality is reputationally sensitive; once reliability is proven in constrained settings, expansion into higher-volume queues becomes a straightforward economic decision. He makes a similar point about accountability: enterprise deployments are judged on sustained outcomes over months, so instrumentation and escalation design are part of the product, not post-sale services. Zhang argues that reliability work is cumulative, and each successful production cycle reduces adoption friction for the next workflow expansion.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Zhang quantifies the enterprise support wedge clearly: many customers have support organizations around 500 people, and moving AI resolution from roughly 15-20% toward 50-80% creates immediate and auditable ROI. The size of this spread matters financially: for large enterprises, improving deflection by tens of percentage points can map directly to millions in annualized labor and service-cost reduction.

> **F2** He describes a measured deployment path rather than a big-bang release: Decagon can launch on a narrow surface area to about 5% of user volume, with fallback escalation when confidence is low. This controlled rollout approach reduces downside risk while still generating real production data, allowing teams to validate quality before expanding automation to broader intents and customer segments.

> **F3** Reliability is treated as product infrastructure: Zhang references simulation harnesses on the order of 10,000 constantly running tests, because one failure mode can damage trust faster than many successful chats can rebuild it. He treats this test infrastructure as necessary for enterprise trust because support mistakes are visible immediately; repeatability, audit trails, and robust fallback behavior are core to adoption.
