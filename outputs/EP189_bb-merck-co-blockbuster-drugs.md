## Background

> The episode frames Merck as one of pharma's great innovation houses, with roots in the late 1800s and a legendary 1980s-1990s run under Roy Vagelos. Sales rose from under $2B in 1980 to about $40B in 2000, then fell to roughly $27B by 2010 after pipeline and patent issues. Today's Merck is a near-$300B market-cap company centered on oncology. Keytruda does about $25B of annual revenue and represents about 40% of sales, while Gardasil contributes about $8.8B. The investment question is whether Merck can manage Keytruda's 2028 IP loss through combination regimens, biologic manufacturing barriers, and external pipeline deals ranging from $250M to $10B.
>
>  The nuance is that Keytruda's cliff is not binary. A small-molecule patent loss can destroy sales quickly, as the transcript's Seroquel example lost 80% in less than three months after 2012 protection ended. Keytruda is a biologic with many indications and combination possibilities, so erosion may be slower, but the revenue base is so large that even gradual loss matters. Merck therefore needs multiple replacement engines: Gardasil, antibody-drug conjugates, immunology, cardiovascular, and business development.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Merck had less than $2B of sales in 1980 and about $40B in 2000; after a difficult decade, sales were around $27B in 2010 before the modern oncology-focused company emerged.

> **F2** Current revenue is about $60B, with roughly $53B from pharmaceuticals; Keytruda alone produces about $25B, near 40% of total sales, and is approved across roughly 40 indications.

> **F3** Gardasil generated about $8.8B in 2023; since 2019 Merck has completed more than 12 transactions ranging from about $250M to $10B to build the post-Keytruda pipeline.

## Mental Model · *Patent Cliff Portfolio Defense*

> **Components**
>
> A pharma company must continually replace finite patent-protected cash flows. Biologics, combinations, indications, vaccines, and acquisitions are all tools to slow erosion and rebuild the curve.
>
> **Application**
>
> MRK is attractive if Keytruda erosion is slower than feared and business development fills the gap. Investors should track subcutaneous Keytruda, combination data, Gardasil durability, and pipeline acquisition returns.

>
> **Q** Will Keytruda fall like a small molecule?
>
> **A** The transcript contrasts small molecules that can lose 80% quickly with biologics and many indications, which may erode more slowly.

>
> **Q** How has pharma changed?
>
> **A** The industry moved from mostly internal sourcing in the 1990s to 50-60% or more external contribution through licensing and acquisitions.
