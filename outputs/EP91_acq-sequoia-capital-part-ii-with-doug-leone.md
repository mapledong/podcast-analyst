## Key Insights

> **1.** 1996 succession was institutional bet on duo.
>
> **Q** What changed when Don stepped back?
>
> **A** Moritz (journalist-turned-legend) plus Doug (sales-hustle immigrant) inherited Sequoia as internet wave began — semiconductor/PC era giving way to web. Fund numbering (VI/VII) marks generational transition and dot-com exposure. Episode context: recorded in Acquired season six (2020) with full transcript on acquired.fm; hosts Ben Gilbert and David Rosenthal apply standard playbook, grading, and investment-implications framing even for interview or Adapting-format episodes.

> **2.** Dot-com integrity defined brand.
>
> **Q** Why not take the mulligan fund?
>
> **A** Fund VI/VII had early wins then zeroes; easy path was new fee-bearing fund. Moritz/Leone worked positions to return capital minimum — Ford-bailout analogy. Many firms took mulligan; Sequoia reputation with LPs/founders benefited from not doing so.

> **3.** Google price regret became folklore.
>
> **Q** Was Google expensive?
>
> **A** Moritz quip 'never paid so much for so little' months in — classic Sequoia founder partnership ('we're in driver's seat, let us figure it out'). Post-IPO gains dominate; growth fund thesis validated. Hosts tie lesson to Acquired playbook themes for builders and investors reviewing the episode.

> **4.** China was disagree-and-commit expansion.
>
> **Q** Why Sequoia China mid-2000s?
>
> **A** Doug pounded table while Tencent/Alibaba existed but China outcome uncertain; Neil Shen second key hire. US partners held fort. Bezosism: look for #2 in enormous markets — India/China parallel.

> **5.** Growth funds follow market structure.
>
> **Q** Why add growth vehicles?
>
> **A** Winner-take-all internet requires faster GO launches and bigger late checks — Airbnb/Dropbox seed sizes ($600K/$1.2M) tiny vs eventual needs. Specialization (Benchmark) vs platform (Sequoia) split industry; vast majority of gains post-IPO so growth captures value.
