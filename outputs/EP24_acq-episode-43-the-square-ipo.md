## Key Facts

> **F1** At Square's start ≈30M US businesses under $100K revenue; only ≈6M could accept credit cards — ≈24M excluded.

> **F2** Square IPO priced $9/share (Nov 19, 2015) for ≈$2.9B valuation vs $6B Series E (≈6 months prior).

> **F3** Series E ratchet granted ≈20% IPO return; ≈$93M additional stock issued to investors when pricing halved round valuation.

> **F4** Goldman Sachs invested in Series E (≈$50M of $150M) and led IPO — banker fees ≈$10M vs ratchet payout incentives.

> **F5** Starbucks invested and moved US store payments to Square (2012) — strategically valuable, financially loss-making per swipe.

## Mental Model · *Underwriting as Product Moat*

> **Components**
>
> Square's wedge was not the dongle — incumbents copied hardware. Risk engine granting incremental volume as merchants prove non-fraudulent behavior opened 24M excluded micro-merchants. Flat 2.75% vs 15-page interchange schedules reduced cognitive load. Free reader shifted capex to CAC; software (Register, Capital, Payroll) increased LTV while payments tax funded bundle. Starbucks forced enterprise reliability; hangover nearly killed IPO story until unit economics cleared.
>
> **Application**
>
> In regulated rails (payments, lending), ML underwriting can be core IP — not a back-office cost center. Founders raising late mega-rounds: ratchets invert banker incentives; employees get diluted when strikes exceed public comps. IPO when private preference stacks threaten talent retention, not when vanity valuation demands it. Hosts note Jack's first post-Twitter pitch was an electric-car company to compete with Tesla — Square emerged only after the card-reader insight. Re-listen for acquisition category frames and investment tickers as Watch-only historical lenses — not live trading advice.

## What Makes It Work

> Square moat combines brand trust with micro-merchant risk models incumbents would not build — Verifone shut competing product after ≈1% fraud losses. UX (Register, inventory, appointments) monetized via payments — AWS-style flywheel cited by hosts. Jack Dorsey storycraft ('Jim the glassblower') aligned GTM; design excellence masked young-company ops. Dual-CEO Twitter/Square split hurt IPO roadshow narrative but did not break transaction growth. Weaknesses: payments low margin; Starbucks mispricing; Goldman conflict optics; competition from Stripe/Clover. Episode documents ratchet era excess more than current Block valuation. Block (formerly Square) narrative post-IPO validated Jack's merchant-OS framing — Cash App diversified TAM later but 2015 lesson is cap-table engineering vs fundamentals disconnect.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Founder stories are GTM infrastructure.
>
> **Q** Morley lawsuit vs glassblower myth?
>
> **A** Official story drives employee, investor, and customer alignment — Morley settled an IP dispute over the card-reader design while the glassblower myth still explains mission better than cap-table fights.

> **2.** Ratchets invert banker alignment.
>
> **Q** Why Goldman price low?
>
> **A** Series E promised ≈20% IPO return via extra shares — ≈$93M transfer vs ≈$10M underwriting fee. Participating as investor + lead underwriter creates incentive to price down; challenges 'let bank into last round' conventional wisdom.

> **3.** Private preferences hide true dilution.
>
> **Q** Why IPO below $6B?
>
> **A** Liquidation stacks mean headline private valuations overstate common upside. Employees hired at $6B strike saw underwater options for months — retention risk forced public market despite bad press.

> **4.** Enterprise detour via Starbucks.
>
> **Q** Was Starbucks worth it?
>
> **A** Lost money per transaction but accelerated Register/inventory enterprise features and credibility — strategic win, financial hangover. Starbucks renegotiated in 2015 and exited as exclusive processor; Square kept enterprise product lessons. Classic big-partner forcing function.

> **5.** Not a payments company — merchant OS.
>
> **Q** How survive 'payments suck' narrative?
>
> **A** S-1 frames access to capital, customers, loyalty — payments as monetization layer for software bundle analogous to AWS subsidizing tools. Fundamentals (transaction revenue ≈$482M/qtr vs services) vindicated post-IPO.
