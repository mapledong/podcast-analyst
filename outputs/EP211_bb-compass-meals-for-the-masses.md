## Background

> Compass provides outsourced food and support services across offices, schools, hospitals, sports venues, and defense sites in roughly 30 countries. The transcript frames a $320B food-services market that could be closer to $700B including adjacent services. Compass has roughly 11-12% market share, $42B of revenue, and more than two-thirds of revenue in North America. Contracts usually run three to five years; retention is about 96%, which still forces the company to win significant new business each year because 4% churn on $42B is large. COVID hit revenue by around 40%, but the post-2018 management era refocused the company on organic wins, sub-sector expertise, and margin discipline.
>
>  Compass's local execution problem is harder than the simple market-share math suggests. Food service contracts require daily reliability, labor scheduling, procurement, health standards, and client-specific menus. Scale helps, but only if local managers keep service quality high. That is why the transcript's 27-subsector operating model matters: hospitals, schools, offices, sports venues, and defense facilities do not buy the same product even when procurement scale is shared.

## Key Facts

> **F1** Compass operates in about 30 countries with roughly $42B of revenue and 11-12% share of a $320B food-services market; including adjacent services, the opportunity may be closer to $700B.

> **F2** Contract retention is around 96%, industry-leading but not perfect; on $42B of revenue, replacing the 4% churn means winning roughly $1.5B of business annually before net growth.

> **F3** More than two-thirds of revenue comes from North America, and the organization is managed across 27 subsectors; during COVID, revenue was down about 40%, showing the cyclicality hidden inside the recurring contract base.

## Mental Model · *Scale Procurement in Local Service*

> **Components**
>
> Low margins can still compound when purchasing scale, route density, and vertical specialization improve win rates. Retention plus new wins creates a steady flywheel.
>
> **Application**
>
> CMPGY should be judged on net new business growth, retention, and margin progression rather than headline food inflation. The risk is wage pressure and client insourcing during downturns.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Retention is not enough.
>
> **Q** Why does 96% retention still require selling?
>
> **A** Four percent churn on $42B of revenue is a large hole; Compass needs roughly $1.5B of wins annually just to replace lost business before net growth.

> **2.** Scale shows up in procurement.
>
> **Q** Where is Compass advantaged?
>
> **A** Food purchasing, labor management, and sector templates improve with size, especially in North America where the company has more than two-thirds of revenue.

> **3.** The market is still fragmented.
>
> **Q** How big is the runway?
>
> **A** Compass has 11-12% share of a $320B market, leaving consolidation and outsourcing growth ahead if service quality holds.
