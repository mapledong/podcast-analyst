## Key Facts

> **F1** Broadcom proposed approximately $103 billion to acquire Qualcomm in November 2017 — largest technology acquisition offer ever at recording — at $70 per share ($60 cash, $10 stock); total purchase price including assumed debt roughly $130 billion; Qualcomm board rejected the offer the day Episode 48 recorded. Ben predicted eventual close near $80/share; hosts grade deal assuming regulatory approval despite CFIUS review tied to pending Brocade merger.

> **F2** Qualcomm reported $25.2 billion revenue in 2015, $23.5 billion in 2016, and $22.2 billion in 2017 — declining three straight years as smartphone market saturated — versus Broadcom's $13.2 billion in 2016 (up from $6.8 billion in 2015 post-Avago merger). Qualcomm ≈56% profit margins vs Broadcom ≈59%; hosts note neither company is obvious acquirer over acquired.

> **F3** Qualcomm's 10-K cited $15.4 billion from equipment and services plus $8 billion from licensing — roughly a third of revenue from patent royalties on CDMA technology commercialized for cellular. Founded 1985 by UCSD professor Irwin Jacobs; CDMA network effect as base stations and handsets multiplied across carriers. Sold handset business to Kyocera, pivoted to Snapdragon chipsets used in Android flagships.

> **F4** Deal financing included Silver Lake Partners (Skype-to-Microsoft seller) plus Andreessen Horowitz committing $5 billion in convertible debt. Qualcomm was simultaneously acquiring NXP Semiconductors for $47 billion while Broadcom was acquiring Brocade for $5 billion — Broadcom's Qualcomm bid was not contingent on either side deal closing. Apple lawsuit and FTC suit depressed QCOM shares below prior-year $70 levels.

> **F5** Bloomberg stat cited on show: combined Broadcom-Qualcomm would supply ≈65% of handset semiconductor bill of materials. Nvidia traded from ≈$20/share in 2015 to $212 at recording (≈10x in ≈2 years); Intel acquired Nervana for $350M; Sequoia led $50M into year-old UK Graphcore ($110M total raised). SoftBank acquired ARM for $32 billion; David grades deal B, Ben B-minus on show.

## Mental Model · *Scale or Die in Silicon*

> **Components**
>
> Semiconductor R&D and fab economics require massive scale — $10B+ to build a fab, so fabless designers (Qualcomm, Broadcom) contract TSMC/Samsung while TSMC itself is a $200B+ market cap commodity manufacturer. Mobile saturation commoditizes non-CPU chips; Apple vertical integration (A11, motion co-processor, DSP) threatens horizontal suppliers even as iPhone X still carries Qualcomm LTE modem and Broadcom wireless-charging/touchscreen chips. Consolidation bundles R&D and supplier power against handset OEMs — textbook economies-of-scale M&A rare in software but real in semis. Standards (CDMA, ARM, x86) create network effects even in capital-intensive hardware. Low interest rates enable debt-financed roll-ups (Qualcomm/Broadcom levered vs typical software stock deals).
>
> **Application**
>
> Evaluate chip M&A on customer concentration (Apple lawsuits depressing QCOM), vertical-integration risk (successful OEMs in-house modems), and national-security overlay (5G, CFIUS) — not synergies alone. Hostile bids exploit legal overhang; Qualcomm management correctly rejected opportunistic $70 offer when shares traded below $70/year-ago levels despite higher intrinsic value per hosts. Fabless model separates design moat from manufacturing — TSMC wins either way.

## What Makes It Work

> Qualcomm moat: CDMA code-division multiple access commercialized for cellular — network effect as more base stations and handsets adopted the standard; $8B licensing stream on $23B revenue. Snapdragon processors plus LTE/Gigabit transceivers in iPhone X (dual-sourced Intel/Qualcomm modems) and Pixel 2 XL. Broadcom moat: Hock Tan acquisition integration machine — today's Broadcom is Avago (HP semiconductor lineage) that took the Broadcom name; iPhone X Broadcom chips for wireless charging, power amplifier, touchscreen controller. Weaknesses: revenue declining 2015–2017; Apple and FTC lawsuits depressing valuation; handset OEMs (Apple nearly all in-house chips) verticalizing; Singapore HQ complicated CFIUS despite Delaware re-domicile plan. Combined entity antitrust concern — 65% BOM share. Hosts grade deal B/B-: necessary consolidation, not Instagram-level excitement; IoT/embedded may outlast phone commoditization where Whirlpool won't design own chips.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Depressed QCOM invited predator.
>
> **Q** Why hostile now?
>
> **A** Apple patent litigation and FTC suit crushed Qualcomm's multiple — trading below $70/share vs $70/year ago while intrinsic value likely higher per hosts. Broadcom opportunistic per Hock Tan playbook: $70/share offer ($60 cash, $10 stock) on ≈$103B headline (≈$130B with debt). Silver Lake committed $5B convertible debt. Ben predicts deal eventually closes ≈$80/share; hosts evaluate rest of episode assuming regulatory approval passes — prescient on price, not on later CFIUS block.

> **2.** CFIUS adds geopolitical layer.
>
> **Q** Would regulators approve?
>
> **A** Hosts skeptical but assume approval for grading purposes — Bloomberg: combined entity ≈65% of handset semiconductor bill of materials. Broadcom acquiring Brocade was delayed for CFIUS; announced Singapore-to-Delaware re-domicile to circumvent foreign-investment review, intertwined with Trump administration politics. Episode recorded before March 2018 executive order blocking deal citing 5G national security — hosts' regulatory assumption proved wrong.

> **3.** Fabless reshaped competition.
>
> **Q** Industry structure?
>
> **A** Neither Qualcomm (San Diego) nor Broadcom (Singapore, re-domiciling) is classic Silicon Valley fab owner — both design, TSMC/Samsung manufacture. iPhone X tear-down: Qualcomm Gigabit LTE transceiver and modem (Intel dual-sourced on some batches); Broadcom wireless charging, power amplifier, touchscreen — despite Apple A11 in-house CPU. Pixel 2 XL heavily Qualcomm, minimal Broadcom. Fabs cost $10B+; Graphcore raised $110M in just over a year competing with Nvidia's ML GPU dominance.

> **4.** Silicon innovation revived.
>
> **Q** Why cover semis in 2017?
>
> **A** ML boom ended boring-semis era: Nvidia ≈$20 to $212 in ≈2 years; Intel bought Nervana $350M; Sequoia $50M into Graphcore ($110M total). SoftBank acquired ARM $32B. Hosts frame 'Silicon in Silicon Valley' returning — Singapore Broadcom bidding on San Diego Qualcomm while true Valley fabs consolidated to TSMC/Samsung/Intel. Sequoia tension: 'capital intensity produces nightmares' vs leading $50M semiconductor rounds.

> **5.** Consolidation grade: necessary not great.
>
> **Q** Acquired grade?
>
> **A** David grades B, Ben B- — 'needs to happen' consolidation, not transformative like Instagram. Revenue declining at Qualcomm ($25.2B→$22.2B) while Broadcom doubled post-Avago ($6.8B→$13.2B). Similar profit margins (≈56% vs ≈59%) make size asymmetry unclear — merger of equals feel. Either you are the consolidator or get consolidated; Broadcom aggressive bid for declining-but-massive Qualcomm at relative discount. Management resistance plus regulatory plus customer conflict = triple headwind hosts underweighted at recording.
