## Key Facts

> **F2** Brian Schultz joined Microsoft corp dev in 1999, left mid-2000s to co-found Ontela in Seattle with Dan Shapiro (now Glowforge CEO); Photobucket acquired Ontela before Brian returned to Microsoft strategic investments — founder experience contrasts with typical investment-banking hires into corp dev.

> **F3** Microsoft relaunched Microsoft Ventures for early-stage VC while Brian's Strategic Investments team writes later-stage, larger checks — David's sidebar: 'The Series B round was your IPO,' and the number-one VC question was what happens when Microsoft enters your space.

> **F4** 2016 M&A surge included Microsoft's LinkedIn acquisition as the largest deal Brian referenced; Ben asks whether companies marketing themselves as great pickups drove industry-wide activity versus Microsoft-specific catalysts.

> **F5** Private equity began active software LBOs circa 2016 as SaaS matured into cash-flowing businesses — opposite of Microsoft's integration thesis; Ben notes PE firms historically bought Heinz-style staples, not tech, until recently; Skype episode context: overseas cash deployment avoided repatriation tax, and PE had 3X'd Skype in roughly 2 years before Microsoft acquired it.

## Mental Model · *Strategic Bet Not VC Fund*

> **Components**
>
> Corporate strategic investments optimize partnership and integration upside — David quotes Brian: Wall Street is not evaluating Microsoft share price on how good you are as an investor. Operating shareholders want sustainable multiples from core business execution; LPs in a 10-year fund seek ≈3X. Microsoft learned from past failed VC-style programs and relaunched Ventures separately while Strategic Investments targets later-stage partnership stakes. Creating an exit for a portfolio company differs fundamentally from putting more money in — Brian stresses this to startup shareholders. Amy Hood may punish bad bets even if Wall Street ignores good ones. Founder-turned-corp-dev brings empathy: startups should build relationships with business units first, not cold-call corp dev expecting a week-close. Milestones coincidentally align with cash-out moments when founders are running out of money.
>
> **Application**
>
> Startups seeking Microsoft should cultivate business-unit relationships years before formal M&A — strategic investment may precede acquisition and must fit roadmap, not create incompatible business lines under one management structure. When evaluating corporate VC, ask whether the parent optimizes partnership value or fund IRR; Microsoft explicitly chose the former. PE LBO rollups represent the opposite playbook — cash-flow extraction without platform integration.

## What Makes It Work

> Microsoft strategic investing leverages enterprise distribution (Azure, Office, Salesforce analog on enterprise side) for portfolio companies — Foursquare data powers Cortana restaurant recommendations and broader location use cases beyond user-facing tips. Schultz's Ontela founder credibility lowers startup suspicion versus pure banker corp dev. Facebook 2007 at $15B validated strategic alignment plus financial return — Bing maps and Windows Phone integrations followed. Weaknesses: horizontal platform conflicts when Microsoft wants democratic participation across container players while holding strategic bets on one; Amy Hood scrutiny on bad investments; org complexity splitting Ventures (early) from Strategic Investments (later). Taylor Barada at Adobe shares relationship-first corp dev thesis — pattern across successful acquirers. PE software LBOs compete for the same assets with opposite integration intent — conglomerate rollups versus single-business-line privates.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Corporate VC ≠ financial VC.
>
> **Q** Why invest if not for IRR?
>
> **A** Ben asks whether effort centers on partnerships through investment rather than returns — Brian's Facebook example shows why: at $15B valuation the world thought Microsoft was crazy, yet tight integrations followed (Bing maps back to Facebook, Windows Phone first-party ties when iOS/Android walled gardens). David quotes Brian: Wall Street is not evaluating Microsoft share price on investing skill. Operating shareholders want sustainable core-business multiples; LPs want ≈3X over 10 years — completely different pressure. Strategic investment creates exits and alignment, not passive capital deployment.

> **2.** Founder empathy changes corp dev.
>
> **Q** Ontela experience?
>
> **A** David notes Brian started at Microsoft in 1999 in corp dev, left mid-2000s to co-found Ontela in Seattle with Dan Shapiro (now Glowforge CEO), sold to Photobucket, then returned — rare founder-in-corp-dev path versus investment-banking analyst backgrounds David shares from his own banking days. Empathy for founders who need money and want to sell changes how he evaluates timing conversations like 'now is the right time to sell.'

> **3.** PE entered software circa 2016.
>
> **Q** Why LBOs in tech?
>
> **A** David flags PE re-emerging in 2016 software — opposite of Microsoft's roadmap-driven integration. Brian explains SaaS companies now cash-flow positive enough for LBO debt PE historically avoided in tech. David contrasts Heinz-style PE staples with Microsoft partnership model. Ben defines LBO (leveraged buyout) for engineering listeners. Skype showed PE path: ≈3X in ≈2 years before Microsoft bought using offshore cash — strategic buyer versus financial sponsor compete for same assets.

> **4.** Relationships precede transactions.
>
> **Q** Startup advice?
>
> **A** David references Taylor Barada (Adobe): relationships take years — don't call corp dev expecting a deal next week. Brian agrees for investing: start with the business unit, not corp dev. David jokes milestones magically coincide with running out of money — founders balance partnership talks against payroll. Pattern matching in mature corp dev orgs usually demands big markets, but breaking rules sometimes works.

> **5.** Overseas cash shaped Skype.
>
> **Q** Tax repatriation?
>
> **A** Ben ties to Acquired's Skype episode: large overseas cash piles made offshore-target M&A tax-efficient versus repatriation. David notes tax lawyers swarm every big deal from his banking days. PE had 3X'd Skype in roughly 2 years before Microsoft bought — capital deployment and tax structure intertwined in large M&A, not just strategic fit.
