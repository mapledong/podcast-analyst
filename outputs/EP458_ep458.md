## Background

> Alex Behring and Daniel Schwartz explain 3G Capital from the inside: Brazilian roots, one investment per fund, operator DNA, Burger King, Tim Hortons, Hunter Douglas, Skechers, and lessons from Kraft Heinz. Alex ran a large Latin American railroad at age 30; Daniel became CFO and CEO of Burger King in his early 30s. The episode is a detailed private-equity operating manual: concentrate because great businesses and great CEOs are rare, preserve downside, keep house capital high, develop talent early, centralize the what not the how, and use cost discipline as a tool for growth rather than a substitute for business quality.

## Mental Model · *One Basket, Watch It Closely*

> **Components**
>
> 3G's reputation overweights cost cutting and underweights business quality. They spend most investment meetings deciding whether a business is truly good. At an RBI offsite, 10 pages covered costs and about 790 covered growth, operations, and new restaurants. The best businesses own the end-customer relationship. Private label and big retailers can disintermediate CPG brands with customer concentration. Burger King, Tim Hortons, Hunter Douglas, and Skechers have more direct brand or distribution control. Young talent works only when ownership, mentorship, and stakes are real. Alex was given the railroad at 30, Daniel became Burger King CEO at 32, Josh Kobza became CFO at 26. They were surrounded by experienced operators and given meaningful economics and responsibility.
>
> **Application**
>
> 3G applies this framework by spending substantial diligence time on customer relationship durability, unit-level economics, and leadership bench depth before deals close. Post-close, they pair clear financial accountability with operator development and decentralized execution. The Burger King-to-RBI path, Hunter Douglas acquisition, and Skechers investment are presented as examples where business quality, global scale room, and governance alignment support multi-year compounding.
