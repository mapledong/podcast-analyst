## Background

> Ben and David host father-and-son investors with opposite public reputations: Howard Marks, Oaktree co-founder and author of memos Warren Buffett reads first; Andrew Marks, TQ Ventures co-founder scaling a growth fund from zero to roughly $1 billion in five years. The conversation centers on their pandemic collaboration — Howard's most-read memo ever — and whether "value" and "growth" remain meaningful categories when global TAMs expand while competitive moats erode faster than ever. They walk through Howard's origin in high-yield when banks shunned it, the Amazon case study from both portfolios, selling discipline (cigar butts vs letting winners ride), and firm-building contrasts: Oaktree's 1,000+ employees and public memos versus TQ's quiet, founder-aligned venture model. Brian Arthur's increasing-returns paper — prose edited by novelist Cormac McCarthy — frames why network-era businesses can justify valuations old value screens reject, without abandoning margin-of-safety thinking about downside.

## Mental Model · *Price vs Value Under Uncertainty*

> **Components**
>
> Howard's frame: separate what an asset is worth (value) from what the market charges (price); margin of safety lives in that gap. Risk means permanent capital loss, not volatility. Andrew's frame: larger addressable markets and faster paradigm shifts raise both upside ceilings and forecast uncertainty — competition arrives faster even as TAMs grow. The memo's synthesis: neither label is sufficient; judgment integrates qualitative business quality, durability of advantage, and price paid. Public markets' information efficiency limits edge; venture accepts illiquidity and failure rates for optionality on outliers.
>
> **Application**
>
> When a stock or startup looks "expensive" on static multiples, ask whether increasing-returns dynamics (network effects, scale, brand) extend the compounding period — Amazon is the episode's proof case for both sides. When a cheap asset looks safe, ask whether disruption shortens future cash-flow years — Howard acknowledges moats are less permanent. Match strategy to temperament: Howard's public writing and institutional LP base suit Oaktree; Andrew's low-profile venture style suits TQ. Selling rules differ: Howard historically favored clearing positions as prices approach value; Andrew and modern growth practice emphasize duration of remaining growth, not one-year growth rates.

## What Makes It Work

> Oaktree's edge stacks specialization, reputation, and cycle discipline. Howard entered high-yield when major banks refused the business — a classic counter-positioning move that built underwriting skill and relationships competitors could not replicate without accepting reputational and loss-profile risk. Memos distributed publicly since the 1990s became both intellectual brand and LP marketing, correlating with committed capital growth once posted online. Distressed cycles reward patient capital with legal and restructuring expertise; Oaktree scaled to 1,000+ employees by recruiting judgment Howard could not solo-source. TQ's advantage is inverse: early-stage access in tech, small fund size relative to megafunds, and alignment with founders who want quiet capital rather than platform theater. Andrew explicitly avoids Howard's memo/podcast megaphone — competitive advantage in venture often comes from deal access and founder trust, not public intellectual branding. TQ's ≈$1B scale is large for a young firm but tiny versus Andreessen or Sequoia, preserving nimbleness. Weaknesses: Oaktree faces perpetual competition as distressed investing democratizes; memo alpha is harder when everyone reads the same letters. TQ faces 2022–23 venture downturn, public-market comparables compressing private marks, and the eternal venture risk that one fund's winners must offset many zeros. Father-son dialogue highlights that neither firm wins on formula alone — both sell judgment under different liquidity and return profiles. The episode also contrasts Howard's cigar-butt selling discipline with venture's hold-the-winner philosophy — a tension every allocator faces when straddling public and private markets.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Value and growth labels obscure a shared core: price versus intrinsic worth.
>
> **Q** Did Howard and Andrew disagree about Amazon?
>
> **A** Howard missed Amazon as a public stock; Andrew's world lives in growth tech. Both use Amazon in the memo to show great businesses can fail traditional value screens while still being rational investments if duration and moat are understood. Ben Graham's GEICO fortune reinforces that "value investing" in practice includes outliers that look growth-like. The argument is semantic cleanup, not abandoning discipline.

> **2.** Moat permanence has shortened even as TAMs expanded.
>
> **Q** Should companies be worth less if disruption accelerates?
>
> **A** Howard entertains shortening implied cash-flow years when competitive destruction speeds up. Andrew counters with globally addressable markets justifying larger terminal values. Both forces are true simultaneously — Brian Arthur's increasing returns versus faster competitive response. Investors must judge which dominates for a given business, not apply one slogan.

> **3.** Selling philosophy diverges between cigar butts and compounders.
>
> **Q** When do you sell a winner?
>
> **A** Howard historically sold as price approached value — cigar butts don't reach $8. Venture and growth practice, illustrated by a Facebook IPO investor who held while partners sold, rewards letting exceptional winners run when decades of growth may remain. The episode's lesson: selling rules should match asset type; applying value sell discipline to Amazon-like compounders destroys returns.

> **4.** Firm design must match investor temperament and asset class.
>
> **Q** Why doesn't Andrew write Oaktree-style memos?
>
> **A** Howard's public letters fit institutional credit LPs and thought leadership; Andrew says TQ's edge is different — founder relationships and early deal access, not media. Howard notes strategy must suit demeanor. Venture's risk is permanent loss on illiquid stakes; Oaktree's is cyclical credit timing — different hiring, compensation, and marketing muscles.

> **5.** Judgment is the residual when credible arguments exist on both sides.
>
> **Q** Where does judgment come from?
>
> **A** When every debate has a smart bull and bear case, outcomes hinge on experience-weighted judgment — not spreadsheets alone. Howard recruited 1,000+ employees partly to scale judgment assessment. Andrew's founder evaluation echoes venture pattern-matching on teams and markets. Both reject pure quant as sufficient in their domains.
