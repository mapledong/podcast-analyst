## Key Facts

> **F1** Intel's global DRAM market share fell from above 80% at the start of the 1980s to roughly 1.3% by 1984 as Japanese producers undercut prices 10% repeatedly per internal battle-card memo Andy Grove cited.

> **F2** Gordon Moore and Andy Grove's 1985 'new CEO' conversation concluded Intel must exit memory; full shutdown took all of 1985 despite president-level mandate — division heads revolted twice.

> **F3** Intel broke second-source agreement with AMD on the 386 (first 32-bit x86); AMD reverse-engineered in a clean room — six-year lag before competitive x86 product (1991).

> **F4** Intel Inside marketing paid ≈50% of PC OEM advertising costs if they included the logo — co-op spend AMD could not match after years outside the market.

> **F5** Under Grove as CEO (1987–1998), Intel revenue grew from $1.9 billion to $26 billion; 2019 revenue ≈$72 billion with ≈$21 billion net income (≈25% margin).

## Mental Model · *Strategic Inflection Point (Grove)*

> **Components**
>
> When a 10X shift hits Porter forces (here: competition), fundamentals change — revenue can flatline while profits vanish (Uber/Lyft price-war analogy). Identity ('Intel = memory') blocks rational exit. External CEO thought experiment unlocks action. Winning pivot requires earlier R&D bets (386 in corner) plus willingness to destroy org structure (factory layoffs). Post-win, paranoia drives power preservation (sole-source 386, Intel Inside subsidies). Japanese 10% rule and Intel second-sourcing in late 1970s accelerated competitor learning — mirror Grove's later AMD sole-source break.
>
> **Application**
>
> Leaders should run 'new CEO' exercises before crisis. Listen to customer-facing ranks reallocating early (Grove's shock: engineers already shifting to CPUs). Commodity businesses with no scale/process/counter-positioning power need exit options years before inflection. COVID parallel: 10X demand collapse in many industries simultaneously — Grove's Swimming Across perspective on adaptation vs nostalgia. For operators in 2020 COVID crisis: Grove's 'new land' quote applies when demand collapses 10X — experiment before inflection, not during. Commodity detection: if competitors can copy within 12 months via capital alone, plan exit option early.

## What Makes It Work

> Post-1985 Intel built process power plus cornered x86 architecture on IBM PC clones — sole 386 supplier for first six years of PC wave. Intel Inside converted ingredient branding into consumer pull and OEM marketing subsidies (≈50% co-op), tsunami-ing share before AMD recovered. Vertical integration (design plus fabs) differed from later fabless ARM ecosystem — captured manufacturing learning curve through 1990s. Weaknesses: memory commoditization blind spot repeated in mobile (missed low-power), Intel Optane 'big bet' on memory (2015) rhymes with Grove exit. AMD x86 return and TSMC manufacturing lead eroded moat 2010s+. Grove's paranoia did not survive successive CEOs indefinitely. Versus Japanese DRAM makers, Intel fled commodity; versus ARM, Intel tried to defend x86 in mobile and lost — disrupter became disruptee. Uber Eats diversification cited as modern parallel to CPU side-bet saving core in crisis. Second-sourcing culture that helped Japanese learn DRAM in late 1970s became AMD x86 path — Intel's paranoia post-386 break was direct response. IBM PC (1981) contract made x86 the clone standard; Compaq 386 launch created pull Intel could not manufacture fast enough after memory exit. Grove Time Man of the Year (1997) capped immigrant arc from Hungary persecution to Silicon Valley icon. 2019 $72B revenue with 25% net margin shows CPU/server dominance decades later, but TSMC foundry lead and Apple M-series shift illustrate disrupter-becomes-disruptee cycle ARM episode explores. Uber Eats surpassing ride revenue during COVID cited as modern side-bet saving core — Intel 386 was equivalent side-bet saved by memory exit timing. Intel Inside co-op spent half of OEM PC marketing when logo included — tsunami vs AMD clean-room delay to 1991. Factory layoffs Andy led personally signaled seriousness during 1985 memory exit revolt. Optane 2015 memory bet shows Grove lesson decay without perpetual paranoid leadership. Potapalooza cross-promotion linked Intel story to Sequoia Part I heritage — Shockley, Fairchild, Arthur Rock $2.5M 1968 round.

## Key Insights

> **1.** Memory was always commodity; Intel did not see it.
>
> **Q** Why didn't R&D save DRAM?
>
> **A** Intel stayed 6–12 months ahead via density until Japanese fabs brought operational excellence plus capital scale. No 7 Powers — hard to do, not durable. Grove: competition 10X'd; profits competed away like Uber/Lyft. Value-added memory features still competed on dimensions buyers (PC OEMs) did not care about.

> **2.** The Moore-Grove conversation is legendary because execution was hell.
>
> **Q** Why did exit take all of 1985?
>
> **A** Two division heads refused shutdown plans; Andy personally closed factories. Sales quotas, identity ('we are the memory company'), and incomplete CPU product line objections — CPU team was '386 in the corner' without A-team or capacity. Organizational immune system fought co-founders.

> **3.** 386 sole-source was as big as leaving memory.
>
> **Q** How did Intel lock the PC?
>
> **A** Broke AMD second-source on 386 despite contract; AMD sued, clean-room copied x86 years later. Compaq first 386 customer — 32-bit step-change. Combined with Intel Inside paying half of Dell/Compaq/Gateway marketing when logo included — marketing spend rivals could not match. Hosts tie lesson to Acquired playbook themes for builders and investors reviewing the episode.

> **4.** Innovate before the inflection.
>
> **Q** When should Intel have bet on CPUs?
>
> **A** Grove: not when you're in crisis — years earlier. CPU R&D existed because Noyce/Moore believed in cutting-edge investment; IBM PC (1981) contract seeded x86 standard. Uber Eats vs Lyft (no delivery) cited as 2020 parallel — side bets before ride-share collapse.

> **5.** History rhymes with Optane.
>
> **Q** Did Intel learn permanently?
>
> **A** 2015 Optane memory launch and 2019 annual report 'big bets' still included memory — Andy Grove 'turning in grave' joke. 2019 $72B revenue, 25% net margin shows CPU/server strength, but mobile miss and foundry lag show inflection lessons decay without leader-after-leader paranoia.
