## Background

> Matt Reustle breaks down GE Aerospace with Ramesh Narayanan, co-founder and portfolio manager of 2Point2 Partners, focusing on the pure-play engine maker after Culp spun off GE HealthCare and GE Vernova. Narayanan opens with fleet scale — roughly 45,000 commercial and 25,000 military engines powering about three of four commercial takeoffs daily — and dominance across narrow-body Leap and legacy CFM56 programs, wide-body GE90 and GEnx lines, and regional jets.
>
> The conversation walks Larry Culp's deconglomeration from the Jack Welch and Jeff Immelt era through lean manufacturing on the shop floor, then dives into why jet engines combine extreme technical barriers with loss-leader OE sales and decades of high-margin spare-parts economics. Narayanan compares GE's CFM joint venture with Safran, Pratt & Whitney's GTF troubles, and Rolls-Royce history to explain consolidated oligopoly dynamics and predictable shop-visit cash flows.

## Key Facts

> **F1** GE Aerospace runs ≈70,000 engines (≈45,000 commercial, ≈25,000 military) and powers roughly three of four commercial takeoffs. Narrow-body share is ≈70% via CFM56 and Leap programs (sole source on Boeing 737 MAX and COMAC C919, ≈60% of Airbus A320neo family); wide-body share is ≈50% on 787 GEnx and 777 GE90 lines. Revenue is ≈$40 billion — ≈75% commercial engine services at ≈25% operating margin and ≈25% defense and propulsion at ≈11–12% margins — with legacy insurance runoff under ≈10% of revenue.

> **F2** About 70% of total revenue comes from aftermarket services: regulated shop visits every six to eight years, spare parts with ≈60%+ gross margins, and long-term power-by-the-hour contracts on ≈60% of Leap engines and higher on wide bodies. OE list prices near $20–22 million per Leap compress to ≈$6 million after 70–80% discounts to Airbus and Boeing; OE runs at a loss until maturity while aftermarket economics reach 4–5 times OE value over a 25-year engine life. Backlog stands near $175 billion — ≈4.5 years of revenue overall, ≈6 years commercial, ≈7 years on services alone.

> **F3** Narayanan expects commercial services revenue to grow ≈8–10% over five years as Leap fleet scale doubles CFM56, ≈40% of legacy CFM56 engines await first shop visits, and mid-single-digit aftermarket pricing persists post-pandemic. CapEx runs below 3% of revenue (≈10% of EBIT) with near-100% earnings-to-FCF conversion in the harvest phase; adjusted return on tangible capital is ≈20–25%, comparable to Safran. Management targets returning ≈70% of excess cash to shareholders (≈30% dividend, remainder buybacks) under Culp's Danaher-style discipline.

## Mental Model · *Loss-Leader OE, Regulated Aftermarket Annuity*

> **Components**
>
> Win airframe slots by certifying engines that push material science limits — turbine temperatures above alloy melting points — then sell initial equipment at deep discounts to consolidated OEM buyers (Airbus, Boeing, COMAC). Once airlines operate fleets for 20–30 years, mandated overhauls and proprietary spare parts shift negotiating power to GE, producing 40%+ aftermarket operating margins on a fragmented airline customer base. Joint ventures like 50/50 CFM International with Safran spread development risk across decades-long programs.
>
> **Application**
>
> Narayanan says the model trades ultra-high ROIC spikes for durable, visible cash flows protected by regulation and sole-source parts — Safran stayed FCF-positive in 2020 when traffic collapsed. Investors should track Leap reliability versus CFM56 benchmarks, Pratt GTF recovery, OE loss burn-off as production ramps, and GE's open-rotor bet versus competitors' geared-fan paths — market leadership can erode over decades (Pratt fell from ≈60% fleet share in 1995 to under 20%).

## Key Insights

> **1.** Backlog visibility underwrites a multi-year growth runway.
>
> **Q** How predictable is GE Aerospace earnings?
>
> **A** Narayanan cites ≈$175 billion backlog — seven years of services revenue alone — plus shop visits in 2025 matching 2019 levels despite ≈40% of CFM56 engines still awaiting first overhauls. Regulatory maintenance is non-discretionary even when traffic dips, and sole-source spare parts let GE push pricing through cycles including the pandemic.

> **2.** Culp's lean ops added margin on an already robust franchise.
>
> **Q** What changed under Larry Culp beyond spin-offs?
>
> **A** Commercial engine services margins hovered near ≈20% pre-Culp; lean manufacturing, customer focus, and portfolio cleanup lifted reported aerospace margins ≈500 basis points while stripping loss-making units. Culp chose to lead GE Aerospace post-spin — Narayanan reads that as a signal of relative quality versus HealthCare and Vernova.

> **3.** Technology bets define the next decade of share.
>
> **Q** What long-cycle risk matters most?
>
> **A** GE is all-in on open-rotor architecture targeting ≈20% fuel-burn gains versus competitors' lower-risk geared fans. Narayanan warns Pratt held ≈60% share in 1995 and fell below 20% — leadership persists only if next-generation architectures win airframe selections a decade out.

## Chronology

> *GE Aerospace*
>
> **1930s** Jet engines invented in the UK; GE builds the first U.S. jet engine based on Frank Whittle's design.
>
> **1970s** CFM International 50/50 joint venture with Safran launches, becoming the dominant narrow-body franchise.
>
> **2018** Larry Culp becomes first outside GE CEO, beginning deconglomeration and lean turnaround.
>
> **2016** Leap engine enters service, succeeding CFM56 on Boeing 737 MAX, Airbus A320neo, and COMAC C919.
>
> **2020s** Culp spins GE HealthCare and GE Vernova, leaving GE Aerospace as the remaining industrial core.
>
> **2025** Narayanan joins Business Breakdowns EP.235 as GE reports ≈$40B revenue, ≈$175B backlog, and commercial services targeting 8–10% growth.
